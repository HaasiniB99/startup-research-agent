# Stage 1 — Business Foundations

Geography: India-first, with global equivalents noted where useful.
Numbers below are illustrative only, to show how a mechanism works — not real company or regulatory data.

---

## 1. What is a "business"?

A **business** is any activity where you provide something (a product or a service) to someone else in exchange for money, on an ongoing basis. That's the whole idea at its core — everything below is a refinement of this one loop: offer something → someone wants it → money changes hands → repeat.

The moment this loop repeats on purpose, three separate questions start to matter, and the rest of this document exists to answer them in order:
- What kind of ambition and size does this business have? *(Section 2)*
- Legally, who is responsible if it goes wrong? *(Section 3)*
- How, exactly, does it make money? *(Section 4)*

These three questions are independent of each other — a business answers all three at once, but the answers don't determine one another. That independence is worth holding onto as you read, because it's easy to accidentally think "startup" implies a certain legal structure, or that "B2B" implies a certain size. It doesn't. Keep them as three separate dials.

---

## 2. Size and Intent: Startup vs. Small Business vs. SME/MSME vs. Enterprise/Corporation vs. Company

Start with the first dial: how big is this, and how big is it *trying* to become?

### Small business
A business built to **run steadily and profitably** at a known scale — a neighborhood restaurant, a tailoring shop, a small accounting firm. Growth is welcome but not the point; stability and owner income are. Most businesses in the world, including in India, are small businesses.

### Startup
This is where people usually get confused, so it's worth being precise: a **startup is not "a small, new company."** Age and size are side effects, not the definition. The actual definition (this framing comes from Y Combinator, so it's worth knowing what that is: a well-known American **startup accelerator** — a program that gives very early startups a small amount of funding, mentorship, and a structured few-month period to grow, in exchange for a small equity stake; it was founded in 2005 by Paul Graham along with Jessica Livingston, Robert Morris, and Trevor Blackwell, and Graham has since written extensively and influentially on what actually makes something a "startup") is that a startup is built to **search for a fast-growing, repeatable, scalable business model** — usually one that doesn't exist yet, or that improves sharply on what exists. It's temporary by design: it either finds that model and grows into a large company, gets acquired, or fails looking for it. A real chance of failure is baked into the definition, not a side effect of youth. *(Source: [Y Combinator — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Y_Combinator))*

**Small business vs. startup, in one line:** a small business optimizes for steady profit at a known scale; a startup optimizes for finding a model that can grow very large, very fast — accepting much higher odds of failure to get there. Same starting point (someone offering something for money), completely different bet being placed.

**India-specific layer:** in India, "Startup" is also an *official government status*, not just a description of intent — the Department for Promotion of Industry and Internal Trade (DPIIT) formally recognizes companies as "Startups" if they're reasonably young, stay under a government-set revenue ceiling, and are genuinely working on innovation (not an existing business relaunched under a new name). Recognition unlocks tax benefits and simpler compliance. The exact age and revenue limits are revised periodically, so treat them as "check at the time you'd actually apply," not something to memorize now. *(Source: [Startup India / DPIIT](https://www.startupindia.gov.in/content/sih/en/startupgov/startup_recognition_page.html))*

### SME / MSME
Notice that nothing above measured actual size — "startup" was entirely about intent. The government, separately, does care about measured size, for the purpose of loans and support schemes: this is the **MSME** classification (Micro, Small, Medium Enterprises). It looks at two things — how much a business has invested in equipment/machinery, and how much revenue (turnover) it earns per year — and sorts businesses into micro, small, or medium tiers based on those two numbers. Cross the top tier's limits, and a business is no longer classified as an MSME at all. You formally get this status through **Udyam Registration** — a free, fully online, self-declared government portal where a business registers itself as an MSME and receives a certificate. The exact current investment/turnover thresholds shift periodically, so — same rule as above — check them at the point of actually registering, not now. *(Sources: [PIB — MSME classification revision](https://www.pib.gov.in/PressReleasePage.aspx?PRID=2098389&reg=3&lang=2), [Udyam Registration — official portal](https://udyamregistration.gov.in/))*

The reason this sits right after "startup" is that the two labels answer completely different questions about the same business: "startup" asks *what are you trying to become*; "MSME" asks *how big are you measured to be right now*. A business can be both — an ambitious, DPIIT-recognized Startup can simultaneously be MSME-classified as "Small," with no contradiction.

### Enterprise / Corporation
Follow either path — a small business that grew very large, or a startup that "made it" — far enough, and you arrive at the same destination: an **enterprise** or **corporation**. These are scale words for large, established organizations with mature, predictable operations. ("Corporation" also names a specific legal structure common in the US — see the global-equivalents note in Section 3.)

### Company
Everything above — small business, startup, MSME, enterprise — describes *intent or size*. None of them describe the *legal shape* the business takes. **Company** is the umbrella word for that legal container; a startup, a small business, and an enterprise are all, underneath, structured as some kind of company (or another legal form). That legal shape is exactly what Section 3 covers next — because deciding "how big and ambitious" still leaves open the more urgent question of who's personally on the hook if things go wrong.

**Quick way to hold this section:**
| Term | Question it answers |
|---|---|
| Small business | Steady profit at known scale, by intent |
| Startup | Searching for a fast-scalable model, by intent |
| MSME/SME | How big, measured right now (investment + turnover) |
| Enterprise/Corporation | Large and established, by scale |
| Company | Legal container — could be any of the above |

A single real business can wear several of these labels at once: a 2-year-old food-delivery company could be a legally registered Private Limited Company (Section 3), officially DPIIT-recognized as a Startup (intent), and MSME-classified as a Small Enterprise (current size) — three different questions, three different answers, same business.

## 3. "Who is legally on the hook?" — Legal / Ownership Structures

Section 2 was entirely about ambition and size. This section answers the question that actually has teeth: if the business fails, gets sued, or can't repay a loan, **whose personal assets are at risk** — and how much paperwork are you willing to accept in exchange for reducing that risk?

Two words will do a lot of work in this section, so let's define them before using them. **Liability** simply means "being legally responsible for a debt or obligation" — if you're liable for something, the law can make you personally pay for it. **Compliance** means the ongoing paperwork and deadlines a law requires you to keep up with once you've chosen a structure — things like annual filings, disclosures, or audits. As a rule, the less liability you're personally exposed to, the more compliance you take on in exchange — that trade-off is exactly what the ladder below is built from:

**Sole Proprietorship** — one person owns and runs it. There's no legal separation between you and the business: **you are personally liable** for its debts, with no cap on that exposure (this is called **unlimited liability**). If it owes money it can't pay, creditors can pursue your personal assets, including your house. This is the lowest rung — easiest to start, because no compliance forces a separation that doesn't legally exist.

**Partnership** — two or more people run it together under an agreement. Same unlimited liability as a sole proprietorship, except now you're also exposed to your partner's decisions made on the business's behalf — a real risk to weigh before entering one. Registration is optional in India, though an unregistered partnership loses certain legal rights (e.g., suing a partner over the business).

**LLP (Limited Liability Partnership)** — the next rung up: it keeps the flexibility of a partnership but adds **limited liability** — the opposite of the unlimited exposure above. Your personal assets are generally protected, and your loss is typically capped at what you invested. Needs at least two partners. More compliance than a plain partnership, less than what's next.

**Private Limited Company ("Pvt Ltd")** — the top rung, and the structure most startups aiming to raise investment eventually adopt. It's a fully separate legal entity: the company itself owns assets and owes debts, distinct from the people running it, giving owners (**shareholders**) **limited liability**. It needs at least two directors (one an Indian resident) and two shareholders. It carries the heaviest compliance load of the four — the price of both the legal wall and of being attractive to outside investors, since ownership here is represented in clean, tradeable **shares**. *(Source: [IndiaFilings — entity comparison](https://www.indiafilings.com/learn/partnership-vs-llp-vs-private-limited-company))*

*(A single-person version of the last structure also exists — an OPC, One Person Company — a middle path between sole proprietorship and Pvt Ltd, worth knowing the name of even if used less often.)*

**Rough global equivalents**, for context: Sole Proprietorship ≈ "Sole Trader" (UK); Partnership and LLP are nearly identical concepts abroad; Private Limited Company is structurally close to the UK's own "Private Limited Company," and loosely like a small, closely-held corporation in the US (not identical, but the same underlying idea of a separate legal entity with shares).

**Why this matters going forward:** the structure you pick here determines whether personal risk is on you or walled off, how much compliance you take on, and — critically for Section 7 later — whether the business can even issue "shares" to bring in outside investors at all. Sole Proprietorships and Partnerships can't really do that; LLPs and Pvt Ltd companies are built for it, in increasing order.

That last point surfaces one more entrepreneurial fork worth naming explicitly here, since it's a decision every founder eventually faces: **bootstrapping vs. raising external funding.** Bootstrapping means funding the business yourself — savings, or money the business itself earns — without giving away any ownership. Raising external funding means taking money from outside investors in exchange for a piece of the company (which is only really practical once you're structured as an LLP or, more commonly, a Pvt Ltd). Bootstrapping keeps full control and ownership but limits how fast you can grow to how much cash you personally have or earn; external funding can fund much faster growth but means sharing both ownership and decision-making. Neither is "correct" — it depends on whether your ambition from Section 2 is a startup chasing fast growth (which usually needs external funding to move quickly) or a small business (which can often bootstrap comfortably). We'll come back to this properly, with real mechanics, in Stage 5.

## 4. "How, exactly, does it make money?" — Business Model

Sections 2 and 3 could be fully answered and you'd *still* not know how the business actually earns anything — they describe ambition and legal risk, not cash mechanics. That's the gap a **business model** fills: it's the answer to *"who pays, for what, how often, and why it's cheaper to deliver than what's charged?"*

It's easy to conflate "business model" with "idea," so it's worth separating them explicitly: the idea is *what* you're building (e.g., "an app connecting tutors and students"); the business model is *how money flows* through that idea (do students pay per session? does the platform take a cut? is there a flat monthly fee instead?). The same idea can carry several different possible business models — testing which one actually works is a core part of the startup search described in Section 2.

Before splitting into the specific mechanics, one concept sits underneath all of them and is worth naming explicitly, because you'll be asked for it constantly as an entrepreneur: your **value proposition** — the one- or two-sentence answer to "why would someone pay you, specifically, instead of doing nothing, or instead of using whatever they use today?" A business model describes *how* money flows to you; the value proposition is *why* it flows to you at all. Every choice below is really just a different container for delivering that same value proposition to a real customer — and "who that customer actually is" is precisely what Section 6 (the market) covers once we get there.

Two layers of choice stack up here:

**First, product vs. service.** A **product** is something standardized, built once and sold many times with little extra effort per sale (software, a physical gadget, packaged food). A **service** is labor performed for a customer, often customized (a consultant, a repair technician, a tuition teacher), taking ongoing effort per customer. Many real businesses blend both — a software product sold alongside paid customer support is a service layered on a product.

**Second, who you sell to and how the payment routes there:**
- **B2B (Business-to-Business):** you sell to other businesses, not individual consumers — e.g., accounting software sold to companies.
- **B2C (Business-to-Consumer):** you sell directly to individual people — e.g., a clothing brand selling to shoppers.
- **B2B2C (Business-to-Business-to-Consumer):** you sell through another business, which then serves the end consumer — e.g., payments technology embedded in a bank's app, where the bank's customer uses it without necessarily knowing your brand.
- **D2C (Direct-to-Consumer):** you sell straight to consumers, deliberately skipping retailers or distributors — usually online, e.g., a skincare brand selling only through its own site.
- **Marketplace/Platform:** you don't make the product or perform the service yourself — you connect two sides (buyers/sellers, riders/drivers) and take a commission per transaction.
- **Subscription:** customers pay a recurring fee for continued access, instead of paying once.
- **Freemium:** a basic version is free; a more capable version is paid — common in software.
- **Franchise:** you license your brand, systems, and playbook to independent operators, who pay fees/royalties to run their own outlet under your name.

These aren't industry categories — they're independent mechanics of payment, and the same idea could legitimately be built as a B2C subscription or a B2B2C marketplace. Once you know the mechanic, the next natural question is whether it's actually working financially — which is exactly Section 5.

## 5. "Is it actually working?" — Revenue, Cost, and Profit

Whichever model from Section 4 you picked, three numbers now tell you whether it's viable:

- **Revenue** — total money received from sales, before subtracting anything (also "turnover" or "sales").
- **Cost / Expense** — everything spent to operate: materials, salaries, rent, marketing.
- **Profit** — what's left: **Profit = Revenue − Cost.**

*Illustrative only, not real figures:* a small bakery selling ₹100 worth of goods in a month, having spent ₹80 on ingredients, rent, and wages, nets a profit of ₹20. Had costs been ₹110 instead, that's a **loss** of ₹10 — revenue alone never guarantees profit.

Here's the trap worth flagging directly, because it catches almost every beginner: **profit is not the same as cash in hand.** A business can be profitable on paper and still run out of actual money — for instance, if a B2B customer takes 60 days to pay an invoice, but ingredients already had to be bought in cash this month. This gap between "profit" and "spendable cash right now" is exactly why profitable-looking businesses can still collapse from running out of money. Precise vocabulary for this (burn rate, runway, cash flow) is Stage 5 — for now, just hold onto the fact that they're different things, because it changes how you'd judge whether a business is actually "doing well."

Profit also assumes something Section 4 didn't fully establish yet: that someone is actually there to buy. That assumption is Section 6.

## 6. "Who is actually going to buy this?" — The Market

A **market** is the real-world group of people (or businesses, if B2B) who might buy what you're offering, plus what they currently do *instead* — use a competitor, use a substitute, or go without entirely.

A market isn't a fixed fact sitting somewhere waiting to be looked up — it's something you define and then verify. "Working professionals in Bengaluru who want healthy snacks delivered" is a far more useful market definition than "everyone who eats food," because how narrowly or broadly you draw that circle changes everything downstream: how many realistic customers exist, and how you'd reach them. Precise sizing methods (TAM, SAM, SOM — terms you'll meet constantly once you start researching sectors) are Stage 5 vocabulary; for now, "market" is simply the honest answer to "who, specifically, and how many."

Once there's a real market and a working profit equation, the business starts pulling other people in — which is Section 7.

## 7. "Who else is involved, and what do they get?" — Key Roles

- **Founder / Co-founder** — the person or people who started the company, typically holding initial ownership and decision-making control.
- **Employee** — someone hired and paid a wage, who does not, by default, own any piece of the business.
- **Customer / User** — whoever receives the product or service. "Customer" usually implies they're paying directly; "user" is used when the payer and receiver differ (e.g., in the B2B2C model from Section 4, the business in the middle is the paying customer, while the end consumer is the "user").
- **Investor** — someone who puts money into the business in exchange for partial ownership, betting it grows in value. This ownership stake itself has a name: **equity** — the general term for "a portion of ownership in a company." This is the formal version of the "outside money in exchange for ownership" trade-off first raised in Section 3's bootstrapping note.
- **Shareholder / Equity holder** — anyone who actually holds equity, represented concretely as "shares" — the exact ownership unit that Section 3 explained only Pvt Ltd companies (and, with more restrictions, LLPs) are actually built to issue. A founder, an employee (via **stock options** — a right, often given as part of a job offer, to buy equity at a fixed price later), and an investor can all be shareholders in the same company at once.

This is where Sections 3 and 5 meet directly: shares are the *legal mechanism* for splitting ownership (from Section 3); profit and growing company value are *what's being split* among the people who hold those shares. Deeper mechanics of equity splits and funding rounds are Stage 5 — for now, just recognize these roles on sight.

## 8. "Where does this all lead?" — Life Stages of a Business

Pull every section together over time. A business tends to move through a rough sequence (general reasoning, not a strict law every business obeys):

1. **Idea stage** — a hypothesis about a problem and a possible solution, nothing built yet.
2. **MVP (Minimum Viable Product)** — the smallest version built to test whether real customers want it, before investing heavily.
3. **Early stage** — some real customers and revenue exist, but the business model from Section 4 is still being adjusted. This entire stage is really the search for what's commonly called **product-market fit** — the point where enough real customers clearly want what you built (rather than you having to convince them) that growth stops being a guessing game. Reaching product-market fit is, in effect, the moment the "search" that defines a startup (Section 2) succeeds.
4. **Growth stage** — product-market fit is behind you and the model clearly works (Section 5's profit math holds); focus shifts to scaling without breaking it.
5. **Mature stage** — growth levels off, operations stabilize; this is roughly the point people start using the word "enterprise" from Section 2.
6. **Exit** — founders and investors (Section 7) convert ownership into cash, either through another company buying yours (**acquisition**) or by selling shares to the public (**IPO — Initial Public Offering**).

This closes the loop back to Section 2: if you're on the **startup** path, this entire six-stage sequence *is* the search for a repeatable, scalable model, with real failure risk at every stage. If you're on the **small business** path, stopping comfortably at stage 3 or 4 forever is not a failure — it's simply a different goal from the outset, chosen back in Section 2.

## 9. "What do I actually have to *do*, on paper?" — Practical basics every entrepreneur runs into

Every section so far has been about concepts and decisions. But regardless of which legal structure you chose in Section 3, or which stage from Section 8 you're at, a few practical, recurring obligations show up in real life — and not knowing they exist (rather than not knowing the exact numbers) is one of the most common ways new entrepreneurs get into avoidable trouble, unrelated to whether their actual business idea was good.

- **PAN (Permanent Account Number)** — an identification number issued by India's income tax department. Both individuals and businesses need one; it's the basic identity a tax authority uses to track a business's income.
- **GST (Goods and Services Tax) registration** — a tax charged on the sale of most goods and services in India. Once a business's revenue crosses a government-set threshold (or in some cases, regardless of revenue, depending on the type of business), it must register for GST, charge it to customers, and remit it to the government. The exact threshold shifts and varies by business type and state, so — consistent with everything flagged so far — check it at the point you approach it, rather than now.
- **Sector-specific licenses** — depending on what you actually do, additional approvals may be required (for example, a food business in India typically needs an FSSAI license, from the Food Safety and Standards Authority of India, before it can legally sell food). These vary widely by sector, which is exactly why Stage 3 (Sector Deep-Dive) will flag the specific ones relevant to whatever sector you eventually choose.
- **Ongoing compliance** — the word introduced back in Section 3: recurring filings and deadlines (tax returns, annual filings for LLPs/Pvt Ltd companies, renewals of the licenses above) that don't go away once you've registered — they repeat, usually yearly.

None of the specific numbers, thresholds, or forms here are worth memorizing today. The important thing Stage 1 wants you to walk away with is simply that this layer exists, sits underneath every legal structure and every stage of growth, and is usually handled with the help of an accountant or company secretary (a **CA — Chartered Accountant** — is the professional most Indian entrepreneurs rely on for this) rather than figured out alone from scratch.
