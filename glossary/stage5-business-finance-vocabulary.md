# Stage 5 — Business & Finance Vocabulary

This is a working reference, not a story — so unlike Stage 1, it's organized as clearly-labeled terms you can look up. But related terms are still grouped together and connected to each other, because these numbers were never meant to be understood one at a time. Numbers used below are illustrative only, to show how a calculation works — not real company data.

---

## Cluster 1: Customer Economics — is each customer actually worth having?

Every business eventually has to answer one question about each customer it acquires: does this person bring in more money than it cost to get and keep them? These four terms are really just different angles on that single question.

### CAC — Customer Acquisition Cost
**What it means:** the total amount of money it takes, on average, to get one new paying customer. This includes marketing spend, sales team salaries, ad costs — everything spent trying to convince people to buy, divided by how many new customers actually resulted.

**How it's calculated, in plain terms:** add up everything spent on marketing and sales in a period, then divide by the number of new customers gained in that same period. *Illustrative example, not real data:* if a business spends ₹10,000 on ads in a month and gains 10 new customers from it, its CAC for that month is ₹1,000 per customer.

**Why it matters:** CAC by itself tells you nothing — ₹1,000 to acquire a customer could be a brilliant deal or a terrible one, depending entirely on how much that customer is actually worth to you. That's exactly what the next term measures.

### LTV (or CLV) — Lifetime Value (Customer Lifetime Value)
**What it means:** the total amount of money a business expects to earn from one customer over the entire time that customer keeps buying from it — not just their first purchase.

**How it's calculated, in plain terms:** roughly, how much a customer spends per purchase, multiplied by how often they buy, multiplied by how long they typically stay a customer before leaving. *Illustrative example:* a customer who spends ₹500 a month and stays for 20 months on average has an LTV of roughly ₹10,000.

**Why it matters:** LTV is only meaningful when compared directly against CAC. A business spending ₹1,000 to acquire a customer worth ₹10,000 over their lifetime (a 10:1 ratio) is in a very different position than one spending ₹1,000 to acquire a customer worth ₹1,200 (barely breaking even once you count the cost of actually serving them). A commonly cited rule of thumb — general reasoning, not a strict law — is that a healthy business wants its LTV to be at least three times its CAC.

### CAC Payback Period
**What it means:** how many months it takes for a single customer's payments to earn back the money it cost to acquire them in the first place, before any of that customer's future payments count as actual profit.

**In other words:** if CAC and LTV tell you *whether* a customer is worth it overall, payback period tells you *how long you have to wait* before you see any of that value — which matters enormously for a young company, because waiting a long time to get money back can be just as dangerous as never getting it back at all, especially if the company is low on cash (see Burn Rate and Runway in Cluster 2).

**Why it matters:** a business with a great LTV:CAC ratio but a very long payback period can still run out of money waiting for that value to materialize — this is a genuinely common trap for new founders to miss.

### Unit Economics
**What it means:** the profit or loss generated by a single unit of whatever the business sells — one customer, one order, one subscription — looked at on its own, separate from the company's overall size.

**In other words:** unit economics asks "if I ignore everything else and just look at one single sale, does the math even work?" A business can be losing money overall while still having good unit economics (common for fast-growing startups deliberately spending heavily to acquire customers) — or it can be profitable overall while having genuinely broken unit economics hidden underneath, which is a much more dangerous position, because growing faster would only lose more money per customer, not less.

**Why it matters:** CAC, LTV, and payback period are really just the specific pieces that make up unit economics — this term is the umbrella that ties the whole cluster together. Getting unit economics right, at the level of one single customer, is usually a precondition for a business to grow safely at all.

## Cluster 2: Company Financial Health — is the whole company okay right now?

Cluster 1 looked at one customer at a time. This cluster zooms out to the whole company: how much it earns, how much it keeps, and how long it can survive.

### Revenue
**What it means:** all the money a business brings in from selling its product or service, before subtracting any costs at all. Already introduced in Stage 1 — repeated here because everything else in this cluster builds directly on top of it.

### COGS — Cost of Goods Sold
**What it means:** the direct cost of actually producing or delivering whatever was sold — raw materials, the labor directly involved in making the product, packaging. It does *not* include costs like office rent, marketing, or the founder's salary — only the cost directly tied to the specific thing sold.

**In other words:** if a bakery sells a cake, COGS is the flour, sugar, and the baker's time to make that specific cake — not the shop's rent or the Instagram ads that brought the customer in.

### Gross Margin
**What it means:** what's left of revenue after subtracting COGS, usually expressed as a percentage of revenue.

**How it's calculated, in plain terms:** Gross Margin = (Revenue − COGS) ÷ Revenue. *Illustrative example:* if a business earns ₹100 in revenue and COGS is ₹40, gross margin is ₹60, or 60%.

**Why it matters:** gross margin tells you how much money is left over from each sale to cover everything else the business needs to pay for — rent, salaries, marketing. A low gross margin means there's very little room to cover those other costs, no matter how much revenue grows.

### OpEx — Operating Expenses
**What it means:** all the costs of running the business that aren't directly tied to producing what's sold — rent, marketing, salaries for staff not directly making the product, software subscriptions, and so on.

**In other words:** OpEx is everything left after you've already accounted for COGS — the cost of *running the business*, as opposed to the cost of *making the thing being sold*.

### Net Margin (Net Profit Margin)
**What it means:** what's actually left over after subtracting *everything* — COGS, OpEx, taxes, interest on loans — expressed as a percentage of revenue. This is the real bottom-line profit, not just the gross margin from earlier.

**Why it matters:** a business can have a great gross margin and still lose money overall if its operating expenses are too high — net margin is the number that actually answers "did this business make money, after all costs, in the end?"

### EBITDA — Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization
**What it means:** a measure of profit that deliberately ignores a few specific costs — loan interest, taxes, and the accounting cost of equipment wearing out over time (depreciation) or intangible assets losing value (amortization) — to show how the core, everyday business is performing.

**In other words:** EBITDA asks "if I strip out financing decisions, tax situations, and accounting-only costs, is the actual day-to-day business making money?" It's widely used because it lets investors compare companies that might have very different loans, tax situations, or equipment ages, on a more level footing.

**Why it matters, and a caution worth stating plainly:** EBITDA is useful, but it's not the same as cash in hand or even true profit — a company can have positive EBITDA and still be losing real money once interest, taxes, and depreciation are counted back in. Treat it as one useful lens, not the final word on financial health.

### Burn Rate
**What it means:** how much cash a business is spending, on net, each month — specifically, how much more it's spending than it's bringing in. Already introduced conceptually in Stage 1's profit-vs-cash discussion; here's the actual term.

**How it's calculated, in plain terms:** Burn Rate = cash spent in a month − cash earned in that same month. *Illustrative example:* if a startup spends ₹15 lakh in a month and earns ₹5 lakh in revenue, its burn rate is ₹10 lakh for that month.

**Why it matters:** most early-stage startups deliberately spend more than they earn, on purpose, to grow fast — burn rate isn't automatically bad. What matters is knowing exactly how fast you're burning, because it directly determines the next term.

### Runway
**What it means:** how many months a business can keep operating before it runs out of cash entirely, at its current burn rate.

**How it's calculated, in plain terms:** Runway = cash currently in the bank ÷ monthly burn rate. *Illustrative example:* a startup with ₹1 crore in the bank and a burn rate of ₹10 lakh/month has 10 months of runway.

**Why it matters:** runway is one of the single most important numbers for a startup founder to know at all times — it's the honest countdown clock on how long you have to either become profitable or raise more money before the business simply stops being able to pay for anything.

### Break-even Point
**What it means:** the point at which a business's revenue exactly equals its total costs — no profit, no loss. Below this point, the business is losing money; above it, the business is making money.

**In other words:** break-even is the finish line burn rate and runway are racing against — the goal, for a business trying to become self-sustaining, is usually to reach break-even before runway hits zero.

## Cluster 3: The Three Financial Statements — how a business actually reports itself

Cluster 2 introduced individual numbers like revenue and margin. In real life, businesses don't hand these numbers over one at a time — they package them into three standard documents, and every investor, bank, and tax authority expects to see all three. Each document answers a genuinely different question, which is why you need all three together, not just one.

### Income Statement (also called P&L — Profit & Loss statement)
**What it means:** a document listing revenue, costs, and profit *over a period of time* — a month, a quarter, a year. This is really just Cluster 2's terms (Revenue, COGS, Gross Margin, OpEx, Net Margin) laid out together in one place, in order, ending in a final profit or loss number.

**The question it answers:** "did this business make money during this specific period?"

### Balance Sheet
**What it means:** a snapshot of what a business owns and owes at one single moment in time — not over a period, like the Income Statement, but frozen at one exact date.

- **Assets** — things of value the business owns: cash in the bank, inventory waiting to be sold, equipment, buildings, and money owed *to* the business by its own customers (called **Accounts Receivable** — an invoice sent out that hasn't been paid yet).
- **Liabilities** — what the business owes to others: loans, unpaid bills to its own suppliers (called **Accounts Payable**), and taxes owed but not yet paid.
- **Equity** (in this specific context) — what's left over for the owners after subtracting liabilities from assets. This connects directly to Stage 1's definition of equity as ownership — here, it's literally calculated as Assets minus Liabilities.

**The question it answers:** "at this exact moment, what does the business actually own and owe, and what's genuinely left for the owners?"

### Cash Flow Statement
**What it means:** a record of actual cash moving in and out of the business over a period — not profit on paper, but real money actually changing hands. This is the formal document behind Stage 1's warning that profit and cash are not the same thing: a business can show a profit on its Income Statement while its Cash Flow Statement reveals it's actually short on real, spendable cash, because customers haven't paid their invoices yet (still sitting in Accounts Receivable on the Balance Sheet).

**In other words:** the Income Statement can include money you're owed but haven't received; the Cash Flow Statement only counts money that has actually landed in the bank.

**The question it answers:** "how much real cash came in and went out, and is there enough of it?"

### Working Capital
**What it means:** a business's short-term assets (cash, inventory, money owed to it that's expected soon) minus its short-term liabilities (bills and debts due soon) — a direct extension of the Balance Sheet's Assets and Liabilities, but narrowed specifically to the near-term.

**Why it matters:** a business can look wealthy overall on its Balance Sheet (lots of assets) while still being unable to pay this month's bills, if most of those assets are tied up in things that can't quickly be turned into cash (like unsold inventory or a building). Working capital is the number that specifically checks whether short-term obligations can actually be met.

### CapEx vs. OpEx — Capital Expenditure vs. Operating Expenses
**What it means:** two different categories of spending, treated differently in these financial statements. **CapEx (Capital Expenditure)** is money spent on things expected to last and provide value for years — a delivery van, a factory machine, a building. **OpEx (Operating Expenses)**, already covered in Cluster 2, is everyday running costs — rent, salaries, subscriptions.

**In other words:** buying a delivery van (CapEx) is treated very differently, on paper, from paying this month's fuel bill for that same van (OpEx) — the van shows up as an asset on the Balance Sheet and its cost gets spread out over several years, while the fuel bill is simply subtracted from this month's Income Statement in full.

### Depreciation and Amortization
**What it means:** the accounting method for spreading the cost of a long-term purchase (CapEx) across the years it's actually used, instead of counting the entire cost in the single year it was bought. **Depreciation** applies to physical things (a machine, a vehicle, a building); **Amortization** applies to non-physical things (a patent, acquired software, a trademark).

**In other words:** if a business buys a delivery van for ₹6 lakh expecting it to last 6 years, depreciation lets it count roughly ₹1 lakh of "cost" per year instead of ₹6 lakh all at once in year one — matching the cost to the years the van is actually being used.

**Why this connects back to Cluster 2:** this is exactly the "DA" (Depreciation and Amortization) in EBITDA — remember that EBITDA deliberately adds these accounting-only costs back in, to see how the core business is doing before this particular kind of paper adjustment.

## Cluster 4: Recurring Revenue & Growth Metrics — for businesses people pay repeatedly

Clusters 2 and 3 covered numbers that apply to almost any business. This cluster is specifically for businesses built on the **subscription** model from Stage 1 — apps, software, memberships — where the same customer pays again and again, and a different set of questions becomes important: is that repeat revenue growing, are customers actually sticking around, and are they actually using the thing they're paying for?

### MRR — Monthly Recurring Revenue
**What it means:** the total predictable revenue a subscription business expects to receive every month from its current subscribers, assuming none of them cancel or change their plan.

**Why it matters:** MRR is the single most-watched number for a subscription business, because unlike one-off sales, it tells you what income you can actually count on next month, not just what you happened to earn this month.

### ARR — Annual Recurring Revenue
**What it means:** simply MRR multiplied by 12 — the same predictable revenue, expressed as a yearly figure instead of a monthly one.

**In other words:** ARR and MRR are the same underlying number at two different zoom levels. Investors and larger companies often talk in ARR because it's a bigger, more comparable number when discussing year-over-year growth; day-to-day, founders usually track MRR because it moves and can be acted on faster.

### Churn
**What it means:** the rate at which customers stop paying — cancel a subscription, stop renewing, or simply leave. There are two related versions worth telling apart: **Customer Churn** (what percentage of customers left) and **Revenue Churn** (what percentage of *revenue* was lost, which can differ from customer churn if the customers who left were paying more or less than average).

**Why it matters:** MRR and ARR only tell you the current state of recurring revenue — churn tells you how much of that revenue is actively leaking out the bottom, which directly determines whether MRR grows, shrinks, or stays flat even while new customers are being added.

### Net Revenue Retention (NRR)
**What it means:** how the revenue from a business's *existing* customers changes over a period, ignoring any brand-new customers entirely — accounting for customers who cancel (revenue lost), customers who downgrade to a cheaper plan (revenue lost), and customers who upgrade to a more expensive plan or buy more (revenue gained), all combined into one number.

**In other words:** NRR asks "if I didn't sign up a single new customer this year, would my revenue from existing customers have grown, shrunk, or stayed the same?" An NRR above 100% means existing customers alone are growing the business's revenue — a genuinely strong signal, since it means growth doesn't depend entirely on constantly finding new customers.

### Cohort Analysis
**What it means:** grouping customers by when they first joined (e.g., "everyone who signed up in January") and tracking how each group behaves over time separately, rather than looking only at one combined average across all customers ever.

**Why it matters:** an overall average churn rate can hide the fact that customers who joined recently behave very differently from customers who joined a year ago — cohort analysis is how a business actually spots whether it's improving or getting worse at keeping customers, since blended averages can stay flat even while the real underlying trend is changing sharply.

### DAU/MAU — Daily Active Users / Monthly Active Users
**What it means:** how many distinct people actually *use* the product on a given day (DAU) or within a given month (MAU) — not how many people have ever signed up, and not how many are currently paying, but how many are genuinely, actively using it right now.

**In other words:** a business can have thousands of registered users and still be in real trouble if very few of them are actually opening the app — DAU/MAU measures real engagement, which usually shows up as a warning sign in churn (above) before it shows up as a drop in revenue.

**Why it matters, connecting the two:** the ratio of DAU to MAU (sometimes called "DAU/MAU ratio" or "stickiness") gives a rough sense of how habitual the product is — a higher ratio suggests people use it most days they're active at all, rather than just occasionally.

### Conversion Rate
**What it means:** the percentage of people who take a specific, desired next step, out of everyone who had the opportunity to take it — for example, the percentage of website visitors who actually make a purchase, or the percentage of free-trial users who become paying customers.

**Why it matters:** conversion rate is usually the first place a business looks when trying to understand why growth is slow — a large number of visitors or trial users means little if only a small fraction of them convert into paying, retained customers.

### ARPU — Average Revenue Per User
**What it means:** total revenue divided by the total number of users (or customers) over a period — a simple average of how much money each user brings in.

**Why it matters, and how it connects back to Cluster 1:** ARPU is a simpler, faster-to-calculate cousin of LTV (Lifetime Value) — where LTV tries to estimate a customer's *entire* future value, ARPU just looks at a single period's actual average, making it easier to track regularly even if it's a less complete picture on its own.

### NPS — Net Promoter Score
**What it means:** a customer satisfaction measure based on one simple survey question — "how likely are you to recommend this to a friend or colleague?" — scored 0 to 10. Respondents are grouped into Promoters (9-10), Passives (7-8), and Detractors (0-6), and NPS is calculated as the percentage of Promoters minus the percentage of Detractors.

**Why it matters:** NPS is widely used as an early-warning signal, because customer satisfaction (or dissatisfaction) tends to show up in an NPS survey before it shows up as actual churn in the numbers above — giving a business a chance to react before customers actually start leaving.

## Cluster 5: Market Sizing — how big could this realistically get?

Stage 1 defined a "market" as the real-world group of people who might buy what you're offering, and said precise sizing was a Stage 5 topic. Here it is. These three terms are usually drawn as three circles, one inside the other, each one a smaller and more realistic slice of the one before it.

### TAM — Total Addressable Market
**What it means:** the entire possible demand for a product or service if a business somehow captured every single potential customer in the world (or in whatever the broadest relevant scope is) — the biggest, most optimistic circle.

**Illustrative example, not real data:** for a company teaching coding to children online, TAM might be framed as "every child in the world who could potentially learn to code" — a huge, mostly theoretical number.

### SAM — Serviceable Addressable Market
**What it means:** the slice of TAM a specific business could actually realistically reach, given its specific product, business model, language, and geography — a smaller, more honest circle inside TAM.

**In other words:** SAM narrows TAM down by asking "of that huge theoretical group, which part could my *specific* business, as it's actually built, ever realistically sell to?"

**Continuing the example:** for the same coding-education company, if it only operates in India and only teaches in English, SAM narrows down to "English-speaking children in India with internet access" — still large, but no longer the entire world.

### SOM — Serviceable Obtainable Market
**What it means:** the slice of SAM a business could realistically capture in the near term, given real-world constraints — competition already in the space, the business's actual marketing budget, and how much it can realistically build and sell in, say, the next one to three years.

**In other words:** SOM is the honest, near-term answer to "given everyone else already competing for this, and given my actual resources right now, how much of this can I actually go get?" This is the smallest of the three circles, and usually the one investors care about most, because it's the closest thing to a realistic near-term revenue ceiling.

**Why all three matter together:** TAM shows the size of the dream, SAM shows the size of the realistic playing field, and SOM shows the size of the realistic near-term prize — using only TAM in a pitch or a plan (a common mistake) makes almost any idea look enormous and exciting, while quietly hiding the much smaller, much more honest number (SOM) that actually determines whether the business can survive on what it can realistically capture soon.

## Cluster 6: Ownership & Equity — who actually owns the company, and how that changes

Stage 1 introduced **equity** (a portion of ownership in a company) and **shares** (the concrete unit that ownership is divided into). This cluster covers how that ownership is actually tracked, and the ways it changes as a company brings in employees and investors.

### Cap Table (Capitalization Table)
**What it means:** a single document listing every person and entity that owns a piece of the company — founders, employees, investors — and exactly what percentage (or number of shares) each one holds.

**Why it matters:** the cap table is the ground truth of who owns what. Every time a company brings in a new investor or gives equity to a new employee, the cap table has to be updated — and the accuracy of this document becomes critical the moment real money or real ownership disputes are involved.

### Dilution
**What it means:** when a company issues brand-new shares (most commonly, to bring in a new investor's money), every existing shareholder's *percentage* of ownership goes down — even though the actual number of shares they personally hold doesn't change at all.

**In other words:** imagine a cake cut into 10 slices, and you own 1 slice — that's 10% of the cake. If the company decides to cut the same cake into 20 slices instead, to give 10 new slices to a new investor, you still have your original 1 slice, but now it's only 5% of the cake. You didn't lose a slice — the cake just got divided into more pieces. That's dilution.

**Why it matters:** dilution isn't automatically bad — if the new investor's money helps the company become much more valuable overall, a smaller percentage of a much bigger company can still be worth more in real terms than a bigger percentage of a small one. But every founder needs to understand that raising money (Cluster 7) almost always means accepting dilution, not avoiding it.

### ESOP — Employee Stock Option Pool (or Employee Stock Ownership Plan)
**What it means:** a set-aside slice of the company's equity, reserved specifically to be given out to employees over time, rather than to founders or investors. This appears as its own line on the cap table.

**In other words:** instead of paying an employee entirely in salary, a company can offer them the right to eventually own a small piece of the company itself — a way to attract people to a risky, early-stage company by letting them share in its potential success, not just its monthly paycheck.

### Vesting
**What it means:** the schedule over which someone (an employee with ESOP, or even a founder) actually earns the right to their equity over time, instead of receiving all of it immediately on day one.

**How it typically works, in plain terms:** a common pattern is a 4-year vesting schedule with a 1-year "cliff" — meaning if the person leaves before completing one full year, they get no equity at all, but after that first year, a portion unlocks immediately, and the rest unlocks gradually (often monthly) over the remaining years.

**Why it matters:** vesting protects the company from a situation where someone joins, receives a large equity stake immediately, and then leaves after a month — still walking away owning a meaningful piece of the company despite barely contributing to it. It aligns equity with actual time spent building the company.

## Cluster 7: Fundraising Instruments & Stages — how outside money actually comes in

Cluster 6 covered how ownership is tracked and how it changes. This cluster covers the actual process and paperwork of raising outside money — the documents used, and the typical sequence of rounds a startup goes through as it grows.

### Valuation
**What it means:** an estimate of what a company is considered to be worth, in total, usually expressed as a single number agreed upon (or negotiated) at the time of an investment.

### Pre-money and Post-money Valuation
**What it means:** **Pre-money valuation** is what the company is considered worth *before* a new round of investment money is added. **Post-money valuation** is simply pre-money valuation plus the new money being invested.

**Illustrative example, not real data:** if a company is valued at ₹10 crore pre-money, and an investor puts in ₹2 crore, the post-money valuation is ₹12 crore — and that investor now owns roughly 2/12 (about 16.7%) of the company, since their money represents that slice of the new, larger total.

### Term Sheet
**What it means:** a document laying out the proposed terms of an investment — the valuation, how much money is being invested, and what rights the investor will get — agreed upon before the final, fully binding legal contracts are drafted.

**In other words:** a term sheet is like a handshake agreement written down — most of it isn't yet legally binding, but it sets the framework both sides expect the final legal documents to follow.

### SAFE — Simple Agreement for Future Equity
**What it means:** an early-stage fundraising document that isn't equity yet — it's a promise that the money invested today will convert into actual equity later, usually at the company's next real priced funding round, once a proper valuation is easier to agree on.

**Why it matters:** a SAFE exists because agreeing on an exact valuation for a very young, unproven company is genuinely hard and slow — a SAFE lets a founder take money in quickly without that negotiation, deferring the "what is this company actually worth" question to a later round when there's more evidence to base it on.

### Convertible Note
**What it means:** an older, more traditional version of the same basic idea as a SAFE — money that starts out structured as a loan (debt), which converts into equity later, but unlike a SAFE, it usually carries an interest rate and a maturity date (a deadline by which it must either convert or be repaid).

**In other words:** a SAFE and a Convertible Note both solve the same problem (raising money before a company is ready to be formally valued) — a Convertible Note does it by temporarily calling the money a loan, while a SAFE skips the loan step and simplifies it into a more direct promise of future equity.

### Funding Stages
**What it means:** the typical sequence of rounds a startup raises as it grows, each stage generally tied to a different level of proof and purpose:
- **Pre-seed and Seed** — the earliest money, usually used to build an initial product and find early signs of product-market fit (Stage 1, Section 8).
- **Series A** — raised once there's real evidence the business model works, used to scale it further.
- **Series B, C, D, and beyond** — later rounds, generally for expanding into new markets, scaling operations, or preparing for an eventual exit (Stage 1, Section 8) — an acquisition or an IPO.

**Why it matters:** each stage typically comes with a different investor expectation — a pre-seed investor is betting mostly on the founder and the idea, while a Series B investor expects to see real, provable numbers (many of the metrics from Clusters 1-4) before committing.

### Bridge Round
**What it means:** a smaller, often faster round raised between two of the major stages above, meant to "bridge" the company financially until it can either raise its next full round or reach profitability on its own.

### Up Round vs. Down Round
**What it means:** an **up round** is a new funding round at a *higher* valuation than the company's previous round — generally seen as a positive signal that the company has grown in value. A **down round** is the opposite — a new round at a *lower* valuation than before, generally a difficult, negative signal, since it means new investors believe the company is currently worth less than earlier investors paid for it.

### Angel Investor vs. VC vs. PE
**What it means, as three distinct types of investor:**
- **Angel Investor** — a wealthy individual investing their own personal money, usually in smaller amounts, often at the earliest stages of a company.
- **VC (Venture Capital)** — a firm that pools money from many outside sources and invests it across many startups, generally betting on high growth and accepting that most individual bets will fail as long as a few succeed enormously.
- **PE (Private Equity)** — firms that typically invest in more mature, already-established companies, often buying a controlling stake, focused more on improving or restructuring an existing business than on betting on unproven future growth.

### LP vs. GP — Limited Partner vs. General Partner
**What it means:** inside a VC or PE fund, **LPs (Limited Partners)** are the actual source of the money being invested — pension funds, wealthy individuals, institutions — who provide capital to the fund but don't make day-to-day investment decisions. **GPs (General Partners)** are the fund's own managers, who decide which startups or companies to actually invest in and manage the fund on the LPs' behalf.

**In other words:** when a startup "raises money from a VC firm," the money ultimately comes from the fund's LPs, but the decision to invest, and the ongoing relationship with the startup, is handled by the fund's GPs.

## Cluster 8: Governance & Deal Terms — the rules attached to an investment

Once money is actually invested (Cluster 7), a set of rules and rights typically comes attached to it — this cluster covers the most common ones, plus a few related terms about how a company is overseen and how it eventually ends.

### Due Diligence
**What it means:** the investigation an investor (or acquirer) carries out before finalizing a deal — checking the company's financial records, legal standing, and the accuracy of its claims about its own business, to confirm things are genuinely as represented.

### Board of Directors
**What it means:** a small group of people with formal authority over a company's major decisions — typically including founders, and, once investors come in, representatives chosen by those investors as well. Recall from Stage 1 that founders and investors are both potential shareholders; the board is where some of those shareholders get direct, ongoing decision-making power, rather than just financial ownership.

### Liquidation Preference
**What it means:** a right investors typically negotiate for, determining who gets paid first, and how much, if the company is ever sold or shut down. A common example: a "1x liquidation preference" means that specific investor gets their original investment amount back first, before any remaining money is split among other shareholders.

**Why it matters:** this specifically protects investors in a bad outcome — if a company sells for a modest amount, liquidation preference can mean investors get most or all of the proceeds, leaving founders and employees with far less than their ownership percentage alone would suggest.

### Pro-rata Rights
**What it means:** the right for an existing investor to put in additional money in a future funding round, specifically to maintain their current ownership percentage rather than being diluted (Cluster 6) by that new round.

### Exit
**What it means:** already introduced in Stage 1 — the point where founders and investors convert their ownership into actual cash, either through an **acquisition** (another company buys the business) or an **IPO** (shares are sold to the public on a stock exchange). Repeated here because it's the event most of the terms in this cluster and the last one are ultimately building toward.

### Unicorn
**What it means:** a privately-held startup (not yet publicly listed) that has reached a valuation of at least $1 billion. Purely a valuation milestone — it says nothing on its own about whether the company is actually profitable.

### Burn Multiple
**What it means:** a more refined version of burn rate (Cluster 2) that connects spending directly to actual growth — specifically, how much net cash was burned to generate each unit of new recurring revenue (from Cluster 4's MRR/ARR).

**How it's calculated, in plain terms:** Burn Multiple = net cash burned in a period ÷ net new recurring revenue added in that same period. *Illustrative example:* a company that burns ₹2 crore to add ₹1 crore in new ARR has a burn multiple of 2.

**Why it matters:** two companies can have the exact same burn rate, but very different burn multiples — one might be burning cash and growing revenue efficiently, while the other burns the same amount for far less growth. A lower burn multiple generally signals healthier, more efficient spending.

## Cluster 9: Legal & IP Basics — protecting ideas and agreements on paper

A short but important cluster: the basic legal vocabulary that shows up around protecting a company's ideas and formalizing early agreements, well before things like term sheets and liquidation preferences become relevant.

### NDA — Non-Disclosure Agreement
**What it means:** a legal contract in which the people involved agree not to share confidential information that gets disclosed between them. Commonly signed before discussing sensitive business details with a potential investor, partner, or new employee.

### IP — Intellectual Property
**What it means:** a broad legal category covering ownership of things created by the mind — inventions, designs, brand names, creative works — that the law allows someone to own and control, similar to how someone can own a physical piece of property, but for something non-physical.

### Patent
**What it means:** legal protection specifically for an invention (a new process, machine, or method), giving its owner the exclusive right to make, use, or sell it for a set period of time, and the right to stop others from doing so without permission.

### Trademark
**What it means:** legal protection for a brand identifier — a name, logo, or slogan — preventing other businesses from using something confusingly similar within the same industry.

### Copyright
**What it means:** legal protection for original creative works — writing, music, software code, art — that generally applies automatically the moment the work is created, giving the creator control over who can copy or distribute it.

**Why these three matter together:** Patent, Trademark, and Copyright are the three most common forms of IP protection, but they protect genuinely different things — an invention, a brand identity, and a creative work, respectively — and a single business might reasonably need all three at once for different parts of what it's built.

### MOU — Memorandum of Understanding
**What it means:** a written document stating that two parties agree on a general plan or intention, signed before a full, formal, legally-binding contract is drafted. Unlike a term sheet (Cluster 7), an MOU can apply to almost any kind of business relationship, not just an investment.

**In other words:** an MOU is a "we're aligned on the general direction" document — it shows serious intent, but usually isn't the final, enforceable agreement itself.

## Cluster 10: Strategic & Operational Vocabulary — how founders talk about progress and advantage

This cluster covers commonly-used terms that describe how a business tracks its own progress and thinks about staying ahead of competitors — vocabulary you'll hear constantly in founder and investor conversations, separate from the purely financial terms in earlier clusters.

### KPI — Key Performance Indicator
**What it means:** a specific, measurable number a business tracks regularly to judge whether it's succeeding at something important. Many terms from earlier clusters — MRR, churn, CAC, conversion rate — commonly serve as a business's KPIs; a KPI isn't a new kind of number, it's a label for "the numbers we've decided actually matter enough to watch closely."

### OKR — Objectives and Key Results
**What it means:** a goal-setting method with two parts — an **Objective**, a qualitative, ambitious goal stated in plain words (e.g., "become the most trusted brand in our category"), and **Key Results**, a small set of specific, measurable numbers that would prove that objective was actually achieved.

**In other words:** the Objective answers "what are we trying to achieve, in words a human can feel motivated by," and the Key Results answer "exactly how will we know, in numbers, whether we actually got there."

### Pivot
**What it means:** a significant change in a startup's business model, product, or target customer, usually made after real evidence shows the original plan isn't working.

**Why it connects back to Stage 1:** this is a direct, practical consequence of Stage 1's definition of a startup as "a search for a repeatable, scalable business model" — a pivot is literally what that search looks like in practice when the first attempt doesn't pan out. It's a normal, often necessary part of the process, not automatically a sign of failure.

### Moat
**What it means:** a durable competitive advantage that protects a business from being easily copied or overtaken by competitors — named after the water-filled ditch built around a castle to keep attackers out.

**Why it matters:** a good idea alone is rarely a moat, because ideas can be copied — a real moat usually comes from something harder to replicate, like a strong brand, exclusive data, high switching costs for customers, or the network effect described next.

### Network Effect
**What it means:** a situation where a product or service becomes more valuable to each individual user as more people use it overall.

**Illustrative example:** a messaging app is far more useful if all your friends are already using it than if you're one of only a handful of users — the value comes from the size of the network itself, not just the app's features. This connects directly back to the marketplace/platform business model from Stage 1 — most marketplaces rely heavily on network effects to become genuinely hard to compete with once they reach enough scale.

### Economies of Scale
**What it means:** the pattern where the cost of producing or delivering each individual unit goes down as a business operates at a larger overall volume.

**Why it connects back to Manufacturing (Stage 2):** this is the exact mechanism behind Manufacturing's "efficiency at scale" competition described in the sector survey — a factory producing a million units can typically spread its fixed costs (rent, machinery) across far more units than one producing a thousand, lowering the cost of each individual unit.

### Scalability
**What it means:** how easily a business can grow its revenue without needing a matching, proportional increase in its costs.

**Why it connects back to IT/Software (Stage 2):** this is precisely why software businesses are often described as highly scalable — once built, selling a software product to one more customer costs very little extra, unlike a business (like a restaurant, or a factory) where serving more customers generally requires proportionally more staff, ingredients, or raw materials.

## Cluster 11: India-specific Tax & Regulatory Finance — the money rules unique to operating here

A closing cluster of terms specific to running a business in India, building on Stage 1's brief mention that a business will eventually need to deal with tax registrations and compliance.

### GST — Goods and Services Tax (recap)
**What it means:** already introduced in Stage 1 — a tax charged on the sale of most goods and services in India, collected by the seller and passed on to the government. Repeated here because the next term directly relates to how taxes get collected in general.

### TDS — Tax Deducted at Source
**What it means:** a system where the person or business *making* certain payments (like a salary, professional fees, or rent above a certain amount) deducts a portion of tax right at the moment of payment, and deposits that amount directly with the government on the recipient's behalf — rather than the recipient receiving the full amount and being responsible for paying all their tax later, separately.

**In other words:** TDS spreads tax collection out across the year, at the source of each payment, instead of leaving it all to be settled at once, later, by the person who received the money.

### ITR — Income Tax Return
**What it means:** the annual filing every taxpayer — an individual or a business — submits to the government, declaring their total income for the year and calculating how much tax is owed, or how much is due to be refunded (for instance, if TDS already deducted more than what was actually owed).

### Angel Tax — a recently changed, historically important term
**What it means:** a tax provision (technically under Section 56(2)(viib) of India's Income Tax Act) that, from 2012 until its removal, taxed a startup on investment money raised above its shares' officially assessed "fair market value" — effectively taxing investment capital itself as if it were income, which was a significant, well-documented pain point for Indian startups trying to raise money.

- **Current status, verified:** Angel Tax was abolished for all categories of investors, effective from Financial Year 2025-26 (starting April 1, 2025), through the Finance Act, 2024. *(Sources: [India Briefing — Abolishing the Angel Tax in India](https://www.india-briefing.com/news/abolishing-the-angel-tax-in-india-applicable-for-fy-2025-26-35289.html/), [The Tribune — Budget 2024-25: angel tax abolished](https://www.tribuneindia.com/news/india/budget-2024-25-leg-up-for-startups-angel-tax-abolished-642690/))*
- **Why it's still worth knowing even though it's gone:** it's a good example of how a single tax rule can meaningfully shape (or block) a whole category of startup fundraising in India — the same kind of regulatory risk worth watching for in any sector-specific deep-dive, not just a historical footnote.

### ESOP Taxation — a practical nuance worth knowing
**What it means:** an employee who receives stock options (ESOP, from Cluster 6) in India typically faces tax at two separate points: once when they **exercise** their options (converting the right to buy shares into actually owning them, usually at a price fixed earlier and taxed as a form of income), and again later when they actually **sell** those shares (taxed as a capital gain, based on how much the shares grew in value since exercising).

**Why it matters:** this is a genuinely practical thing to know before joining a startup for equity, or before designing an ESOP as a founder — an employee can end up owing real tax money at the exercise step even before they've actually sold anything and received real cash for those shares.
