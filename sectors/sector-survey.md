# Stage 2 — Sector Survey

Geography: broad/global patterns, with India-specific notes only where they're genuinely well-established and useful — deep India-vs-world comparison is Stage 3's job, once you've picked a sector. Numbers here are illustrative only, not real data (per Stage 1's rule).

**Purpose of this stage, stated plainly:** this is not the deep-dive yet — it's a wide, shallow map so you can point at 2-3 sectors and say "that one, tell me more." Every sector below is explained the same way, using vocabulary you already have from Stage 1: what it is, what it actually sells (product vs. service, from Section 4), how it broadly makes money (its dominant business model), and what sub-fields live inside it.

**How the survey is organized:** rather than list 16 unrelated sectors alphabetically, they're grouped by where each one sits in the *flow of value through an economy* — because that flow itself is worth understanding, not just the labels. Something is grown or extracted, then turned into a product, then moved and sold, then used by a person for daily life, all while a layer of money and information cuts across everything else. Five batches, following that exact order:

1. **Where value starts** — Agriculture/Agritech, Energy, Manufacturing
2. **Things people directly buy** — Consumer Goods, Food & Beverage, Fashion & Apparel, Beauty & Skincare
3. **Getting things and people to the right place** — Retail & E-commerce, Logistics & Transport, Real Estate & Construction
4. **Services for a person's life** — Healthcare/Medical, Education/Edtech, Hospitality & Travel
5. **The money and information layer that cuts across all of it** — Financial Services/Fintech, IT/Software, Media & Entertainment

We'll do one batch per message, and I'll pause after each for your reaction before moving on — per your request. Here's Batch 1.

## Batch 1 — Where value starts: raw inputs and physical production

Every other sector in this survey eventually depends on something being grown, drilled, or physically built first. These three sectors are that starting point — nothing here is sold to an everyday consumer in its raw form; it all becomes an ingredient for something else later.

### Agriculture / Agritech

**In practice, what actually happens here:** someone grows crops, raises livestock, or catches fish, at any scale from a single small farmer working a few acres to a large commercial farm running mechanized equipment. What comes out the other end is almost always a **product** (Stage 1, Section 4), not a service — grain, vegetables, milk, cotton, fish — usually in a raw or only lightly processed state.

**How the money actually moves:** most of it is sold through wholesale markets — in India these are often called **mandis**, government-regulated wholesale marketplaces where farmers sell to traders and processors in bulk. That's a **B2B** relationship (Stage 1, Section 4) far more often than a farmer selling one vegetable at a time to a household, even though small direct-to-consumer sales (a farmer's own stall) do exist too. A defining financial trait of this sector: margins per unit are thin, and the whole model depends on volume and on the harvest actually succeeding — a failed monsoon or a pest outbreak can wipe out a season's revenue entirely, which is a very different risk profile from most other sectors in this survey.

**The layers underneath the label:** the sector splits into crop farming, dairy/livestock, and fisheries as the actual physical production, plus a distinct newer layer called **agritech** — technology *sold into* this sector rather than farming itself. This includes apps giving crop advice, marketplaces that let farmers sell directly to buyers (bypassing the mandi and its traders), cold-storage and supply-chain technology (since fresh produce is perishable and spoils fast), and equipment rented out as a service instead of bought outright. Two structural facts worth holding onto for later: agriculture employs a very large share of India's workforce relative to its share of the country's total economic output (a widely acknowledged, general characteristic of the Indian economy — exact current proportions would need checking separately), and Indian farmland is famously fragmented into many small individual landholdings rather than a few large farms — a pattern that shapes almost every agritech business model built on top of it.

### Energy

**In practice, what actually happens here:** this sector produces and moves the power that every other sector in this survey depends on to function at all — electricity generated from coal, gas, hydro, nuclear, or renewable sources like solar and wind, plus fuels like petrol and diesel. A solar farm converts sunlight into electricity; a grid operator then physically carries that electricity to a factory or a home.

**How the money actually moves:** energy is sold as a **product** in the most literal sense — priced per unit of consumption — but increasingly bundled with **services** too (a company installing and maintaining solar panels for a client, for instance, not just selling them electricity). Because setting up power generation requires enormous upfront investment and because supply must be reliable for entire cities and industries, sales here run overwhelmingly through long-term contracts rather than one-off purchases, and the buyers are typically other businesses or the government itself (**B2B** and **B2G** — Business-to-Government, since government bodies and utilities are central buyers and regulators here) rather than individual consumers directly.

**The layers underneath the label:** it's worth explicitly separating two businesses people often lump together — **generation** (actually producing power: a coal plant, a solar farm) and **transmission/distribution** (the entirely separate business of physically moving that power to where it's used, and billing for it). A useful analogy: generation is like a factory making a product; distribution is like the delivery company that gets it to your door — often different companies entirely, even for the same unit of electricity. Within generation, the split between conventional sources (coal, gas, hydro) and renewables (solar, wind) matters a great deal right now: India has been visibly and publicly expanding its renewable energy capacity in recent years, a well-established directional fact even without pinning down the exact current numbers. One genuine, widely-recognized open challenge in this sector, useful to know for opportunity-spotting later: storing energy efficiently (batteries, at scale) remains a largely unsolved problem worldwide, which is exactly why solar and wind — which don't generate power on demand — are harder to rely on alone.

### Manufacturing

**In practice, what actually happens here:** raw or semi-processed materials get physically turned into finished products — a phone, a car, a packet of biscuits, a shirt. This ranges from heavy industry (steel plants, machinery, automobile assembly lines) to light, consumer-facing manufacturing (electronics assembly, textiles, packaged food production).

**How the money actually moves:** this is the clearest possible real-world version of the profit formula from Stage 1, Section 5 — source raw materials at one cost, add labor and machine time, sell the finished product for more than that combined cost. Manufacturing itself is rarely sold straight to an end consumer; far more often it sells to another business that assembles, brands, or retails the finished item, making it overwhelmingly a **B2B** sector. A specific, important pattern worth naming: **contract manufacturing** — a factory that makes products *for* other companies' brands rather than selling under its own name at all. A concrete example of the idea (not a real claim about any specific company): a single factory might simultaneously assemble phones for several different phone brands, none of which own that factory themselves.

**The layers underneath the label:** heavy manufacturing and light/consumer manufacturing behave quite differently — heavy manufacturing needs enormous upfront capital (a steel plant costs vastly more to set up than a small garment workshop), which is itself a real barrier to entry worth remembering later when you're evaluating how easy or hard it would be to enter a given market. India's government has also been publicly prioritizing domestic manufacturing growth in recent years, often framed around reducing reliance on imported goods — again, a well-known directional fact rather than a sourced statistic here. Because margins in manufacturing are usually won through efficiency and scale rather than premium pricing, this sector tends to reward operational discipline over branding — a genuinely different skill set from, say, the consumer-facing sectors coming up next.

Nothing in this batch sells much directly to an everyday consumer yet — that's exactly where Batch 2 picks up: sectors that take these raw and manufactured inputs and turn them into the specific products people buy for themselves.

## Batch 2 — Things people directly buy

Batch 1 pulled value out of the ground and turned it into materials or finished goods. This batch takes over from there: it's where a manufactured or grown input gets branded, packaged, and sold to an actual person for their own use. One honest note before starting — these four sectors overlap messily in the real world (a packaged snack could arguably belong to both "Food & Beverage" and "Consumer Goods"). That overlap is worth naming rather than hiding, because each sector below is really distinguished by *what dynamic dominates it*, not by a clean, non-overlapping definition.

### Consumer Goods

**In practice, what actually happens here:** this covers everyday items bought repeatedly for personal or household use — soap, detergent, toothpaste on one end, and larger, infrequently-bought items like refrigerators, air conditioners, and furniture on the other. The industry itself splits these into two very different buckets: **FMCG (Fast-Moving Consumer Goods)** — cheap, frequently repurchased items — and **consumer durables** — expensive items bought rarely. Food and beverages technically behave like FMCG too, but they're split into their own sector next, because perishability and food regulation make them behave differently enough to deserve separate treatment.

**How the money actually moves:** Manufacturing (Batch 1) makes the physical item; a consumer goods company's real job is branding it, marketing it, and getting it onto as many shelves as possible, then selling directly to individual buyers — almost entirely **B2C**. FMCG and durables make money in opposite ways: FMCG earns a small margin on each unit but sells enormous volumes because people repurchase constantly (you rebuy soap every month); durables earn a much larger margin per unit but sell far less often (a refrigerator gets bought once every several years). Because the underlying manufactured product is often not that different between competing brands, this sector competes heavily on brand trust and marketing rather than on the product itself — a genuinely different game from Manufacturing's efficiency-at-scale competition.

**The layers underneath the label:** beyond the FMCG/durables split, one structural feature is worth knowing specifically for India — the **kirana** store, a small, family-run neighborhood general store, has historically been the dominant retail channel here, meaning a huge part of competitive advantage in Indian FMCG has traditionally been about physically getting a product into hundreds of thousands of these small individual shops (a distribution challenge, not just a marketing one). More recently, **D2C** brands (Stage 1, Section 4) have started challenging this model by selling directly online instead, skipping the kirana network entirely — a real, currently-unfolding shift in this sector worth knowing about generally, even without attaching a specific number to how far it's gone.

### Food & Beverage

**In practice, what actually happens here:** producing, processing, and serving food and drink for people to actually consume. It's worth splitting this immediately into two quite different businesses hiding under one label: **packaged food and beverages** (a product business — snacks, packaged drinks, bottled water) and **food service** (a service business — restaurants, cafés, and delivery-only kitchens actually preparing and serving fresh food).

**How the money actually moves:** packaged food behaves almost exactly like the FMCG side of Consumer Goods above — brand-driven, sold at retail, thin margin per unit, high repeat volume. Food service is a completely different mechanic: revenue depends on footfall (how many people actually walk in or order) and margin is fought over three things at once — ingredient cost, staff labor, and rent (which pulls in the Real Estate sector from Batch 3, since location quality directly drives a restaurant's revenue). Beverages carry one more wrinkle worth flagging generally: alcoholic drinks are heavily regulated in India, with rules that vary significantly state by state — a well-known structural fact, without needing to cite specific state rules here.

**The layers underneath the label:** packaged foods/beverages sit on one side; on the food-service side, restaurants further split into categories like **QSR (Quick Service Restaurant** — fast, standardized, lower-cost dining, think of the fast-food model**)** versus full-service or fine dining. A genuinely important recent structural shift worth knowing: **cloud kitchens** — a kitchen built only to prepare food for delivery, with no physical dine-in space at all — emerged directly because of food delivery apps, and represent a real, currently relevant new sub-field. Those delivery apps themselves are worth flagging as a cross-cutting business: they're technically a **marketplace** (Stage 1, Section 4) connecting restaurants and diners, which means a full understanding of them actually belongs partly to the IT/Software sector in Batch 5, not fully to Food & Beverage — a good early example of how these sector boundaries are conveniences, not hard walls.

### Fashion & Apparel

**In practice, what actually happens here:** designing, producing, and selling clothing and accessories — ranging from large mass-market clothing brands, to high-end luxury fashion, to unbranded clothing made by local tailors. That last category matters more in India than the label "fashion industry" might suggest at first: a very large share of clothing bought in India is still unbranded, tailor-made, or bought loose from local shops rather than through a recognized brand — a genuinely important structural feature of this market, worth knowing generally even without a precise figure attached.

**How the money actually moves:** the basic mechanic resembles Consumer Goods — buy or manufacture at cost, sell at a markup justified by brand, design, or trend appeal. But fashion carries a risk the other sectors so far don't: strong dependence on trend cycles and seasons means unsold inventory (clothes that miss their season) is a real, recurring cost, unlike soap or a packet of biscuits, which don't "go out of style." This produces a genuine business-model fork worth naming: **fast fashion** — cheap, quickly-produced clothing chasing current trends, thin margin, high volume, fast inventory turnover — versus **luxury/slow fashion** — far fewer items, high margin per piece, where exclusivity and brand prestige *is* the value proposition (Stage 1, Section 4) almost entirely, more than the garment's practical function.

**The layers underneath the label:** mass-market/fast fashion, luxury fashion, and unbranded/local tailoring as the three broad tiers, plus footwear and accessories as closely adjacent categories that usually get grouped in with apparel. A newer sub-field worth knowing exists: online resale and thrift/secondhand fashion platforms, a small but real and growing challenge to the "buy new every season" default this sector has traditionally run on.

### Beauty & Skincare

**In practice, what actually happens here:** personal care products — skincare, haircare, cosmetics — on one side, and personal care services — salons, spas, grooming — on the other. This is the same product-vs-service split seen in Food & Beverage above, just applied to a different category of daily life.

**How the money actually moves:** the product side behaves like FMCG in Consumer Goods — brand-driven, repeat-purchase, sold largely B2C at retail or increasingly online. The service side (a salon) behaves much more like the restaurant model from Food & Beverage — revenue depends on footfall/appointments, and profit is about efficiently managing staff time and rent, not about a branded product at all. One structural feature genuinely worth knowing for this sector specifically: customer acquisition here leans unusually heavily on influencer and social-media marketing compared to most other sectors in this survey, a well-established general pattern that changes what "marketing cost" looks like for a beauty brand versus, say, a durables company relying on traditional advertising.

**The layers underneath the label:** skincare/cosmetics and haircare as the product side, salons/spas/grooming as the service side, and a genuinely active newer sub-field — **D2C beauty brands** (Stage 1, Section 4 again) that sell only through their own website or app, deliberately bypassing large retail distribution the way traditional beauty brands have relied on for decades.

---

### Where this leaves us

Notice what changed between Batch 1 and Batch 2: every sector here finally sells to an actual person, not just to another business — and every one of them revealed the same underlying fork, again and again, between a **product** business (brand-driven, repeat-purchase, thin margin/high volume) and a **service** business (location- and labor-driven, margin fought over footfall, staff time, and rent). That fork is worth carrying forward, because Batch 3 is exactly about the infrastructure that makes both kinds of businesses reachable at all — getting products to shelves and people to locations in the first place.

## Batch 3 — Getting things and people to the right place

Batch 2 answered "what do people buy." This batch answers the question that made every one of those purchases physically possible: how does the product reach a shelf or a webpage, how does it get to the buyer's door, and where does any of this — a shop, a factory, a warehouse — physically stand in the first place?

### Retail & E-commerce

**In practice, what actually happens here:** this is the literal last step of everything in Batch 2 — the shelf, the store, or the webpage where a consumer good, snack, garment, or skincare product actually gets bought. It splits cleanly into physical retail (supermarkets, malls, standalone shops) and e-commerce (online marketplaces and webstores).

**How the money actually moves:** retail usually doesn't make the product itself — it buys from a manufacturer or brand at a wholesale price and resells at a markup (the "retail margin"). E-commerce marketplaces often go a step further and don't even own the inventory at all — they simply connect many sellers to buyers and take a commission per sale, the **marketplace** model from Stage 1, Section 4. Physical retail's economics are dominated by rent and location (the same footfall logic as restaurants in Batch 2), plus real inventory risk (unsold stock is a cost); e-commerce trades that physical rent for delivery/logistics cost and heavy spend on digital customer acquisition instead.

**The layers underneath the label:** organized retail (chain supermarkets, malls) sits alongside unorganized/traditional retail — small standalone shops, including the kirana stores from Batch 2 — which, worth remembering, is still the dominant retail channel by sheer number of stores in India, a well-known general pattern. On the e-commerce side, multi-seller marketplaces differ from single-brand e-commerce (a D2C brand's own website). One recent, genuinely notable shift worth naming without attaching numbers to it: **quick commerce** — very fast delivery of small orders, often within minutes rather than days — has emerged as a distinct new model in Indian retail in recent years.

### Logistics & Transport

**In practice, what actually happens here:** physically moving goods (trucking, rail, shipping, air cargo) and, separately, moving people (ride-hailing, buses, trains, airlines). It also includes warehousing — storing goods in between movement — and **last-mile delivery**, a term worth defining because it matters a lot: the final short trip from a local hub to a customer's actual doorstep, which is consistently the most expensive and logistically complex part of delivering anything ordered online.

**How the money actually moves:** charged per shipment, trip, weight, or distance — overwhelmingly **B2B** when it's goods movement (a manufacturer paying to get products to a retailer), but often **B2C** or **B2B2C** for last-mile and ride-hailing (an e-commerce company hiring a delivery service that then reaches the actual individual consumer). Margins are thin and driven almost entirely by fuel cost and route efficiency — fewer empty or wasted trips means more profit — making this, like Manufacturing in Batch 1, fundamentally a scale-and-efficiency game rather than a brand game.

**The layers underneath the label:** freight/cargo (road, rail, sea, air) versus passenger transport versus warehousing versus last-mile delivery as its own increasingly specialized field, driven largely by e-commerce growth. Worth a quick callback to Batch 1's Agriculture section: **cold chain** logistics — refrigerated storage and transport — exists specifically because perishable goods (fresh produce, dairy) spoil without it, tying this sector directly back to a problem named two batches ago.

### Real Estate & Construction

**In practice, what actually happens here:** constructing physical structures (construction) and then selling, buying, or renting the space inside them (real estate) — covering homes (residential), offices and shops (commercial), and factories or warehouses (industrial — directly the physical space Manufacturing and Logistics from earlier batches actually need to operate in).

**How the money actually moves:** construction companies are typically paid per project, on contract — a **service** business paid once per job rather than repeatedly, which is a different shape from most sectors covered so far. Real estate itself earns money two distinct ways: a one-time sale (selling a property outright) or recurring rent (leasing space over time) — and that second shape, recurring rent, behaves closer in spirit to a subscription (Stage 1, Section 4) than to a one-time sale, even though the underlying asset is entirely physical.

**The layers underneath the label:** residential, commercial, and industrial real estate as the three broad categories; construction (building/creating) as a genuinely separate business from real estate brokerage (buying, selling, renting existing space) — the same company rarely does both; and property management (the ongoing upkeep and operation of a property after it's built) as a further distinct service layer on top. One structural fact worth holding onto, since it shapes this entire sector's behavior: land itself has a fixed, unexpandable supply — nobody can simply manufacture more of it — which is part of why real estate values often behave differently from every other sector in this survey.

### Where this leaves us

Batches 1 through 3 have now covered the entire physical backbone of an economy: pulling raw value out of the ground, turning it into things people buy, and getting both those things and the people who buy them to the right place. Batch 4 shifts registers completely — it's about sectors that sell almost nothing physical at all, and instead serve a person directly: their health, their education, their time away from work.

## Batch 4 — Services for a person's life

Batches 1-3 built and moved physical things. This batch is different in kind: these sectors sell almost nothing physical — they sell an outcome for a person (better health, a qualification, a good trip), and the "product," where one exists at all, is really just a delivery vehicle for that outcome.

### Healthcare / Medical

**In practice, what actually happens here:** diagnosing, treating, and caring for people, spanning hospitals and clinics (in-person care), pharmaceuticals (manufacturing medicines), diagnostics (labs and testing), and newer health-tech (apps and software sold into this sector). Health insurance also lives conceptually inside healthcare, though the actual mechanic behind insurance is really a Financial Services concept — we'll define it properly in Batch 5.

**How the money actually moves:** hospitals and clinics are a **service** business — paid per consultation or procedure, with margin fought over doctor and staff time, equipment cost, and physical space (pulling Real Estate from Batch 3 back in). Pharmaceuticals, by contrast, are a **product** business — manufacture a drug once, sell many units — but with two twists that don't apply to ordinary Manufacturing (Batch 1): heavy upfront **R&D (Research & Development)** cost, meaning the expensive part is discovering and testing a new drug *before* it can be sold at all, and heavy government regulation, since a drug typically needs official approval before it can legally reach a market — a barrier to entry unlike almost anything else covered so far. Diagnostic labs sit in between — a lighter service business, charging per test, driven mostly by volume and speed.

**The layers underneath the label:** hospitals/clinics, pharmaceuticals, diagnostics, health insurance, and healthtech (telemedicine apps for remote consultations, digital health records, wearable trackers) as the five broad layers. Two general, well-known facts about India worth holding onto: a very large private healthcare sector operates alongside government/public healthcare here, and India is also widely recognized as a destination for **medical value travel** (sometimes called medical tourism) — patients from other countries travelling to India specifically for treatment, typically at lower cost than in their home country. Both are broadly established patterns, not sourced statistics.

### Education / Edtech

**In practice, what actually happens here:** teaching and credentialing people, spanning formal schools and colleges, private tutoring and coaching, vocational/skill training (teaching a specific practical skill for employment, distinct from academic study), and edtech — technology-delivered learning like apps and online courses.

**How the money actually moves:** formal institutions charge fees per term or year, with a well-known general split in India between government-run/subsidized institutions and market-priced private ones. Private tutoring and coaching is a **service** business, paid per session or course — and it's genuinely oversized in India specifically because of the country's competitive exam culture (entrance exams for top colleges and professional courses), which has produced a large, well-known coaching industry running alongside formal schooling rather than replacing it. Edtech, meanwhile, monetizes through mechanics you already have from Stage 1: **subscription** access to a learning platform, one-time course purchase, or **freemium** (free basic content, paid certification or advanced material).

**The layers underneath the label:** K-12 schooling, higher education, private tutoring/coaching, vocational training, and edtech as a technology layer cutting across all of them — the same "sold into the sector" pattern seen with agritech and healthtech. One distinction worth being precise about, because it explains why formal education still holds value even with free content everywhere online: **credentialing** — a school or university's ability to issue a recognized degree or certificate — is a separate value from teaching itself. A private tutor can teach the same material as a university, but cannot grant the university's actual degree.

### Hospitality & Travel

**In practice, what actually happens here:** serving people away from their home base — hotels and other accommodation, travel transport (which overlaps directly with the passenger-transport side of Logistics & Transport from Batch 3), tour operators and travel agencies, and online travel booking platforms.

**How the money actually moves:** a hotel is genuinely a hybrid of Real Estate (owning and operating a physical property, Batch 3) and a service business (staffing and guest experience) — revenue is driven by **occupancy rate** (the share of available rooms actually booked on a given night) and the average price charged per room. A specific structural fact worth knowing: hotel rooms (like airline seats) are a **perishable** kind of inventory — an empty room tonight is revenue lost forever, not something that can be "saved" and sold later once the date passes, which puts real pressure on pricing and occupancy strategy. Tour operators, travel agencies, and online travel platforms mostly earn on commission — the same marketplace mechanic as Batch 3's e-commerce, taking a cut of a booking rather than owning the hotel or plane themselves.

**The layers underneath the label:** accommodation (hotels, plus newer short-term home-rental platforms that connect individual homeowners directly to travelers — another real marketplace example), transport (already covered structurally in Batch 3), tour operators/agencies, and online travel aggregator platforms. Worth knowing generally: India's travel sector includes both inbound international travel and a very large domestic travel market (Indians travelling within India) — two genuinely distinct customer segments with different needs, price sensitivities, and seasonal patterns.

### Where this leaves us

Every sector in this batch sold an outcome for a person rather than a physical thing — and each one still resolved into the same product-vs-service fork seen throughout this survey (pharma vs. hospitals, formal schools vs. tutoring, hotels vs. booking platforms). What's been mostly implicit so far — money, and the software/information running underneath everything — becomes the explicit subject of the final batch.

## Batch 5 — The money and information layer that cuts across everything

Every sector so far has quietly depended on two things running underneath it: money moving and being managed, and software/information making things work. This batch makes both explicit.

### Financial Services / Fintech

**In practice, what actually happens here:**
- Banking — taking deposits, lending money, and moving payments between people and businesses.
- Insurance — pooling risk: many people pay a small recurring amount (a **premium**), and the few who face a covered event draw a large payout. This is worth calling out as a genuinely different kind of business from everything else in this survey — you're not selling a product or a normal service, you're selling protection against an uncertain future event.
- Investment/wealth management — helping people or businesses grow money over time, through mutual funds, stock brokerages, or advisors.
- Fintech — technology sold into or replacing parts of this sector: payment apps, digital lending platforms, and robo-advisors (automated investment advice delivered through software instead of a human advisor). Same "tech layer sold into an existing sector" pattern already seen with agritech, healthtech, and edtech.

**How the money actually moves:**
- Banks earn mainly on the **spread** — the gap between the low interest they pay depositors and the higher interest they charge borrowers.
- Insurance earns on the gap between premiums collected and payouts made, assuming the risk was priced correctly in the first place (a specialized skill called actuarial pricing — worth naming, though its mechanics go beyond this survey).
- Investment managers typically earn a fee as a percentage of the total money they manage, not a slice of the profit they generate — a distinct model worth noting.
- Fintech companies usually earn through transaction fees (a small cut per payment processed) or subscription — both mechanics you already have from Stage 1.

**The layers underneath the label:**
- Banking, insurance, and investments/wealth management as the three traditional pillars.
- Fintech as the cross-cutting tech layer sitting on top of all three.
- Worth knowing generally: India has seen a very large, well-recognized shift toward digital payments in recent years — a broadly established directional fact, making this one of the most tech-disrupted sectors in this entire survey.

### IT / Software

**In practice, what actually happens here:**
- Building software products — a company builds something like an app or a platform once and sells it to many customers.
- IT services — a company builds custom software or does technical work for one specific client at a time, essentially the "contract manufacturing" idea from Batch 1, applied to software instead of physical goods.
- India specifically has been globally known for the IT-services side of this sector for decades — large companies doing custom software and technical work for clients abroad — a well-established general fact about the Indian economy.

**How the money actually moves:**
- Software products monetize through **SaaS** (Software as a Service — software paid for on a recurring basis, usually accessed online rather than owned outright), a one-time license, or freemium — mechanics already familiar from Stage 1.
- IT services get paid per project, or per unit of people's time (a structure sometimes called "time and materials") — much closer in spirit to Construction from Batch 3 than to a product business.
- A defining trait worth naming precisely: once software is built, selling it to one more customer costs very little extra — unlike a physical product, which costs real material and labor for every additional unit. This is exactly why software businesses can often scale revenue very fast without their costs scaling at the same rate.

**The layers underneath the label:**
- Software products (apps, platforms, tools) and IT services/consulting as the two core halves.
- Every sector-specific "tech" mentioned earlier in this survey — agritech, healthtech, edtech, fintech — is technically a software business aimed at one particular sector. In that sense, IT/Software isn't really a separate, contained sector at all — it's the layer running underneath most of the others already covered.

### Media & Entertainment

**In practice, what actually happens here:**
- Producing and distributing content people watch, read, listen to, or play — film, TV, music, news/journalism, and gaming.
- Two distinct roles worth separating: content creators/producers (who actually make the film, song, game, or article) versus distribution platforms (who deliver that content to an audience — a streaming service, a social media app, a gaming platform).

**How the money actually moves:**
- Direct payment — a ticket, a subscription (streaming), or a one-time purchase (a game).
- Advertising — content is given free or cheap to the audience, and the real paying customer is an advertiser paying to reach that audience's attention. This is worth naming as its own distinct pattern: the actual payer (the advertiser) is different from the person actually using the product (the viewer) — related to, but a bit different from, the B2B2C idea in Stage 1.
- Gaming often runs on freemium — free to play, with payment for in-game items — a mechanic already familiar.

**The layers underneath the label:**
- Film & TV, music, news/journalism, and gaming as the core content categories.
- Distribution platforms (streaming services, social media apps) as a structurally separate layer — and worth calling out explicitly: these platforms are, underneath, another kind of marketplace (Stage 1, Section 4), connecting creators to audiences and often taking a cut or controlling the advertising revenue. That also means they're fundamentally IT/Software businesses wearing a Media & Entertainment label.

## Sectors mentioned but not covered in depth ("and others")

A handful of sectors exist that didn't get a full breakdown in this survey, either because they overlap heavily with what's already covered or because they're less commonly the starting point for a first-time founder. Worth knowing they exist:

- **Telecom** — the networks (mobile, internet connectivity) that most of Batch 5's fintech, edtech, and media businesses actually run on top of.
- **Automotive** — sits partly in Manufacturing (Batch 1) and partly in Logistics & Transport (Batch 3), depending on whether you're looking at making vehicles or moving people/goods with them.
- **Professional & Business Services** — consulting, legal, and accounting services sold to other businesses — a pure B2B service sector not tied to any single industry.
- **Public Sector / Government** — not a "startup sector" in the usual sense, but worth remembering it shows up as a competitor, regulator, or customer across almost every sector above (Stage 3 will flag this specifically wherever it matters for your chosen sector).
