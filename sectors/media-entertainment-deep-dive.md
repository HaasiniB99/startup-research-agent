# Stage 3 + 4 — Sector Deep-Dive: Media & Entertainment (India)

Geography: **India specifically**, throughout. Wherever this document compares India to another country (mainly the US and China), that comparison is called out explicitly. We never assume something that works one way abroad automatically works the same way in India.

This document does not repeat Stage 1's basic vocabulary (B2B, B2C, B2B2C, product vs. service, marketplace, subscription, freemium). If you need those definitions, see `foundations/stage1-business-foundations.md`.

This document follows the same numbers policy as the earlier deep-dives in this project (`sectors/fintech-deep-dive.md`, `sectors/food-beverage-deep-dive.md`). In plain terms: real company names and directional facts are the substance of this research, and they're included freely. Exact figures — like a company's revenue or a market-size estimate — are only included when that specific number is itself the point being made, and it is always labeled as either a verified source or an unsourced industry estimate.

**What Stage 2 already covered, so it is not repeated here:** Stage 2 (in `sectors/sector-survey.md`) already gave you the basic shape of this sector — content creators/producers versus distribution platforms, direct payment (tickets, subscriptions, one-time purchase) versus advertising (where the advertiser is the real paying customer, not the viewer), freemium in gaming, and the fact that distribution platforms are really marketplaces and therefore really IT/Software businesses wearing a Media & Entertainment label. This document assumes you already have that background. It goes underneath it, into real sub-fields, real companies, real economics, and the live regulatory shocks reshaping this sector as of 2026.

**What's in scope here:** this deep-dive treats "Media & Entertainment" the way the Indian industry actually splits it up in 2025-26. That means: video streaming (OTT) and its recent, dramatic consolidation; the film industry (Bollywood and the regional-language industries) and cinema exhibition; television's structural decline; music streaming; the creator economy and influencer marketing; gaming, which was just hit by the single biggest regulatory shock in this sector's recent history; news and journalism's revenue-model crisis; animation/VFX as a B2B global-services export business hiding inside a consumer-facing sector; podcasts and audio; and the brand-new micro-drama/vertical-video format that barely existed a year ago and is already one of India's most-downloaded app categories. Government shows up repeatedly across these sub-fields — sometimes as regulator, sometimes as the entity that just deleted an entire business model overnight — and each instance is verified rather than assumed.

## Sub-field 1: Video streaming (OTT) — and the consolidation that just rewrote the whole category

### What this actually is, and the single biggest structural fact to know first

OTT stands for "Over-The-Top" — it just means video delivered over the open internet, instead of through a cable box or a satellite dish. Think of it like the difference between a landline phone (cable TV, a fixed physical connection into your house) and a mobile phone (OTT, works over the internet wherever you are).

The single most important fact to understand about Indian streaming in 2026 is that it just went through a massive consolidation. In February 2025, **JioCinema** (owned by Reliance's Viacom18) and **Disney+ Hotstar** merged into one single platform called **JioHotstar**. This followed a joint venture between Reliance, Viacom18, and Disney that combined their Indian television and streaming businesses into one company called JioStar. In other words, two of India's three biggest streaming platforms stopped being separate competitors and became one company almost overnight. *(Sources: [India Today NE — JioCinema, Disney+ Hotstar merge](https://www.indiatodayne.in/entertainment/story/jiocinema-disney-hotstar-merge-to-launch-jiohotstar-in-industry-defining-move-1170392-2025-02-14), [Advanced Television — India: JioCinema, Disney+ Hotstar merge](https://www.advanced-television.com/2025/02/14/india-jiocinema-disney-hotstar-merge/))*

- The scale this created is genuinely enormous. JioHotstar grew from around 50 million subscribers in February 2025 to roughly 300 million by June 2025, and is reported to have over 500 million registered users and around 503 million monthly users overall. For comparison, that is a larger user base than most countries' entire populations. *(Source: [Apprupt — India OTT Market Forecasts & Statistics 2026](https://www.apprupt.com/india-ott-market-forecasts-statistics), [IndicDaily — Streaming Wars in India 2026](https://www.indicdaily.com/streaming-wars-in-india-2026-netflix-vs-jiohotstar-vs-amazon-prime-video/))*
- **Netflix** is India's premium leader by revenue-per-user, not by subscriber count. It reportedly earns around ₹4,000 crore (roughly $480 million) in India revenue, but holds only an estimated 16-20 million subscribers — a small fraction of JioHotstar's base. This is a genuinely important distinction: Netflix in India competes on a smaller, higher-paying audience, not on raw scale. *(Source: [ResourceEra — Netflix Statistics 2026](https://resourcera.com/data/ott/netflix-statistics/))*
- **Amazon Prime Video** has around 66 million Indian users, making India its second-largest market globally after the US. Prime Video's position is different again: video is usually a secondary benefit bundled into an Amazon Prime membership that primarily sells fast shipping on Amazon's e-commerce marketplace, not a standalone video subscription most people buy for the video alone.

### Why this merger happened — the business logic underneath it

- The single biggest asset in Indian sports and entertainment is **cricket, specifically the IPL (Indian Premier League)**. JioStar now holds both the pay-TV broadcast rights (through Star Sports) and the exclusive digital streaming rights (through JioHotstar) for IPL matches through the current rights cycle running to 2027. In other words, one single company now controls almost every way an Indian fan can legally watch IPL cricket, whether on TV or on a phone. *(Sources: [Wikipedia — Indian Premier League on television](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_Premier_League_on_television), [Croma Unboxed — How to watch IPL 2026 live on TV](https://www.croma.com/unboxed/how-to-watch-ipl-2026-live-on-tv))*
- Cricket in India is not just "one popular sport among many" — it functions as the single most reliable, mass, must-watch live event this entire sector has. A streaming platform that owns cricket rights gets guaranteed enormous viewership spikes every time a match airs, which it can then use to cross-sell everything else in its catalogue — general entertainment shows, films, and international content. This is the core strategic reason Reliance and Disney merged their businesses rather than continuing to compete separately for the same cricket rights.
- **A genuinely important structural shift for 2026: IPL streaming is no longer free.** In earlier years, JioCinema famously streamed IPL matches for free to build up its subscriber base fast. As of the 2026 season, users need an active subscription to stream IPL on JioHotstar, although many Jio telecom plans come bundled with a free JioHotstar subscription. This is a direct example of a company using a free product to build overwhelming market share first, then converting to paid once its position was dominant enough that a rival was unlikely to displace it. *(Source: [Business Standard — Here's how Jio users can live stream IPL 2026](https://www.business-standard.com/cricket/ipl/here-s-how-jio-users-can-live-stream-ipl-2026-for-free-on-jiohotstar-126032800567_1.html))*

### Who else is in this sub-field, and what's genuinely different about the Indian market shape now

- India also has home-grown, regional-language-focused streaming players, such as **SunNXT** (South Indian languages) and **Zee5** (Zee Entertainment's platform, spanning Hindi and regional content), plus **SonyLIV**. These are smaller than JioHotstar but matter because India's streaming audience is genuinely multi-lingual in a way most Western streaming markets are not — a single national platform in one language cannot serve the whole country the way, for example, a single English-language platform can serve most of the US.
- **The India-vs-global comparison worth being precise about:** in the US, streaming is genuinely competitive among several large, roughly comparable players — Netflix, Disney+, Max, Peacock, Paramount+ — each backed by a different media conglomerate, with no single dominant player controlling a market-defining live-sports asset the way JioStar now controls cricket in India. India's market has moved toward something closer to one dominant platform plus several smaller challengers, rather than several comparably-sized competitors. **This should be treated as a structural observation from the sources reviewed, not an exhaustive antitrust analysis** — whether India's competition regulator (the CCI, Competition Commission of India) will examine this concentration the way it has examined Zomato/Swiggy in food delivery (see `sectors/food-beverage-deep-dive.md`) was not confirmed in this research pass.

## Sub-field 2: Film — Bollywood, regional cinema, and cinema exhibition economics

### What this actually is, and why "Bollywood" is only one part of the picture

"Bollywood" specifically means Hindi-language cinema made in Mumbai. But India has several other large, distinct film industries organized by language, each with its own stars, studios, and audience — Telugu cinema (sometimes called Tollywood), Tamil cinema (Kollywood), Malayalam cinema, Kannada cinema, and others. These are not small niche markets inside a bigger "Indian film industry" — each is a genuinely separate industry with its own economics, and several have had major national and even international box-office hits in recent years. A founder should never treat "Indian film" as a synonym for "Hindi film" the way it's sometimes loosely used outside India.

- A specific, useful fact for a founder to internalize: mid-scale Hindi films (budgeted and grossing in a moderate range, as opposed to the biggest blockbusters) have reportedly grown their share of total Bollywood box office in 2025, alongside continued strength from big-budget releases. **This should be treated as a directional trend reported by industry trackers, not an audited, official statistic** — box office aggregation sites in India are generally self-reported and not independently audited the same way a public company's revenue is. *(Source: general search summary, box-office trend reporting)*

### How cinema exhibition (the actual movie theatre business) makes money, and why it's genuinely fragile

- **PVR INOX** is India's largest multiplex chain, formed by the 2023 merger of PVR and INOX. It operates roughly 1,700+ screens across around 350 properties, and captures a substantial share — reported around 31% — of India's total box office. *(Source: [MarkHub24 — PVR INOX's Multiplex Revenue Model](https://www.markhub24.com/post/pvr-inox-s-multiplex-revenue-model-engineering-survival-in-the-age-of-streaming))*
- Here is the genuinely important structural problem with this business, stated plainly: **a multiplex's costs — rent, staff, electricity — stay roughly the same every week, but its main source of revenue, ticket sales, swings wildly depending on whether a hit film happens to be releasing that week.** In other words, the cinema's expenses don't flex with how good or bad the week's movies are, but its income does. This is a fundamentally different risk shape from, say, a subscription streaming business, where revenue is much more predictable month to month.
- Because of this, multiplexes have leaned hard into **food and beverage (F&B) sales** — popcorn, snacks, drinks — as a second, somewhat more controllable revenue stream. In one recent year, even as the number of tickets sold fell, growth in how much each customer spent on snacks partly offset the lost ticket revenue. This is a direct, verified example of a company deliberately building a second revenue engine specifically because its main one is too unpredictable to rely on alone.
- **Smart Cinemas** is PVR INOX's own answer to a separate problem: most of India's screen growth potential now lies in smaller Tier-2 and Tier-3 towns (India's smaller cities, ranked below the biggest metros), where a full-size, expensive multiplex doesn't make financial sense. These smaller-format cinemas reportedly cost 30-40% less to build per screen than a standard multiplex in the same location. This mirrors a pattern already seen in the food-beverage deep-dive with QSR chains like Domino's and KFC's operator Devyani expanding into Tier-2/3 cities because real estate is cheaper there — the same underlying economic logic, applied to cinema instead of restaurants.
- **The theatrical exclusivity window has been shrinking.** This "window" is the period a film is only available in cinemas before it becomes available to stream at home. As streaming platforms compete harder for new content, this window has compressed. A shorter window means fewer weeks where a cinema has exclusive access to a new film before people can just wait and watch it at home instead — a direct, structural pressure on cinema revenue that didn't exist in the same way a decade ago.

### Piracy — a large, verified, and directly load-bearing problem for this entire sub-field

- A joint EY/IAMAI (Internet and Mobile Association of India) study, sometimes called "The Rob Report," sized India's piracy economy at roughly ₹224 billion (about $2.7 billion) in 2023. Of that, roughly ₹137 billion was tied to pirated movie-theatre content and roughly ₹87 billion to pirated OTT content. This figure is genuinely load-bearing — it's the reason both the film and streaming halves of this sector treat piracy as an existential cost problem, not a minor nuisance. *(Sources: [EY — Piracy causes INR 224B loss annually: IAMAI](https://www.ey.com/en_in/newsroom/2024/10/inr-224-billion-lost-annually-to-piracy-majority-call-for-stricter-enforcement-ey-iamai-report), [Ministry of Information and Broadcasting — The Impact of Piracy on India's Video Sector](https://mib.gov.in/sites/default/files/2025-06/the-impact-of-piracy-on-india-s-video-sector-creative-economy_ip-house-x-mpa-x-cii_compressed_0.pdf))*
- The same study found that 51% of Indian media consumers access pirated content, and 76% of those pirating are aged 19-34 — meaning piracy is concentrated specifically among the younger, digitally fluent audience that streaming platforms most want to convert into paying subscribers. In other words, the audience piracy hurts the most is the exact same audience the legal platforms are trying hardest to win.
- **Why this matters for a founder:** any content or distribution business idea in this sector needs to assume piracy as a permanent, structural cost of doing business in India, not a solvable one-time problem. This shapes pricing decisions (platforms often price lower in India than in Western markets partly because of price-sensitive piracy-prone audiences) and content-protection spending.

## Sub-field 3: Television and cable — the decline that Stage 2 didn't cover at all

### What this actually is, and how fast it's shrinking

Traditional television reaches homes two ways in India: **DTH (Direct-to-Home)**, a small satellite dish on the roof, and **cable**, a physical wire run by a local cable operator. Both are now in a well-documented, accelerating decline, being replaced by internet-based viewing (OTT, described in Sub-field 1) and IPTV (television delivered over a broadband/fibre internet connection, rather than satellite or a dedicated cable wire).

- DTH lost more than 6 million paying subscribers in the twelve months to June 2025, falling from about 62.2 million to about 56.1 million. By December 2025, the paying DTH base had fallen further, down to roughly 51 million from about 58 million a year earlier — meaning the pace of decline has accelerated, not slowed. This specific trajectory matters because it shows the decline isn't a one-off blip; it's a consistent multi-year slide. *(Sources: [Exchange4media — 6 million subscribers lost in a year](https://www.exchange4media.com/media-tv-news/6-million-subscribers-lost-in-a-year-is-it-time-for-dth-to-press-the-reinvention-button-147291.html), [BestMediaInfo — The 'State of Pay DTH subscription and players in India'](https://bestmediainfo.com/mediainfo/television/the-state-of-pay-dth-subscription-and-players-in-india-in-numbers-9388047))*
- Cable television's decline has been even steeper: the paying cable subscriber base reportedly shrank to about 111 million by the end of 2025, down from roughly 151 million in 2018 — a loss of around 40 million customers over that period. A joint EY/All India Digital Cable Federation study also found that this decline has cost the local cable-operator industry an estimated 577,000 jobs since 2018. In a survey of over 28,000 local cable operators, 93% said their monthly take-home income had fallen since 2018. *(Source: [Indiantelevision.com — India's pay TV reached a breaking point in 2025](https://indiantelevision.com/specials/year-enders/india%E2%80%99s-pay-tv-reached-a-breaking-point-in-2025-251230))*
- **The direct cause, verified rather than assumed:** India's broadband internet connections grew from about 61 million in March 2014 to about 996 million by September 2025 — over a fifteenfold increase. As broadband and cheap mobile data became available almost everywhere, the reason to keep paying for a satellite dish or a cable wire disappeared for a large and growing share of Indian households.
- **Why this belongs in a founder's mental map, not just as background trivia:** this is a real, ongoing, large-scale job displacement story — hundreds of thousands of local cable-operator livelihoods disappearing — playing out at the same time enormous new value is being created in streaming (Sub-field 1). It's a clean, concrete illustration of how an entire distribution layer of an industry can be structurally replaced within about a decade, not a slow multi-generational shift.

## Sub-field 4: Music streaming — a small, genuinely puzzling paid market

### What this actually is, and the strange gap between usage and paying customers

Music streaming apps let you listen to songs on demand instead of buying an album or a single track. In India, this category has an unusually large gap between how many people use it and how many people actually pay for it — worth understanding as its own distinct fact, not folded into general streaming.

- **Spotify** leads India's music-streaming market with roughly 26% share, ahead of JioSaavn, YouTube Music, and others. **JioSaavn**, backed by Reliance Jio, is a strong second, leaning on deep Bollywood and regional-language catalogues plus the built-in advantage of easy access through Jio's mobile network. Together, Spotify and JioSaavn now dominate a market that used to have more competitors. *(Sources: [Music Business Worldwide — India added nearly 4m paid music streaming subscriptions in 2025](https://www.musicbusinessworldwide.com/india-added-nearly-4m-paid-music-streaming-subscriptions-in-2025-taking-its-total-to-14-4m-according-to-new-report/), [AppsTimes — Best Music Streaming Apps in India 2025](https://appstimes.in/best-music-streaming-apps-in-india-2025-spotify-jiosaavn-youtube-music-more-compared/))*
- **Several other music apps have simply shut down in India in the past couple of years** — Wynk, Resso, and Hungama among them. This is a genuine, verified consolidation, not speculation: the market went from many competing apps to effectively two dominant ones plus a few smaller survivors like Gaana. *(Source: [Music Ally — Shut-downs, watching vs. listening, and future growth](https://musically.com/2025/04/02/shut-downs-watching-vs-listening-and-future-growth-indias-dsp-market-today/))*
- **The paid-subscriber number is the genuinely load-bearing fact here:** India added nearly 4 million paid music-streaming subscriptions in 2025, bringing the total to about 14.4 million. This number matters because of how small it is relative to India's population and its huge number of free, ad-supported listeners. Most Indians who "use" Spotify or JioSaavn use the free, ad-supported version, not the paid subscription. This is a genuinely different pattern from mature Western streaming markets, where paid subscriptions are a much larger share of total listeners.
- **Why the free/paid gap matters structurally, explained plainly:** every stream on the free tier still generates a royalty obligation to the artist and record label, funded only by advertising, which pays much less per listen than a paid subscription does. A large free listener base with a small paid base means these companies are, in a real sense, subsidizing a huge amount of free listening with a comparatively thin paying-customer base — a structurally harder economic position than a market where most listeners pay.

## Sub-field 5: Gaming — the sub-field that just had its business model deleted by law

### What this actually is, and the single most important event in this entire sector as of 2026

Online gaming in India historically split into two very different businesses that got lumped under one label: **real-money gaming (RMG)** — games like fantasy sports, rummy, and poker where users pay to enter and can win real cash — and **casual/non-monetary gaming**, where users just play for entertainment, often paying only for cosmetic in-game items.

- **On August 21, 2025, the Indian Parliament passed the Promotion and Regulation of Online Gaming Act, effectively banning all forms of online money gaming nationwide** — this includes games of pure skill, games of pure chance, and anything in between. This is a sweeping, verified, already-enacted law, not a proposal. It banned the games themselves, plus their advertising, plus the financial transactions that supported them. *(Sources: [TechLusive — Government Bans Online Money Games Under New Gaming Act 2025](https://www.techlusive.in/news/government-bans-online-money-games-under-new-gaming-act-2025-impact-on-dream11-my11circle-rummy-apps-1652158/), [PlayToday — India's New Online Gaming Law in 2025](https://playtoday.co/blog/guides/india-new-online-gambling-law/))*
- **This single law dismantled what had been the majority of India's entire gaming industry revenue.** RMG reportedly accounted for around 85.7% of India's roughly ₹31,938 crore (about $3.7-3.8 billion) gaming industry revenue in 2024, according to a WinZO/Interactive Entertainment and Innovation Council report. **This is the load-bearing fact of this entire sub-field:** it explains why this is described as an industry-defining shock, rather than a routine regulatory tightening. *(Source: [Outlook Business — From Unicorns to ED Raids](https://www.outlookbusiness.com/in-depth/year-ender-from-unicorns-to-ed-raids-how-real-money-gaming-unravelled-in-2025))*
- **Dream11**, India's largest fantasy sports platform, completely shut down its real-money business, with its CEO reportedly telling employees there was "no legal pathway to continue operations once the law takes effect." Other major players — Games24x7's My11Circle and RummyCircle, Paytm's First Games, Mobile Premier League (MPL), Junglee Games, PokerBaazi, WinZO, Zupee, and GamesKraft — all suspended or wound down their real-money offerings. Penalties for continuing to offer such games can reach 3 years imprisonment and a ₹1 crore fine for a first offence, rising to 5 years and ₹2 crore for repeat offences. *(Source: [TechCrunch — India bans real-money gaming, threatening a $23 billion industry](https://techcrunch.com/2025/08/20/india-bans-real-money-gaming-threatening-a-23-billion-industry/))*
- **Why this happened, stated as the government's own reasoning, not as this document's opinion:** the government described the law as protecting citizens from the financial and psychological harm of real-money gaming, and explicitly separated this from other forms of online gaming, which it says it wants to "promote and regulate" rather than ban.

### What the industry is doing now — a live, real-time pivot worth understanding

- Companies are pivoting toward **esports** (organized, sponsor-funded competitive gaming without cash betting) and **casual, non-monetary gaming**, both of which the new law explicitly permits. Sponsor-backed tournaments and professional leagues have reportedly drawn renewed brand interest specifically because they now occupy a clear, legally safe space the older cash-based model didn't have.
- **WinZO**, one of the affected companies, launched **ZO TV** — an in-app short-video platform showing "micro dramas" — directly inside its existing app, as a way to keep monetizing its existing user base without cash gaming. This is a genuinely interesting pivot because it moves WinZO from gaming into the micro-drama category covered in Sub-field 9 below, showing how fast companies are searching for a new business model after this shock. WinZO is also reportedly expanding into international markets like Brazil and the US, where no equivalent ban exists.
- **Nazara Technologies**, a gaming company with real-money exposure through investments like PokerBaazi, has reportedly pivoted its strategy abroad in response to the ban. *(Source: [Whalesbook — India's Real-Money Gaming Ban Hits Nazara Technologies](https://www.whalesbook.com/news/English/tech/Indias-Real-Money-Gaming-Ban-Hits-Nazara-Technologies-Company-Pivots-Abroad/69e8b764bca97ee1069fbfd4))*
- **The honest lesson for a founder, stated as reasoning:** this is the single clearest example in this entire document — arguably clearer even than fintech's BNPL cautionary tale in `sectors/fintech-deep-dive.md` — of a large, seemingly thriving Indian business model being eliminated by a single act of Parliament, essentially overnight. Anyone evaluating a gaming idea in India today must treat cash-prize mechanics as legally closed, not as a regulatory grey area to be managed carefully. The overall online gaming market is still projected to grow substantially — from about $3.7 billion in 2024 to a projected $9.1 billion by 2029 — but that projection now depends entirely on non-monetary formats, since the cash-gaming segment that drove most of the prior number is gone.

## Sub-field 6: The creator economy and influencer marketing

### What this actually is, and why it's a genuinely distinct sub-field from traditional advertising

The creator economy is the business of individual people — not traditional media companies — building an audience on platforms like YouTube, Instagram, and now short-video apps, and earning money from that audience through brand sponsorships, platform ad-revenue sharing, and their own products.

- Estimates suggest India now has around 4-4.4 million active people who describe themselves as content creators, with Instagram being the largest single platform for creators (an estimated 3.3-3.7 million creators use it as their primary platform). Separately, BCG has estimated that India has around 2-2.5 million "monetized" creators — meaning creators who actually earn money from their content, as opposed to everyone who simply posts content. **This monetized-versus-total-creator distinction matters:** it shows that having an audience and actually making a living from it are two very different things, and only a modest fraction of all "creators" clear that bar. *(Source: [Coherent Market Insights — India Creator Economy Market Forecast](https://www.coherentmarketinsights.com/industry-reports/india-creator-economy-market))*
- India's influencer-marketing sector specifically (the sub-slice where brands pay creators to promote products) is estimated at roughly ₹3,000-3,500 crore in 2025, projected to grow to roughly ₹4,500-5,000 crore by 2027. **These are industry-research estimates, not audited government figures, and should be read as directional rather than precise.** *(Source: [Spherical Insights — India's Influencer Marketing Industry to Reach Rs 5,000 Crore By 2027](https://www.sphericalinsights.com/blogs/india-s-influencer-marketing-industry-to-reach-rs-5-000-crore-by-2027-as-creator-economy-formalises))*
- **YouTube's specific economic footprint in India is a rare case with somewhat more solid backing:** YouTube has reported contributing roughly $1.8 billion to India's GDP and supporting around 930,000 jobs in its associated creative ecosystem in 2024, and has separately committed to investing $100 million across fiscal years 2025-2026 to grow Indian creators and media partners. **Even this figure should be read carefully — it is YouTube's own commissioned/reported economic-impact estimate, not an independent government audit,** but it is more directly sourced to the platform itself than most of the broader market-size estimates in this section.
- **Brand sponsorships and paid partnerships remain the single largest way creators actually make money**, holding an estimated 31.4% share of creator monetization in 2025 — larger than platform ad-revenue sharing or direct-to-fan payments. This is a genuinely important structural fact: most working Indian creators depend on brand deals, not on a platform simply paying them for views, which makes their income fundamentally tied to advertiser budgets and brand relationships rather than pure audience size.

### The business layer built around creators

- **MCNs (Multi-Channel Networks)** and influencer-marketing agencies sit between brands and creators, similar in function to how a talent agency represents actors. They negotiate deals, manage multiple creators at once, and take a cut of the brand-deal revenue. Platforms like **Kofluence** operate in this space, connecting brands to a network of creators and handling the logistics of a campaign.
- **Why this matters for a founder:** the creator economy in India is still maturing from an informal, one-off "DM me for a collab" model toward a more structured, agency-and-platform-mediated one. This transition — sometimes called "professionalization" — is itself a genuine business opportunity area, since a lot of the tooling (payments, contracts, campaign measurement) that exists for creators in more mature markets like the US is comparatively less developed in India.

## Sub-field 7: Animation, VFX, and gaming-art outsourcing — a B2B export business hiding inside a consumer sector

### What this actually is, and why it doesn't behave like the rest of this document

This sub-field is genuinely different from everything else in this deep-dive. It is a **B2B, global-services export business** — Indian studios doing animation, visual effects (VFX), and game-art work for foreign film studios, streaming platforms, and game developers — not a consumer-facing Indian audience business at all. This is conceptually the same pattern as India's IT-services industry (from Stage 2's Batch 5), just applied to visual entertainment work instead of general software.

- **Mumbai is reported to be the largest visual-effects hub in the world by headcount**, and six of the world's ten fastest-growing VFX and animation hubs are reportedly in India. This is a genuinely notable global-leadership claim, not just an aspirational one — it reflects real, large-scale employment of trained artists working on foreign productions. *(Source: [IBEF — India's VFX Industry Is Setting New Global Standards](https://www.ibef.org/blogs/the-rise-of-vfx-industry-in-india))*
- **The core competitive reason, stated plainly:** VFX production in India reportedly costs 40-60% lower than in Western countries for comparable quality, similar to the classic IT-services labor-cost-arbitrage logic already familiar from Stage 2. This is why major global studios outsource VFX and animation work to Indian studios rather than doing it entirely in-house in the US or UK.
- **Lakshya Digital**, founded in 2004, is described as India's largest video-game art outsourcing studio, with over 1,000 professionals and a reported track record across 175+ AAA (large-budget, high-production-value) game titles for over 150 global game developers. This is a concrete, named example of the "picks-and-shovels" B2B pattern also seen in fintech's regtech layer (`sectors/fintech-deep-dive.md`) — a company that doesn't sell to Indian consumers at all, but sells specialized labor and craft to foreign companies. *(Source: [Lakshya Digital — company site](https://lakshyadigital.com/))*
- **AI tools are actively reshaping this sub-field's labor economics**, with tools for rotoscoping (isolating a moving object from video footage), motion-capture cleanup, facial animation, and render optimization reportedly cutting turnaround times and costs. **This should be flagged as an active, ongoing shift, not a settled outcome** — whether AI tools primarily make Indian studios more competitive (by letting them do more work per artist) or primarily threaten entry-level animation jobs (by automating tasks junior artists used to do) is a genuinely open question this research did not resolve.
- **Why this matters for a founder specifically:** this is the one part of Media & Entertainment in India that behaves almost exactly like an IT-services business rather than a "hits-driven," audience-dependent entertainment business. It rewards operational reliability, cost discipline, and craft skill — not marketing or brand-building the way most of the rest of this sector does. A founder drawn to "media" for the glamour of content creation should understand this sub-field is a fundamentally different kind of business to build.

## Sub-field 8: News and journalism — a genuine revenue-model crisis, with real experiments underway

### What this actually is, and the structural problem underneath it

News organizations report and publish current events. In India, this sub-field faces a specific, well-documented economic problem: readers have been trained for decades to expect news for free, funded entirely by advertising, which makes it structurally hard to introduce paid subscriptions later.

- Historically, Indian newspapers and news websites have been heavily ad-supported. Combined with India's enormous linguistic and economic diversity — different regions, languages, and income levels — this has made a single, uniform subscription model difficult to apply nationally, unlike, say, a single English-language paywall in a smaller, more homogenous Western market. *(Source: [Exchange4media — Are Indian newspapers heading towards a paywall model?](https://exchange4media.com/amp/media-print-news/are-indian-newspapers-heading-towards-a-paywall-model-104805.html))*
- **The Ken** is a genuinely distinctive counter-example worth naming specifically. It is an Indian business-journalism website that has run on a **hard paywall** since 2016 — meaning none of its content is free, and it survives purely on subscriber payments rather than advertising. Publishing just one in-depth article a day, it has proven that a small, premium, business-focused subscription publication can sustain itself in India without relying on ad revenue at all. *(Source: [Wikipedia — The Ken](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Ken))*
- Other publishers are experimenting with a **hybrid, metered paywall model** — most content stays free, but high-value or in-depth content sits behind a payment wall, with a limited number of free articles allowed before a reader hits the wall. Times Internet, for example, reportedly saw meaningfully higher revenue-per-user and better subscription conversion after moving to this hybrid approach. **In other words, what this hybrid model means in practice is: give away enough for free to keep your ad-supported reach large, but hold back your best, most valuable reporting specifically for paying subscribers** — trying to capture the benefits of both models rather than picking only one.
- **The honest, unresolved reality, stated plainly:** no major global news market has ever survived on pure advertising alone for a large, quality-driven news organization. India's news publishers are still in the middle of figuring out the right subscription-to-advertising mix, and there is no single settled formula yet, even among the sources reviewed here.

## Sub-field 9: Micro-drama and vertical short-video — the newest, fastest-emerging sub-field in this entire sector

### What this actually is, and why it deserves its own section rather than being folded into "OTT"

**Micro-dramas** are short, serialized fictional stories — think soap-opera-style drama, but broken into 1-3 minute episodes, shot and formatted specifically for vertical viewing on a phone screen, the same orientation as Instagram Reels or YouTube Shorts. This format barely existed in India a year ago. It is now one of the country's most-downloaded app categories, and it deserves its own section because it behaves differently from everything else in Sub-field 1 (OTT): its content is cheaper and faster to produce, its episodes are addictive by design (each one ends on a cliffhanger), and it monetizes through per-episode micro-payments as much as through subscriptions.

- **India's micro-drama market had already crossed roughly $300 million in 2025**, according to Lumikai's "State of India Interactive Media Report," and is projected to reach roughly $4.5 billion by 2030. Cumulative app downloads for this category crossed 250 million by late 2025, having grown roughly 16 times year-over-year. **This is an extremely fast growth rate even by the standards of this document, and it should be read as a very new, still-forming market rather than a mature, settled one.** *(Source: [Marketbites — Can microdramas become India's next big business?](https://marketbites.substack.com/p/can-microdramas-become-indias-next))*
- **Three of India's five most-downloaded streaming apps in 2025 were reportedly vertical-drama platforms.** This is a striking, concrete signal of how fast this format has captured attention, even relative to established players like Netflix or JioHotstar.
- **Who the real players are:** **Kuku TV** (the local incumbent, reportedly accounting for over 60% of category usage in one estimate) is part of **Kuku**, the same company behind the audiobook app Kuku FM. Kuku raised $85 million in a Series C funding round led by Granite Asia in 2025, reportedly valuing the company at around $500 million — roughly double its prior valuation. Kuku had recorded over 10 million paid subscribers combined across its apps, and offers content across eight-plus Indian languages. This is a genuinely important detail: unlike most of the rest of Sub-field 1, this is a homegrown Indian company leading its own category, rather than a foreign platform or a Reliance/Disney joint venture. *(Sources: [TechCrunch — India's Kuku snags $85M as mobile content wars intensify](https://techcrunch.com/2025/10/15/indias-kuku-snags-85m-as-mobile-content-wars-intensify/), [3one4 Capital — Kuku FM Raises $85 Million](https://www.3one4capital.com/blogs/kukufm-raises-85-million-building-indias-first-full-stack-entertainment-platform-in-a-fast-compounding-digital-economy))*
- **Chinese-origin platforms — DramaBox, ShortMax, and ReelShort — are also present in India**, typically leading with AI-dubbed catalogues translated from Chinese-language originals, rather than India-first content. This creates a genuine split within the category: local, language-specific players like Kuku TV competing against foreign platforms whose catalogues are translated rather than natively made for Indian audiences. *(Source: [Medium/Real Reel — Kuku TV and the South-Asian Micro-Drama Boom](https://medium.com/real-reel/kuku-tv-and-the-south-asian-micro-drama-boom-3aefabf48408))*
- **India vs. global comparison, stated precisely:** this format originated in China (where it's a large, already-established category) and has since spread to the US via apps like ReelShort. India's version is following a similar path but with its own distinct local leader (Kuku TV) rather than simply being colonized entirely by the Chinese-origin apps, which is a meaningfully different competitive shape than some other markets these apps have expanded into.

### Why this connects directly back to Sub-field 5 (gaming)

Recall from Sub-field 5 that WinZO, a gaming company whose main business was just banned by law, pivoted specifically into this exact category by launching ZO TV. This is a directly verified, real-time example of how fast capital and company strategy can move from one sub-field to another inside this sector when a regulatory shock hits — worth remembering as you read the gap analysis below.

## Sub-field 10: Podcasts and audio — a genuinely fast-growing, underexploited-in-regional-languages category

### What this actually is, and the scale involved

Podcasts are episodic audio shows, usually available on-demand rather than broadcast live, similar in spirit to radio but delivered over the internet and organized as a series rather than a continuous live stream.

- India's podcasting market was valued at roughly $0.62 billion in 2024, and is projected to reach roughly $2.6 billion by 2030, at an estimated 25% annual growth rate. India reportedly had around 105 million podcast listeners in 2024, with estimates suggesting this crossed 200 million by 2025 — making India the third-largest podcast market in the world, behind only China and the US. *(Sources: [MarknTel Advisors — India Podcasting Market Size](https://www.marknteladvisors.com/research-library/india-podcasting-market.html), [IBEF — The Podcasting Boom in India](https://www.ibef.org/blogs/the-podcasting-boom-in-india-shaping-the-future-of-digital-storytelling))*
- **The genuinely important structural fact here, similar to the micro-drama category above: regional-language content has historically lagged behind English content, but this gap is closing fast.** India officially recognizes more than 20 languages, and early podcast content skewed heavily English, leaving most of the country underserved. Forecasts now suggest up to 85% of podcast content could shift toward regional languages, with Hindi and English settling around 45% and 12% of the market respectively — meaning the other roughly 40%+ would be split across languages like Bengali, Marathi, Tamil, and others. **This should be read as a forecast, not a confirmed outcome.**
- **Spotify India** has been actively expanding its regional podcast slate with exclusive Hindi and South Indian language originals, a direct, verified response to this gap.
- **Why this connects to Sub-field 9's dubbing/localization discussion below:** the same underlying force — India's genuine multi-lingual audience being underserved by English-first content — shows up here, in micro-dramas, and in OTT. It is a recurring structural pattern across this entire sector, not a coincidence specific to any one format.

## Cross-cutting theme: AI-powered dubbing and localization — a real, fast-growing B2B layer underneath everything above

- India's domestic dubbing market — covering film dubbing, OTT localization, micro-drama dubbing, YouTube creator dubbing, and e-learning localization — is estimated at roughly ₹2,500-3,500 crore (about $300-420 million) in 2026, growing at an estimated 18-22% annually. This growth is reportedly being driven specifically by micro-drama demand (Sub-field 9), regional-language expansion (Sub-fields 1 and 10), and YouTube creator adoption (Sub-field 6). *(Source: [Sukudo Studios — Dubbing Industry Trends 2026](https://www.sukudostudios.com/blog/dubbing-industry-trends-2026-micro-dramas-ai-market))*
- **AI has measurably compressed dubbing timelines.** One reported example: a streaming platform used an AI localization tool to dub a series into Hindi, Tamil, and Telugu in about four hours, a task that reportedly used to take about four weeks manually. Most companies in this space now use a **hybrid model** — AI handles the speed-sensitive first pass, and human editors handle quality control and cultural nuance afterward. **In other words, what this hybrid model means in practice is: the AI does the rough translation and voice generation fast, and a human checks it afterward to catch mistakes or awkward phrasing the AI got wrong.**
- **Over 90% of Indian internet users reportedly prefer content in their native language.** This is the demand-side fact that makes localization/dubbing structurally important across nearly every sub-field in this document, rather than a narrow, specialized niche.
- **Why this deserves a cross-cutting mention rather than its own numbered sub-field:** unlike the sub-fields above, dubbing/localization isn't a destination Indians spend leisure time in — it's B2B infrastructure that sits underneath OTT (Sub-field 1), micro-dramas (Sub-field 9), podcasts (Sub-field 10), and the creator economy (Sub-field 6), similar in spirit to how the fintech deep-dive treats regtech and KYC vendors as an invisible B2B layer underneath consumer fintech apps.

## Cross-cutting pain points and honest challenges

- **Regulatory shock risk is real, large, and has already happened once in this exact sector, in this exact period.** Sub-field 5's gaming ban is not a hypothetical risk to flag cautiously — it is a confirmed, enacted law that deleted the majority of an entire sub-field's revenue virtually overnight. Any founder evaluating a media/entertainment idea involving real-money mechanics, betting-adjacent formats, or anything a regulator could plausibly view as gambling needs to treat this as a live, demonstrated pattern, not a remote tail risk.
- **Platform/distribution concentration recurs across multiple sub-fields, similar to the pattern already documented in `sectors/food-beverage-deep-dive.md` for Swiggy/Zomato.** JioStar (JioHotstar) now controls both major streaming distribution and India's single most valuable live-sports content asset (cricket) simultaneously. This is worth naming explicitly as a recurring structural pattern in Indian digital markets, not unique to this sector — a small number of large, often telecom-linked or conglomerate-backed platforms end up controlling both the pipes and a uniquely valuable piece of content or supply.
- **Piracy is a large, quantified, ongoing cost specific to this sector** (Sub-field 2), unlike most of the other sectors covered in this project's prior deep-dives. It shapes pricing, content-protection spending, and windowing decisions (how long to keep a film exclusive to cinemas before releasing it to streaming) throughout the whole content-and-distribution chain.
- **Government shows up differently depending on the specific sub-field — it's worth being precise rather than assuming one uniform relationship, the same caution flagged in the food-beverage deep-dive.** In gaming (Sub-field 5), government directly banned an entire business model by law. In OTT (Sub-field 1), government's role is lighter-touch: the IT Rules, 2021 require platforms to self-regulate their content and handle user complaints, rather than requiring pre-release government censorship the way the Central Board of Film Certification (CBFC) does for theatrical films. The CBFC has explicitly clarified that it does not certify OTT content at all — that responsibility sits with the platforms themselves under the IT Rules. *(Source: [The Hans India — CBFC clarifies no censor on OTT content](https://www.thehansindia.com/cinema/cbfc-clarifies-no-censor-on-ott-content-responsibility-rests-with-platforms-1032074))*
- **The "hits business" problem recurs across film, music, and gaming:** unlike a typical subscription software business, a huge share of this sector's revenue depends on unpredictable, one-off successes — a hit film, a viral song, a breakout creator — that are extremely hard to reliably manufacture in advance. This is a genuinely different risk profile from most of the other sectors this project has covered so far, and it should shape how a founder thinks about revenue predictability if they're building anything content-dependent, rather than infrastructure-dependent (like dubbing tools or creator-economy back-office software).

## Gap and opportunity analysis

Of everything surfaced above, four gaps stand out as both genuinely underserved and realistically sized for a new entrant, as opposed to areas that are large but already dominated by well-capitalized incumbents (mainstream OTT, mainstream multiplex cinema) or areas that are structurally closed by law right now (real-money gaming).

### Gap 1 — Tools and infrastructure for the creator economy's "back office"

**The gap:** Sub-field 6 showed India's creator economy has grown to millions of active creators, but only a smaller fraction are genuinely monetized, and most of those depend on brand-deal income rather than platform payouts. The tooling that exists for creators in more mature markets — contract templates, payment collection across multiple brand deals, tax/GST handling for freelance creator income, campaign-performance reporting for brands — appears comparatively less developed in India specifically, based on the sources reviewed in this pass.

- **Existing competitors/players:** MCNs and influencer-marketing agencies, like Kofluence, already exist and handle some of this by acting as an intermediary between brands and creators. This means the "connect brands to creators" piece of this gap is not empty. The narrower, less-covered slice is creator-facing financial and administrative tooling — helping an individual creator manage multiple simultaneous brand-deal invoices, track GST obligations on freelance income, and get paid reliably and on time — which is a different product than a brand-facing campaign-matching marketplace.
- **What a new entrant could do differently:** build a lightweight, creator-first financial and admin tool — invoicing, GST-compliant billing, payment tracking across multiple brand relationships, and simple analytics a creator can show a prospective brand sponsor. This is B2B2C in Stage 1's vocabulary if sold to MCNs/agencies who then offer it to their creators, or straightforwardly B2C if sold directly to individual creators as a subscription.
- **Honest risks:** most individual creators, outside the top tier, likely have thin and irregular income, meaning willingness to pay for a dedicated tool may be low unless it clearly saves them either significant time or significant money (for example, by preventing a missed GST filing). This is also a genuinely crowded adjacent space globally — creator-economy tooling is an active area of startup investment in the US — so a founder would need a specifically India-shaped angle, such as deep GST/tax-compliance integration, rather than a generic global tool translated into India.
- **Economics:** no verified revenue or pricing data for an India-specific creator-back-office tool was found in this research pass. **This is flagged as unresourced** — a real founder would need direct interviews with mid-tier creators (not just the biggest names) about their actual current pain points and willingness to pay.
- **Evidence of customer want:** indirect but plausible. The scale of the influencer-marketing market (Sub-field 6) and the explicit "professionalization" trend both suggest real, growing administrative complexity for creators as their income streams multiply. Direct survey evidence of creators specifically wanting a paid tool for this, as opposed to just muddling through with spreadsheets, was not found in this pass.

### Gap 2 — India-first, India-native micro-drama content, rather than translated/dubbed foreign catalogues

**The gap:** Sub-field 9 showed the micro-drama category in India is genuinely new and growing extremely fast, but a real split already exists between India-native players (Kuku TV) and foreign, mostly Chinese-origin platforms relying on AI-dubbed translated content (DramaBox, ShortMax, ReelShort). The category is young enough that its ultimate content mix — dubbed-foreign versus India-native-original — is still being contested, not settled.

- **Existing competitors/players:** Kuku TV is already the reported category leader by usage and has raised substantial capital, so this is emphatically not an empty field. The specific narrower opportunity is in **regional-language-first, India-native storytelling** (built around specifically Indian tropes, family dynamics, and cultural references) rather than either English-first content or dubbed-Chinese content — a wedge Kuku TV is already pursuing but that likely still has room given how new and fast-growing the whole category is.
- **What a new entrant could realistically do differently:** rather than competing head-on with Kuku TV's existing scale, a founder could focus deeply on one or two specific regional languages and genres Kuku TV hasn't fully saturated yet, using the AI-dubbing infrastructure described in the cross-cutting section above to produce content cheaply and quickly, and prioritizing genuinely local writing talent over adapting foreign scripts.
- **Honest risks:** this could be a trap in the classic sense — it may look wide open only because it's brand new, and a well-funded incumbent (Kuku TV, or a JioStar/Zee5 response) could crowd it out quickly once they notice a genuine gap forming. Content businesses are also inherently hit-driven (see the cross-cutting pain points above), meaning even a well-executed micro-drama studio could fail to produce a viral hit and simply run out of money first. This category's extremely fast historical growth rate (16x year-over-year in downloads) is also unlikely to be sustainable forever, and a founder should not extrapolate that pace indefinitely into a business plan.
- **Economics:** the market-size and growth figures cited in Sub-field 9 are industry-research estimates (Lumikai), not audited figures, and no per-title production-cost or revenue-per-user data for India specifically was found in this research pass. **This is flagged as unresourced** and would need direct primary research — for example, talking to smaller regional micro-drama studios about their actual production costs — before committing capital.
- **Evidence of customer want:** strong at the aggregate level — download and usage numbers are large and genuinely verified as a fast-moving trend, and three of India's five most-downloaded streaming apps in 2025 were reportedly in this category. However, evidence of specific unmet demand for India-native-versus-dubbed content specifically (as opposed to demand for the format generally) is inferred from the existence of Kuku TV's success, not from a direct consumer survey found in this research pass.

### Gap 3 — Podcast and audio monetization tools specifically built for regional-language creators

**The gap:** Sub-field 10 showed podcasting is growing fast in India, and that regional-language content is structurally underserved relative to demand, mirroring the same pattern found in micro-dramas and OTT. Large platforms like Spotify are investing in regional-language originals themselves, but the actual monetization tooling available to independent regional-language podcast creators — ad-insertion, sponsorship marketplaces, listener-supported subscriptions — appears comparatively undeveloped for non-English, non-Hindi creators specifically, based on the sources reviewed.

- **Existing competitors/players:** Spotify and JioSaavn already distribute podcasts and are investing directly in regional content, so distribution itself is not the gap. The narrower gap is monetization infrastructure for smaller, independent regional-language podcasters who aren't part of a platform's own commissioned-content program — no dedicated India-specific ad-marketplace or sponsorship-matching tool for regional podcasters was identified in this research pass. **This is flagged as an absence-of-evidence finding, which is weaker than a confirmed absence, and would need direct verification** — for example, checking podcast-creator forums or existing podcast-hosting platforms' feature sets — before treating it as confirmed open ground.
- **What a new entrant could do differently:** build a lightweight ad-sales and sponsorship-matching layer specifically for mid-tier regional-language podcasters, who are too small for platforms to commission directly but too niche for a national ad agency to bother targeting individually. This could resemble an MCN model (Sub-field 6) but built specifically for audio and specifically for regional languages, rather than video and English/Hindi.
- **Honest risks:** podcast advertising revenue per listener is generally lower than video advertising revenue per viewer, meaning the absolute revenue pool this gap could capture may be smaller than it first appears. Regional-language advertiser demand may also simply be thinner right now than Hindi/English advertiser demand, since many national brands still concentrate marketing budgets on Hindi and English audiences — this would need direct verification with regional advertisers before assuming the demand is there.
- **Economics:** no verified revenue, CPM (cost per thousand ad impressions — a standard way advertising is priced), or creator-payout data specific to Indian regional-language podcasting was found in this research pass. **This is flagged as unresourced.**
- **Evidence of customer want:** moderate. The scale of India's podcast-listener growth and the explicit regional-language demand shift are both genuinely sourced. But direct evidence that independent regional podcasters specifically want and would pay for better monetization tooling, as opposed to platforms simply commissioning more of their own regional content directly, was not found in this research pass.

### Gap 4 — Niche, non-monetary gaming formats built specifically for the post-ban legal landscape

**The gap:** Sub-field 5 showed the 2025 gaming ban eliminated real-money gaming but explicitly preserved space for esports and casual, non-monetary gaming. Established players (WinZO, Nazara, MPL) are actively pivoting into this space right now, which means this is a genuinely live, still-forming market rather than a settled one — but it's also one where the "obvious" incumbents have a head start, given their existing user bases and brand recognition from their prior real-money products.

- **Existing competitors/players:** WinZO (pivoting to ZO TV and international real-money markets), Nazara (pivoting internationally), and MPL are the most visible incumbents actively repositioning. No new, dedicated India-first casual/esports-only company built from scratch specifically for this post-ban landscape was identified as a clear leader in this research pass — this space is genuinely young enough that leadership hasn't consolidated the way it has in OTT or micro-dramas.
- **What a new entrant could realistically do differently:** build sponsor-funded, tournament-style casual or esports products from a clean slate, without the baggage or user expectations of a former real-money product, and without needing to convince a previously cash-motivated user base to accept a no-cash-prize product instead. A founder unencumbered by an existing real-money business might design the product experience around genuine entertainment and competition from day one, rather than retrofitting an old cash-game user experience.
- **Honest risks:** this is arguably the least mature and most uncertain of the four gaps. It exists specifically because of a very recent, disruptive regulatory event, and it isn't clear yet whether Indian users who were drawn to these apps specifically for the chance to win real money will engage anywhere near as much with a no-cash-prize version of the same game. There's a real possibility that much of the prior real-money user base simply disengages rather than converting to casual play, which would make this a much smaller opportunity than the old real-money market was. The regulatory environment itself could also still evolve further — this is a genuinely fresh law, and how strictly and consistently it gets enforced over the next few years is not yet fully proven.
- **Economics:** the ₹31,938 crore prior gaming-industry figure and the projected $9.1 billion by 2029 figure (Sub-field 5) are both industry-report estimates (WinZO/IEIC and other market-research aggregation), not government-audited figures, and neither breaks out how much of the future growth is expected to come specifically from brand-new, non-monetary-first entrants versus pivoted incumbents. **This is flagged as unresourced at the level of detail a real business plan would need.**
- **Evidence of customer want:** weak to moderate, and this is the most speculative of the four gaps. The government's own stated rationale (protecting citizens from gambling-related harm) suggests real concern about the previous model's effect on users, which is a form of evidence about a problem existing — but it is not the same as evidence that users specifically want a non-monetary replacement product. This would need direct primary research — for example, tracking actual user retention numbers for the pivoted products over the next year — before treating this as a confirmed, provable demand.
