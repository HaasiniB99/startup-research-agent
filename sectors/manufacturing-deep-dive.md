# Stage 3 + 4 — Sector Deep-Dive: Manufacturing (India)

Geography: **India specifically**, throughout. This document talks about global comparisons too — China, Vietnam, Bangladesh, and others. Every time one of these comparisons appears, it is called out explicitly. It is never assumed that what happens in another country automatically also happens in India. This document does not repeat Stage 1's basic vocabulary (B2B, B2C, product vs. service, etc.). It only applies that vocabulary. If you need the definitions themselves, see `foundations/stage1-business-foundations.md`.

**What was already covered in Stage 2 (`sectors/sector-survey.md`), and is not repeated here:** The survey already gave you some basics. It explained the split between heavy manufacturing (steel, machinery, auto assembly) and light or consumer manufacturing (electronics assembly, textiles, packaged food). It explained the simple profit formula for manufacturing: you take raw materials plus labor plus machine time, and sell the result at a markup (a markup means selling for more than it cost you to make, and keeping the difference). It also introduced contract manufacturing — this means a factory makes products for other companies' brands, instead of selling under its own brand name. It covered capital intensity (how much upfront money a business needs before it can even start operating) as a barrier to entry in heavy manufacturing. And it gave you the one-line fact that India's government has been pushing domestic manufacturing to reduce reliance on imports. This document assumes you already know all of that, and it goes deeper underneath it.

**What's in scope here:** This deep-dive treats "Manufacturing" the way India's policy apparatus (the government's system of departments, rules, and schemes) and industry actually segment it today. It is not one undifferentiated sector. It is a set of distinct sub-fields. Each sub-field is shaped by very different things: how much capital it needs, how much the government is involved, and how much global competitive pressure it faces. The sub-fields covered here are: electronics and mobile phone assembly, the semiconductor build-out, auto and auto-components (including the shift to electric vehicles), pharmaceuticals and bulk drugs, textiles and apparel, and the broader MSME-dominated base of "everything else" (toys, footwear, general engineering, and contract manufacturing for D2C brands) — this last group is actually where most manufacturing employment sits. This is a deliberately deep pass on structure, real players, and honest economics. It is not a repeat of the survey-level overview.

## Sub-field 1: Electronics and mobile phone assembly

### What this actually is
This is the sub-field the government has bet on hardest. It is also the one with the clearest, most-verified success story in Indian manufacturing over the last five years. The activity itself is simple to picture: assembling phones, laptops, and other electronic devices — largely for global brands — inside India. The alternative, which used to be the norm, was importing these devices fully built from another country.

- The mechanism driving this is the **PLI (Production Linked Incentive) scheme**. This is a government program. In plain terms: the government pays a manufacturer a cash reward, but only for the *extra* production or sales the manufacturer actually achieves inside India, above some baseline. It is not a tax break, and it is not a subsidy on the raw materials or inputs a company buys. In other words, this scheme pays companies for making more, not just for existing. The PLI scheme now covers 14 different sectors. Electronics has been one of the two clearest early winners, alongside pharmaceuticals. Together, these two sectors account for the large majority of the incentive money that has actually been paid out so far. *(Source: [PIB — PLI Scheme drives strong industry participation across 14 sectors](https://www.pib.gov.in/PressReleasePage.aspx?PRID=2230621&reg=3&lang=1))*
- Electronics production supported by the PLI scheme has grown sharply since the scheme's first full year. Mobile phones and smartphones have become India's single largest export category, counting every brand together. This is a genuinely significant shift. A decade ago, phones in India were overwhelmingly imported, not made locally.
- Apple's iPhone exports out of India grew sharply through 2025. This continues a multi-year trend: Apple has been shifting more and more of its phone assembly work to India. This is a real, well-documented shift. However, the exact share of global iPhone production that India now represents changes often. It should be treated as a moving number that gets updated frequently, not as a fixed fact. *(Source: China Plus One research pass)*

### How the business model actually works
- This sub-field runs almost entirely on two related models from Stage 2: the **EMS (Electronics Manufacturing Services)** model and the **contract manufacturing** model. Here is how it works in practice: global brands like Apple and Samsung design the product and own the brand name. A separate company then physically assembles that product under contract. This is a pure **B2B** relationship — meaning the assembler's actual customer is the brand itself, not the person who eventually buys the phone in a store.
- **Dixon Technologies** is India's clearest home-grown EMS success story. It is a listed Indian company (meaning its shares are traded on the stock market). Dixon assembles products for multiple global and domestic brands, across categories like mobile phones, LED TVs, and appliances. Dixon does not own any consumer-facing brand of its own. In other words, you will never see "Dixon" printed on a phone box in a shop — but Dixon may have built the phone inside that box. This is a textbook illustration of the contract-manufacturing concept from Stage 2, in action.
- **Foxconn** is headquartered in Taiwan and is the world's largest electronics contract manufacturer. It operates in India through local subsidiaries (a subsidiary is a company that is owned by a larger parent company). Its Indian unit is sometimes referred to as Yuzhan Technology India. Foxconn is primarily responsible for assembling iPhones in India. It is also reportedly expanding into components and, reportedly, into semiconductors.
- The government has recently widened its support one layer down the value chain (the value chain is the full sequence of steps needed to turn raw materials into a finished product a customer buys). It did this through the **Electronics Components Manufacturing Scheme (ECMS)**. Here is why this scheme exists: assembling a phone in India still mostly means importing the high-value components — chips, displays, camera modules — from elsewhere. So assembly alone was not enough; the government wanted to pull the component-making itself into India too. Newly cleared proposals under this scheme include Dixon, Foxconn, Samsung Display, and Hindalco. Hindalco is an aluminium and metals company — its presence on this list shows that even suppliers of basic raw materials are being pulled into this push. These approved projects are spread across eight different states, rather than being concentrated in just one or two existing manufacturing hubs. *(Source: [Upstox — Govt clears 22 proposals under electronics component scheme](https://upstox.com/news/business-news/latest-updates/govt-clears-22-proposals-under-electronics-component-scheme-dixon-foxconn-hindalco-enter-approval-list/article-187120/))*

### The honest gap underneath the headline success
- **Assembling a phone is not the same as manufacturing its high-value components.** This is the single most important structural fact to understand about India's electronics story. India has gotten very good, very fast, at *final assembly* — meaning putting an already-designed phone together from parts that were mostly imported. But the components that actually carry the real profit margin — chips, precision optics, display panels — are still largely imported from elsewhere. This is directly comparable to the "MDR is zero" fact from the fintech deep-dive: it is the one load-bearing fact that explains why the rest of this sub-field's strategy exists at all. In other words, everything described next (the ECMS scheme, and the semiconductor push in Sub-field 2) only makes sense once you understand that assembly alone does not capture the real money.
- This is precisely why the government is now trying to push incentives one layer upstream, through the ECMS scheme described above. It is also why the government is pushing, even more ambitiously, into semiconductors themselves — covered next, in Sub-field 2. As long as India only does assembly, there is a cap on how much value it actually captures from each device made.

## Sub-field 2: The semiconductor build-out

### What this actually is
Semiconductors are computer chips. Every electronic device described in Sub-field 1 depends on them. Until recently, India manufactured essentially none of them domestically. Every chip inside every "Made in India" phone was — and mostly still is — imported from elsewhere. The **India Semiconductor Mission (ISM)** is the government's attempt to change this. It is a multi-year, multi-billion-dollar program.

### Where this actually stands, concretely, as of 2026
- **Tata Electronics** has partnered with Taiwan's **PSMC (Powerchip Semiconductor Manufacturing Corporation)**. Together they are building a fabrication plant, usually just called a "fab," at **Dholera, Gujarat**. A fab is the facility that actually turns raw silicon into working chips. It is the single most capital-intensive step in the entire chip supply chain — think of it as the difference between a workshop that assembles furniture from pre-cut wood, versus the sawmill that turns whole trees into that wood in the first place. This plant is targeting its first working chips by late 2026. The chips it will make are aimed at automotive, industrial, and IoT-grade uses (IoT means "Internet of Things" — everyday devices like sensors or smart appliances that connect to the internet), not at cutting-edge smartphone or AI chips. Tata Electronics also signed an agreement with **ASML** in 2026 to secure equipment supply. ASML is a Dutch company that makes specialized lithography machines (machines that essentially "print" chip patterns onto silicon) — every advanced fab in the world depends on ASML's machines to operate. *(Source: [Abhishek Gautam — India Semiconductor Mission 2.0](https://www.abhs.in/blog/india-semiconductor-mission-2-tata-micron-2nm-dholera-2026))*
- **Micron Technology** opened India's first operational facility of this mission cycle, in Sanand, Gujarat, in February 2026. It is important to be precise about what this facility actually does. It is an **ATMP (Assembly, Test, and Packaging)** plant. That means it takes chips that were manufactured somewhere else, and it assembles, tests, and packages them. It does not make raw silicon chips from scratch. This distinction is genuinely useful for a founder to know. In Indian news coverage, the phrase "semiconductor facility" can mean two very different things: a fab (making chips from scratch, extremely capital-intensive) or an ATMP plant (finishing and packaging already-made chips, meaningfully less capital-intensive). Confusing the two would make you misjudge how far India has actually come in this space.
- Government officials have stated a roadmap: four plants operational by end-2026, two more in 2027, and India's first full fabrication unit ready at Dholera by 2028. This should be read as a stated government target, not a guaranteed outcome. Large infrastructure timelines slip often, in every country, so this roadmap needs to be treated with some caution.

### Why this matters for a founder evaluating the sector
- In this sub-field, the "gap" is the absence of domestic chip fabrication. That gap is being closed by a small number of extremely large, extremely capital-intensive, multi-billion-dollar projects. These projects are run by giant companies and the government itself. This is genuinely not a space a new, independent startup can enter directly — not at the fab level, and not at the ATMP level either. The realistic opportunity for a smaller company sits in the ecosystem that grows up around this build-out instead: things like chip design services, testing and calibration equipment, specialty gases and materials, or cleanroom construction and maintenance. Building the fab itself is not realistic for a small company; supplying or servicing the fab-building effort is where the opening actually is.

## Sub-field 3: Auto and auto-components, including the EV transition

### What this actually is
India's automotive sector splits cleanly into two separate businesses. The first is **OEMs (Original Equipment Manufacturers)** — these are the companies that design and sell the actual vehicle under their own brand name, such as Tata Motors, Mahindra, Maruti Suzuki, and the two-wheeler makers. The second is the **auto-components industry** — a much larger and more fragmented set of companies that supply the thousands of individual parts that go into each vehicle. This second group operates almost entirely on a **B2B** basis, meaning their customers are the OEMs, not everyday car buyers.

### How the EV transition is reshaping this sub-field specifically
- A conventional internal-combustion (petrol or diesel) vehicle has roughly 30,000 individual parts. An electric vehicle (EV) has meaningfully fewer — a widely cited estimate is around a one-third reduction. Why? Because an EV doesn't need an engine, an exhaust system, or a traditional transmission. This is a genuinely important structural fact for anyone evaluating auto-components as a space. Here is what it means in practice: a supplier whose entire business is built around parts specific to petrol or diesel engines — fuel injectors, exhaust systems, transmission components — faces a real shrinking of its market as EVs become more common. This is not a hypothetical risk. It is a mechanical certainty, built directly into how EVs are engineered.
- Because of this, India's auto-component makers are running **two product lines in parallel**: one for existing petrol/diesel vehicles, and one for EVs. This creates a genuine "dual investment squeeze" — a situation where a company has to spend money in two directions at once. They must keep serving today's still-large demand for petrol/diesel parts. At the same time, they must invest in EV-specific capability — batteries, electric motors, power electronics — that may not pay off for years. This is a more acute version of transition risk than most other manufacturing sub-fields face.
- Battery cell manufacturing is the highest-value new component category in an EV. It is being built out domestically, with support from the PLI scheme described in Sub-field 1, by players including **Ola Electric**, **Amara Raja**, **Exide Industries**, and **Reliance New Energy**. Separately, **Tata Motors** is building its own battery gigafactory (a very large-scale battery manufacturing plant). This is a genuinely new domestic capability. It barely existed in India a few years ago.
- **The honest gap:** For the highest-cost EV parts — battery cells, electric motors, DC-DC converters, on-board chargers — domestic localization remains low. Localization means the share of a component's value that is actually made inside India, rather than imported. This "low" localization is true despite multiple central and state government incentive schemes that are specifically aimed at raising it. *(Source: [Zetwerk — EV Manufacturing in India 2026](https://www.zetwerk.com/blog/manufacturing-technology/ev-manufacturing-india-2026/))*

### Why this matters for India vs. global comparison
China built a fully vertically integrated EV and battery supply chain over roughly a decade (vertically integrated means one company, or one tightly connected group of companies, controls most steps of production, from raw material to finished product). China now dominates global battery cell production as a result. Compared to China, India is still assembling its EV supply chain largely from a standing start. There are real domestic wins in some layers — cell manufacturing is now actually operating, not just planned. But India still relies heavily on imported high-value sub-components in other layers. This is a genuinely different position from electronics, described in Sub-field 1. In electronics, India's role is mature, high-volume assembly of a finished consumer product. In EVs, India is earlier-stage, and it is still building the component base itself from scratch.

## Sub-field 4: Pharmaceuticals and bulk drugs (APIs)

### What this actually is
India is globally known as the "pharmacy of the world." This reputation is specifically about **formulations** — finished medicines like tablets, capsules, and syrups — which India sells at low cost, both domestically and to export markets. Here is something much less well known outside the industry: this entire formulations business depends on **APIs (Active Pharmaceutical Ingredients)** and **KSMs (Key Starting Materials)**. These are the actual chemical compounds that make a drug work — think of them as the raw "active ingredient" flour that later gets baked into the finished "bread" (the tablet or capsule you actually take). India imports the large majority of these raw ingredients from a single country.

### The single most load-bearing fact in this sub-field
- India imports an estimated 70-80% of its total API and KSM requirement from **China**. For specific, individually verified bulk drugs, the dependence is even more extreme. Reported China-import shares are around 91% for Paracetamol, 95.8% for Penicillin and its salts, and 95.2% for Ibuprofen. This specific set of numbers is worth keeping, unlike most figures in this document. Here is why: it is the exact fact that explains the entire policy response described below. It is not routine financial color — it is the actual reason the API-PLI scheme and the "China Plus One" framing (a strategy of diversifying supply chains away from sole reliance on China) exist for this sub-field specifically. *(Source: [Policy Circle — India struggling to free pharma industry from dependence on Chinese APIs](https://www.policycircle.org/industry/apis-import-depencence-on-china/))*
- This dependence became a visible national vulnerability during COVID-19. Disrupted supply from China forced India to restrict exports of 26 drugs, including paracetamol and various antibiotics, in order to protect its own domestic supply. This is a real, documented event. It is not a hypothetical risk.
- The government's response is a dedicated **API PLI scheme** (using the same PLI mechanism explained in Sub-field 1). It runs with a multi-year budget outlay, specifically meant to bring bulk drug manufacturing back onshore. However, multiple sources note that imports from China have continued growing even after the scheme launched. In other words, the fix is, so far, only partially working. *(Source: [Policy Circle — Bulk drugs push must fix India's API dependence](https://www.policycircle.org/industry/bulk-drugs-india-api-dependence/))*

### Why this is structurally different from Sub-fields 1-3
In electronics assembly, India is capturing volume but not the highest-margin component step. In EVs, India is building an entirely new supply chain roughly alongside the rest of the world. Pharma's API dependence is different from both of these. It is a case where India *used to* have more domestic capability, and lost it over decades to cheaper Chinese production. This makes it a genuine reshoring problem (reshoring means bringing back manufacturing capability that used to exist domestically but moved elsewhere) — not a first-time build-out like the other two. This is a meaningfully different starting position. It should not be blended together with the electronics or EV stories.

## Sub-field 5: Textiles and apparel

### What this actually is
This sub-field covers growing or importing fiber (cotton, synthetic materials), spinning that fiber into yarn, weaving or knitting the yarn into fabric, and then cutting and sewing that fabric into finished garments. It is one of India's oldest and most labor-intensive manufacturing sub-fields. Historically, it has also been one of India's largest employers.

### The honest, verified competitive picture
- India is currently the world's sixth-largest textile exporter. This is a real position, though not a dominant one. Comparing export scale rather than exact figures: **Bangladesh** and **Vietnam** have both grown their apparel exports meaningfully faster than India in recent years. Both countries now export considerably more in this category than India does. Meanwhile, **China** remains far larger than all three of these countries combined. *(Source: [Ken Research / stattimes aggregated summary](https://www.stattimes.com/air-cargo/how-india-bangladesh-and-vietnam-are-redrawing-global-textile-maps-1356402))*
- **The structural reason usually cited, and worth naming plainly, is factory scale.** China's textile competitiveness has historically rested on large, vertically integrated mega-factories (recall from Sub-field 3 that "vertically integrated" means one connected operation controls most of the production steps). India's major textile clusters — Tirupur, Surat, Ludhiana, and the Delhi-NCR belt — grew organically into fragmented networks of small and medium enterprises instead. Their average factory size is far smaller than Bangladesh's leading export-oriented units. This directly echoes the "missing middle" problem, which is described in more detail in the MSME section below. It is not a coincidence that the same underlying pattern shows up in more than one sub-field.
- **Bangladesh's specific competitive lever is worth naming, because it is the clearest single explanation for the gap.** Bangladesh pays meaningfully lower garment-worker wages than India. This matters enormously, because in this industry, labor cost is the single largest input after the fabric itself.
- The Indian government has a stated four-point action plan aimed at reversing this trend, with the goal of reaching a much larger apparel-export target by 2030. This is a stated policy goal, not yet an achieved outcome. It is worth tracking as a goal, rather than assuming it will simply happen on its own.

### Why this sub-field is a useful contrast to electronics
Textiles is the clearest example in this document of a labor-intensive, low-capital-barrier manufacturing sub-field where India is actually *losing* relative ground — in this case, to smaller neighboring economies. This is a direct contrast to electronics, described in Sub-field 1, where India has gained ground fast against a much larger, more capital-intensive backdrop. The lesson for a founder: do not treat "manufacturing" as one uniform growth story. The picture varies enormously from one sub-field to the next.

## Sub-field 6: The MSME base — the sector's real center of gravity

### What this actually is
Everything described above is genuinely important. But it is not where most Indian manufacturing employment, or the largest number of companies, actually sits. The large majority of Indian manufacturing happens inside **MSMEs (Micro, Small, and Medium Enterprises)**. These are smaller factories, often family-run or run by a single founder-promoter. They make everything from general engineering components to toys, footwear, and furniture — and, increasingly, goods that are contract-manufactured for D2C (direct-to-consumer) brands that don't own any factory of their own.

### The "missing middle" — a structural pattern worth understanding precisely
- India's manufacturing base is dominated by a huge number of very small (micro) firms. There is a comparatively thin layer of mid-sized firms that have actually managed to scale up. This is a well-documented pattern, often called the **"missing middle."** It matters because it is the same underlying dynamic that drives the textile-cluster fragmentation problem described in Sub-field 5. In other words, without a strong layer of mid-sized firms, India struggles to build the kind of large, efficient, globally competitive factories that countries like China — and increasingly Vietnam and Bangladesh — have built in specific product categories.
- **Financing is a recurring, well-documented barrier.** This is distinct from the more general, sector-neutral MSME credit gap already covered in the fintech deep-dive. Manufacturing MSMEs specifically need financing for two things: machinery, and working capital tied to physical inventory (working capital means the cash a business needs on hand to cover its day-to-day running costs). These businesses continue to report difficulty getting timely, affordable credit. This is true even though the government has backed schemes — such as collateral-free loan guarantee programs — that are meant to solve exactly this problem.
- **Compliance cost is a specifically well-documented burden on small manufacturers.** Compliance means following the required paperwork, filings, and legal rules a business must satisfy. Multiple sources describe Indian manufacturing MSMEs facing high annual compliance costs, driven by local bureaucratic and tax-filing requirements. This is a genuinely disproportionate burden. A small, resource-constrained business feels this burden much more heavily than a large company, which can afford to hire a dedicated compliance team.
- A March 2026 Parliamentary Committee report reportedly found that a majority of recent MSME-focused budget announcements remained un-operationalized months after being announced. "Un-operationalized" means the announcement was made, but the scheme was not yet actually working on the ground. This is a useful, concrete signal. It tells you that policy intent and actual on-the-ground implementation are two different things in this sub-field. Worth remembering: an announced scheme is not the same as a scheme that is already delivering results.

### Import-substitution niches: toys, footwear, and leather
- **Toys** are a genuine, well-documented import-substitution success story at the numbers level. Import substitution means replacing goods that used to be imported with goods now made domestically instead. Toy imports fell sharply after a 2020 Quality Control Order. This order created a mandatory certification standard, enforced by the **BIS (Bureau of Indian Standards)**. In practice, it made it harder for low-quality imports — overwhelmingly from China — to enter the Indian market. This is a rare, clearly verified case of a *non-financial* policy lever working as intended. In other words, it worked not through a subsidy or cash payment, but through a quality rule that simply made cheap, low-quality imports harder to bring in.
- **Footwear and leather** show the opposite pattern. There is real government intent here too — a dedicated development programme is discussed further below. But that intent has been undercut by an **"inverted duty structure."** Here is what that phrase means in practice: import duties (taxes charged on goods brought into the country) are structured so that a finished imported shoe can enter India more cheaply than it costs the domestic industry to import the machinery and components it needs just to *make* shoes domestically. In other words, it is currently cheaper to bring in a finished foreign shoe than to build the tools needed to make an Indian one. This is a specific, well-documented example of a policy working against its own stated goal. It's worth remembering as a general pattern: India's manufacturing policy is not uniformly well-designed across every sub-field, even when the government's stated intent is consistent.
- Both toys and footwear/leather are now being folded into an expanded PLI-style incentive scheme. This uses the same PLI mechanism already explained for electronics and pharma in Sub-fields 1 and 4.

### Contract manufacturing for D2C brands — a genuinely underappreciated layer
- There is a distinct and growing pattern here. It connects Stage 2's "contract manufacturing" idea directly to the D2C wave covered in the Consumer Goods and Beauty & Skincare sections of the sector survey. Small D2C brands — in beauty, food and beverage, nutraceuticals, and home goods — increasingly don't own any factory at all. Instead, they use existing contract manufacturers, under one of three arrangements. **White-label** means the manufacturer already has a generic product, and the brand simply relabels it with its own name. **Private-label** means the manufacturer has a base formulation, and the brand makes only minor customizations to it. **ODM (Original Design Manufacturing)** means the manufacturer actually designs the product to the brand's brief — this is used when the brand has no in-house research and development capability of its own.
- This matters structurally for a clear reason: it lowers the capital barrier to *starting* a product brand dramatically. There's no factory to buy, no machinery costs, and no compliance or licensing burden falling directly on the brand. But there's a tradeoff. The actual manufacturing capacity, the quality control expertise, and the profit margin all sit with the contract manufacturer — not with the brand. In other words, a founder building a D2C brand this way is, in effect, renting manufacturing capability rather than being a manufacturer themselves. This is an important distinction to be precise about, whenever someone says they are "in manufacturing."

### Industrial automation — a real, quantified adoption gap
- India's industrial robot installation count grew fast in percentage terms in the early 2020s. India is now the world's sixth-largest market for annual industrial robot installations. But there's a more revealing number than that growth rate: adoption density, meaning robots per 10,000 manufacturing workers. India's adoption density remains far below the global average, and dramatically below leaders like South Korea. Even within automotive — India's most automation-friendly sub-field — India's robot density trails China and South Korea by a wide margin. This gap is worth keeping as a specific comparison, not just a vague "India is behind" statement. The density comparison, rather than just the growth-rate headline, is what actually reveals how early-stage Indian automation still is relative to its peers. *(Source: [Automate.org — A Tale of Two Indias](https://www.automate.org/robotics/industry-insights/a-tale-of-two-indias-and-other-key-takeaways-on-robotics-and-automation-from-the-worlds-largest-country))*
- The barriers cited are consistent and well-documented across sources. First, there's a high upfront capital cost relative to an SME's balance sheet (a balance sheet is a snapshot of what a company owns versus what it owes). Second, there's a shortage of workers skilled in operating and maintaining modern automation equipment. Third, small manufacturers genuinely struggle to calculate a believable **ROI (Return on Investment)** case for automation. ROI means the expected payback compared to the money spent. This calculation is especially hard given uncertain demand, and the added risk of not being able to service the equipment themselves if it breaks down.

## Cross-cutting pain points affecting most sub-fields

These are structural issues. They show up across several of the sub-fields described above, rather than being specific to just one. This section deserves its own space, because a founder evaluating "manufacturing" broadly needs to know that these problems apply almost regardless of which specific niche they pick.

- **Labor law was, until recently, a genuine and well-documented barrier — and it is now mid-transition.** Here is the old regime: laws like the Industrial Disputes Act, 1947, required a manufacturer to get government permission before it could lay off workers or close a factory unit, once that unit crossed 100 employees. That threshold was low enough that it pushed many manufacturers toward hiring contract labor specifically to avoid ever crossing it, rather than growing a permanent workforce. **This changed concretely on November 21, 2025.** On that date, India brought four consolidated Labour Codes into force. These new codes raised that threshold from 100 employees to 300 employees. They also introduced a mandatory "Reskilling Fund" contribution — a fixed number of days' wages per worker who is laid off — as a partial offset. In other words, this is a real, dated, verified change in the rules, not just a proposal still being discussed. It is squarely the kind of "government as rule-setter, not just competitor" dynamic that the fintech deep-dive flagged when discussing NPCI. A founder evaluating a labor-intensive manufacturing idea today is operating under a meaningfully different rulebook than someone who evaluated the same idea back in 2024.
- **Land acquisition remains a slower, more friction-heavy process than in most competing manufacturing destinations.** Here is what that means in practice: agricultural or private land generally cannot be used to build a factory until it is formally converted to industrial-use status. That conversion process is reported to often move slowly. Two further complications make this worse specifically in India: land fragmentation (meaning many small individual landowners, rather than one large seller who can sign off in one step) and local land-ceiling rules (rules that cap how much land one entity can hold). These frictions are distinct from what you'd see in the U.S. or Southeast Asian markets used for comparison elsewhere in this document.
- **Quality and testing infrastructure is a specific, well-documented drag on export competitiveness** across sub-fields. Many exporters, especially those outside large cities, lack easy access to accredited testing labs and inspection agencies (accredited means officially certified to a recognized standard). A recurring, specific problem: **BIS (Bureau of Indian Standards)** certification is not automatically recognized in many foreign markets. This forces exporters to separately certify their products against each destination market's own standards. Multiple sources note something important here: most failed Indian export attempts are not due to poor product quality itself. They fail because of missing certifications, documentation, or compliance systems. This is a genuinely different, and more fixable, problem than a quality problem — worth being precise about.
- **The "missing middle" (Sub-field 6) and factory fragmentation (Sub-field 5) are really the same underlying pattern showing up twice.** India has been comparatively good at two extremes: either very small, informal manufacturing, or a small number of very large, government- or big-corporate-backed projects, like semiconductors or large EMS players. India has been comparatively weak in the middle — the mid-sized, professionally-run, export-competitive factory that countries like Vietnam and Bangladesh have built at scale in specific categories like garments.
- **Whether India's manufacturing share of GDP is actually stagnant is a genuinely unresolved, technical debate among economists — not a settled fact.** GDP stands for Gross Domestic Product, the total value of everything a country produces in a year. Some research finds that manufacturing's share of India's GDP actually rose meaningfully, once you use a newer measurement methodology. Other researchers continue to describe "premature deindustrialization" instead. This phrase means an economy's manufacturing share peaks and then declines at a lower income level than historical industrializers — like the U.S., Japan, or South Korea — experienced. This should be treated explicitly as an active academic and policy disagreement, not something this document can resolve on its own. A founder should not treat either version as settled fact without checking the latest data directly.

## Gap and opportunity analysis

Of everything surfaced above, three gaps stand out as both genuinely underserved and realistically sized for a new entrant. This is distinct from spaces that are large but require billions in capital and government-scale backing — like fabs or gigafactories — or spaces that are already crowded with well-funded incumbents.

### Gap 1 — Export compliance and certification-as-a-service for small manufacturers

**The gap:** As documented in the cross-cutting pain points above, a large share of failed Indian export attempts trace back not to product quality, but to missing certifications, incompatible documentation, or unfamiliarity with a destination market's specific standards. Recall also that BIS certification — India's own domestic quality mark — is not automatically recognized abroad. Small and mid-sized manufacturers are exactly the "missing middle" segment described earlier. They are the least likely to have in-house expertise or a dedicated compliance team to navigate this problem. Large exporters, by contrast, can typically afford to build or buy this capability internally.

- **Existing competitors/players:** General "export readiness consulting" firms already exist — one example surfaced in this research is IMARC Engineering. A government body, the Export Inspection Council, has also been expanding accredited testing lab capacity. But based on the sources reviewed here, this still appears to be a fragmented, consultant-driven space, rather than one with a single dominant, well-known specialized platform. This should be treated as an absence-of-evidence observation, not as confirmation that no strong player exists. A founder should verify this further — for example, by checking industry-specific trade body directories and exporter forums — before treating this gap as wide open.
- **What a new entrant could do differently:** A focused, vertical-specific service could work better than a generic one. For example, a service that goes deep specifically on food and agri-processing exports, or specifically on one destination market like the EU, rather than a broad "we do export compliance for everyone" pitch. Such a service would combine document preparation, lab-testing coordination, and up-to-date tracking of a specific market's standards. In spirit, this is closer to the "fintech infrastructure" B2B layer described in the fintech deep-dive than to manufacturing itself — it is a service business layered on top of the manufacturing sector.
- **Honest risks:** This is a services business, not a manufacturing business, so its risk profile is different. The core risk is that it is a knowledge-and-relationship business. That means real defensibility (what protects you from competitors copying you) comes from expertise and trust built over time — which is slow to build, and hard to differentiate on through marketing alone. There is also a risk that government efforts succeed at reducing the underlying problem this gap depends on — for example, if BIS signs mutual recognition agreements with foreign certification bodies, that would shrink the need for this service.
- **Economics:** No verified revenue or pricing data for this specific service category in India surfaced during this research pass. **This is flagged as an unresourced estimate area.** A real founder would need to do direct primary research — talking to MSME exporters and trade bodies like FIEO — rather than relying on published figures.
- **Evidence of customer want:** The evidence here is indirect, but plausible. Multiple sources repeatedly and consistently cite certification and compliance failures — rather than product-quality failures — as the leading cause of failed export attempts. This is a real signal, even though it is inferential (meaning it is inferred rather than directly measured), that manufacturers are hitting this wall often enough for it to be a recognized, named problem in industry commentary.

### Gap 2 — Automation-as-a-service for SME manufacturers (cobots, RaaS)

**The gap:** As documented in Sub-field 6, India's SME manufacturers face a well-documented, specific barrier to automation. The upfront capital cost of robotics is too high relative to their balance sheets. They also cannot confidently build an ROI case, given the added risk of not being able to service unfamiliar equipment themselves if it breaks. This is a genuinely well-quantified gap — recall that India's robot density trails the global average by a wide margin, despite fast headline growth in installation counts — rather than a vague impression.

- **Existing competitors/players:** Global cobot makers are reportedly already entering the Indian SME market. A cobot, short for "collaborative robot," is a robot designed to work safely alongside humans, without the heavy safety cages that traditional industrial robots need. **RaaS (Robotics-as-a-Service)** is cited as an emerging model here. RaaS means paying a recurring fee for access to automation, instead of buying the hardware outright — this is directly analogous to the SaaS (Software-as-a-Service) mechanic described in Stage 2's IT/Software section, just applied to physical robots instead of software. No dominant India-specific RaaS platform was clearly identified as already at scale, in the sources reviewed here.
- **What a new entrant could do differently:** Rather than selling hardware outright — the traditional industrial-automation sales model, which runs directly into the SME capital-cost barrier described above — a new entrant could offer a subscription or pay-per-use model instead. This model would bundle the robot itself, its maintenance, and operator training into one recurring fee. This directly removes the two most commonly cited barriers — capital cost, and the skill/serviceability risk — rather than just marketing around them.
- **Honest risks:** This gap may partly exist because it is genuinely hard to make the underlying economics work. Cobots and automation hardware are themselves capital-intensive for whoever owns them under a RaaS model. This means a RaaS provider needs either deep capital backing, or a financing partner. It also needs enough scale across many small customers to make per-unit maintenance and training economically viable. There is a real risk that this becomes a low-margin, service-heavy business, rather than a scalable software-like one — despite the SaaS-style framing that makes it sound easier than it may actually be.
- **Economics:** No verified India-specific RaaS pricing or unit-economics data surfaced during this research pass. **This is flagged as unresourced.** Global RaaS benchmarks would not reliably transfer to India's SME price sensitivity without direct local validation.
- **Evidence of customer want:** This evidence is reasonably strong and specific. The barriers cited — capital cost, skill gap, and ROI uncertainty — are described consistently across multiple industry sources as the actual reasons SMEs give for not automating. This is a more concrete form of evidence than a general "automation is growing" market-size projection would be.

### Gap 3 — Mid-market contract manufacturing / co-manufacturing platforms connecting small D2C brands to underutilized MSME factory capacity

**The gap:** As covered in Sub-field 6, a large and growing set of small D2C brands want contract manufacturing — whether white-label, private-label, or ODM, as defined earlier. But these brands are often too small to get attention from India's largest, most sought-after contract manufacturers. Separately, the "missing middle" problem described in Sub-field 6 suggests that many small-to-mid-sized factories likely run below full capacity utilization at any given time, especially outside peak season. Read together, these two facts suggest a matching or discovery problem — meaning brands and factories that could work well together are struggling to find each other. This specific connection is this document's own inference, rather than a directly sourced claim, and is flagged accordingly.

- **Existing competitors/players:** Individual contract manufacturers already market themselves directly — this is based on sources reviewed on beverage, cosmetics, and food contract manufacturing. Some platform-style intermediaries also exist in specific categories, for example Frigate, which works in D2C contract manufacturing broadly. This gap is more crowded than Gaps 1 and 2 above. A founder should not assume "no platform exists" here without checking category-specific players directly — for example, specifically within nutraceuticals, or specifically within apparel — before committing to this as an open gap.
- **What a new entrant could realistically do differently:** Rather than building a horizontal "we connect any brand to any factory" platform — which risks being thin and hard to differentiate — a new entrant could take a genuinely narrow vertical focus instead. For example: specifically matching small food-and-beverage or personal-care D2C brands to certified, export-ready MSME manufacturers with verified spare capacity. This could be paired with the quality and compliance support described in Gap 1. Why pair the two? Because a small brand's biggest fear in trusting an unfamiliar small factory is exactly the certification and consistency risk already documented above.
- **Honest risks — the clearest "possible trap" of the three:** This idea sits at the intersection of two already-crowded categories: contract manufacturing marketplaces, and general B2B sourcing platforms. That means the "obvious" version of this idea has very likely already been tried by better-funded players. A new entrant would need a genuinely narrow, defensible wedge — one product category, one region, or a specific trust/verification mechanism that competitors lack — rather than a general marketplace pitch.
- **Economics:** No verified take-rate, factory-utilization, or unit-economics data for a platform like this in India surfaced during this research pass. **This is flagged as unresourced.** It would need direct primary research with both MSME factory owners and D2C brand founders, before anyone could responsibly underwrite a business plan around it.
- **Evidence of customer want:** This evidence is indirect. The sheer volume of content and services already built around "how to find a contract manufacturer in India" — multiple guide-style sources surfaced in this research pass — suggests real, recurring demand for exactly this kind of matching help. However, this is an inference drawn from search-content volume. It is not a direct customer survey.

## Flagged as unverified / needs checking
- The exact current share of global iPhone production India represents — cited sources describe strong, fast growth, but the precise current percentage should be re-checked against the most recent quarter's reporting before being used in any pitch or plan.
- Whether India's semiconductor mission timeline (four plants by end-2026, Dholera fab pilot production in H2 2026, full fab by 2028) will actually be met — these are stated government/company targets, not delivered outcomes, and large fab projects worldwide have a well-known history of schedule slippage.
- The precise, current localization percentage for high-cost EV components (batteries, motors, DC-DC converters, on-board chargers) — sources describe this as "low" and "increasing" without giving a single reliable current figure.
- Whether the API PLI scheme has meaningfully reduced China-import dependence for pharmaceutical bulk drugs in the most recent reporting period, given that at least one source found imports from China continuing to grow even after the scheme's launch — this needs a more recent data check.
- Whether any dominant, at-scale India-specific export-certification-as-a-service platform (Gap 1) or RaaS/cobot-subscription platform (Gap 2) already exists under a name not surfaced by these searches — both gaps are framed as "not clearly found in this research," which is weaker evidence than "confirmed not to exist," and should be checked more thoroughly (Tracxn/YC-style startup databases, trade-body directories) before committing capital.
- The unresolved academic/policy debate on whether India's manufacturing share of GDP is stagnant, rising, or falling depends heavily on measurement methodology (base-year revisions, deflation approach) — this document deliberately does not take a side, and a founder should check the latest official National Accounts data directly rather than relying on any single commentator's framing.
- Whether the government's stated four-point plan to reach a much larger textile/apparel export target by 2030 has any interim, verifiable progress markers yet — sources described the plan and the target but not yet a tracked progress update.
