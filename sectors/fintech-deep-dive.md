# Stage 3 + 4 — Sector Deep-Dive: Financial Services / Fintech (India)

Geography: **India specifically**, throughout. Global comparisons (US, China, and others) are called out explicitly wherever they appear. They are never assumed to transfer to India automatically. This document does not repeat Stage 1's basic vocabulary (B2B, B2C, product vs. service, etc.). It only applies those ideas. See `foundations/stage1-business-foundations.md` for the definitions themselves.

**What was already covered in Stage 2 (`sectors/sector-survey.md`), and is not repeated here:** the survey gave you the three traditional pillars (banking, insurance, investment management) plus fintech as a cross-cutting tech layer. It also covered the basic idea of a bank's "spread," insurance as risk-pooling, and the one-line fact that India has shifted hard toward digital payments. This document assumes you already have that background. It goes underneath it, into more detail.

**What's in scope here:** this deep-dive treats "Fintech" the way the Indian startup and investor ecosystem actually treats it in 2025–26. It is not simply "banks that use apps." It is a set of distinct sub-industries that mostly sit *around* or *on top of* traditional banks and insurers. These sub-industries are often built by non-bank companies — startups and NBFCs (Non-Banking Financial Companies, defined in Sub-field 2 below) — using licenses and infrastructure that banks and the regulator control. The sub-fields covered are: digital payments, digital lending, insurtech, wealthtech, neobanking, embedded finance/credit-on-UPI, and the B2B "fintech infrastructure" layer (regtech, KYC, account aggregation, payment aggregation) that quietly underlies all the others. This is a pilot deep-dive — the first of a planned series across sectors. So it goes deliberately deep on structure, real players, and honest economics, rather than staying at survey level.

## Sub-field 1: Digital Payments (UPI and beyond)

### What this actually is
This is the part of fintech most Indians touch daily: moving money between people, and between people and merchants, digitally instead of with cash. In India this is now dominated by one piece of public infrastructure: **UPI (Unified Payments Interface)**. UPI is a system built and run by **NPCI (National Payments Corporation of India)**. It lets you send money instantly between any two bank accounts using just a virtual ID, like a phone number or a "name@bank" handle. It is free, and it works 24/7. In other words, UPI is like a shared set of pipes that every bank and app plugs into, so money can move between any two people in the country in seconds, without anyone needing to know the other person's full bank account number. Apps like PhonePe, Google Pay, and Paytm are not banks. They are **"TPAPs" (Third-Party App Providers)** that sit on top of UPI and provide the interface — think of them as the shop-front screen you tap on, while the actual money movement happens bank-to-bank underneath, out of sight.

- Digital payments is, by every account, the single largest slice of the Indian fintech market. This is according to Mordor Intelligence's industry estimate. *(Source: [Mordor Intelligence — India Fintech Market](https://www.mordorintelligence.com/industry-reports/india-fintech-market))*
- Scale and trajectory, per NPCI-sourced reporting: UPI transaction volume kept growing sharply year over year through 2025. A single month, August 2025, crossed a major milestone threshold for the first time. *(Sources: [DD News — UPI transactions cross 20 billion](https://ddnews.gov.in/en/upi-transactions-cross-20-billion-in-august-phonepe-google-pay-lead-market/), [Angel One — PhonePe and Google Pay Lead UPI Ecosystem in December 2025](https://www.angelone.in/news/economy/phonepe-and-google-pay-lead-upi-ecosystem-in-december-2025))*
- Market share by transaction volume, as reported via NPCI data as of December 2025: PhonePe and Google Pay are the clear co-leaders. Paytm is a distant third. Newer entrants Navi and super.money hold small single-digit shares. Between them, PhonePe and Google Pay handle the large majority of all UPI volume. *(Source: [Angel One, citing NPCI](https://www.angelone.in/news/economy/phonepe-and-google-pay-lead-upi-ecosystem-in-december-2025))*
- NPCI itself has set a volume cap per app, specifically to prevent this concentration from getting worse. It has not yet forced a redistribution. Both leaders were still reportedly losing share gradually as of that report, but they remained far above the cap. That last point — "losing share gradually" — is a reported detail, not a confirmed long-term trend, so treat it as a snapshot rather than a settled fact. *(Source: [Inc42 — UPI market share December 2025](https://inc42.com/buzz/upi-in-december-phonepe-google-pay-lose-market-share-for-the-seventh-consecutive-month/))*

### How the business model actually works (and why it's genuinely unusual)
This is the single most important structural fact to understand about Indian fintech. It explains almost every other strategic decision payments companies make.

- Since January 2020, the government set the **MDR (Merchant Discount Rate — the small fee a merchant's bank charges to accept a digital payment)** to **zero** for UPI and RuPay debit card transactions, to encourage adoption. In other words, imagine every shop currently pays its bank a small cut every time a customer taps a card — the government simply removed that cut entirely for UPI and RuPay debit payments. This means PhonePe, Google Pay, and every bank involved earn **nothing directly** from the transaction itself when you pay a shop with UPI.
- The government partially offsets this with a subsidy paid to banks and app providers. Multiple sources report that this subsidy has consistently and materially fallen short of the industry's actual cost of running the rail. This shortfall has happened year after year. *(Sources: [Fintech Industry Examiner — PhonePe's IPO tests the economics of India's 'free' payments rail](https://fintech.industryexaminer.com/phonepe-ipo-upi-unit-economics/), [PIB — Cabinet incentive scheme for BHIM-UPI](https://www.pib.gov.in/PressReleasePage.aspx?PRID=2112771))*
- This is a genuinely important thing for a founder to internalize: **the core product that made Indian fintech globally famous is structurally not a profit center for the companies running it.** In other words, the very thing UPI is famous for — free instant payments — is also the reason no company can simply make money from running those payments. This is unusual compared to most other countries. It is a direct policy choice by the Indian government to treat payments as public infrastructure, like a public road, rather than a private profit stream, like a toll road.
- Because of this, every major payments player is forced to monetize *around* the free rail rather than on it. They do this by cross-selling other financial products through the same app: insurance distribution, lending (often as a partner to a bank/NBFC, not as the lender itself), investment products (mutual funds, stocks), and merchant-side services like store management tools and working-capital loans. This is why PhonePe, Paytm, and Google Pay all look increasingly like "financial supermarkets" rather than pure payment apps. It is a direct, forced response to zero MDR, not a random growth strategy. In other words, since they can't charge you for the payment itself, they instead try to sell you other things once you're already inside their app.
- There's a live, unresolved policy debate about reintroducing MDR, at least for large merchants. A Parliamentary Committee reportedly pushed for this, and industry groups have lobbied for it. As of the sources found here, it has not been implemented. **This should be flagged as an active, unresolved policy question, not a settled fact.** A founder building anything payments-adjacent needs to watch this closely. It could materially change the economics overnight, in either direction. *(Sources: [Medianama — Parliamentary Committee calls for return of MDR](https://www.medianama.com/2026/03/223-parliamentary-committee-calls-return-mdr-upi-implications-for-users-small-merchants/), [Inc42 — End of Free UPI?](https://inc42.com/features/end-of-free-upi-why-fintech-startups-want-mdr/))*

### Who the real players are
- **PhonePe** — market leader by UPI volume. It is backed by Walmart, via Flipkart's parent structure. It is preparing an IPO (an IPO, or Initial Public Offering, is when a private company first sells shares to the general public on a stock exchange). As of reports found, PhonePe filed an Updated Draft Red Herring Prospectus in January 2026. This is a formal pre-IPO disclosure document filed with India's securities regulator, SEBI. Its target valuation is notably *lower* than what it reached in its 2023 private funding round. That drop is itself a signal about how public markets may be pricing the "free rail" economics discussed above. In other words, investors may now be judging PhonePe as worth less than before, partly because of the structural problem explained above — that payments alone don't generate direct profit. PhonePe's losses have been narrowing year over year, and it recently turned Adjusted-EBITDA positive (EBITDA is a common way to measure a company's profit from its core operations, before certain accounting deductions). However, it remains loss-making on a fully-reported basis once employee stock compensation is factored in — this is a significant drag on revenue. *(Sources: [ForgeUp — PhonePe IPO 2026 Valuation](https://forgeup.in/news/phonepe-ipo-2026-valuation-10-5b-valuation-key-investors/), [Outlook Business — PhonePe vs Paytm](https://www.outlookbusiness.com/corporate/phonepe-vs-paytm-scale-profits-and-the-valuation-puzzle))*
- **Google Pay** — second largest by volume. It monetizes indirectly. It is a small piece of Google's broader ecosystem strategy in India, not a standalone India business in the way PhonePe or Paytm are.
- **Paytm (One97 Communications)** — third by UPI volume. It has been publicly listed since 2021. It has diversified heavily into lending distribution and merchant devices, such as its "soundbox" and point-of-sale machines. This is its own answer to the zero-MDR problem described above.
- **Navi and super.money** — smaller, newer entrants. They illustrate that new entry is still technically possible, even against two dominant incumbents, though at a small fraction of the leaders' scale.
- **Razorpay** and **Pine Labs** — these are a distinct category from consumer UPI apps. They are **payment aggregators**, who serve *merchants and businesses* rather than end consumers. This is a B2B model, in Stage 1's vocabulary, versus the B2C UPI apps above. Razorpay is the clear scale leader in online payment gateways, and it charges merchants directly. This is possible because merchant-facing card/wallet payments are not subject to the same zero-MDR rule as UPI/RuPay. Pine Labs is a competitor focused more on offline, in-store checkout — think point-of-sale terminals, gift cards via its QwikCilver acquisition, and in-store EMI (paying in installments). Pine Labs went public in 2025. Razorpay is reported to be meaningfully larger by revenue and carries a higher valuation than Pine Labs. *(Sources: [Coinlaw — Razorpay Statistics 2026](https://coinlaw.io/razorpay-statistics/), [PrivateCircle — Pine Labs vs Razorpay](https://blog.privatecircle.co/pine-labs-vs-razorpay-battle-for-indias-fintech-frontier/))*

### The honest pain points here
- **Zero MDR is a double-edged sword.** It's the single biggest reason UPI became so widely adopted — nothing stopped merchant adoption, since accepting UPI cost merchants nothing. But it's also the single biggest reason no pure-payments company in India has found it easy to be reliably, sustainably profitable on payments alone. Any founder looking at "payments" as a business idea in India needs to internalize this: the obvious-looking business, simply processing UPI payments, is not, by itself, a viable standalone revenue model today.
- **Fraud is a large and growing problem, riding on the same rail that made adoption easy.** Cyber fraud losses and complaint volumes have both been rising year-on-year. Government data shared in Parliament confirms UPI-specific fraud spans a large number of incidents. One survey cited found that one in five UPI users had experienced fraud. Of those, 51% never reported it. This 51% figure is genuinely load-bearing, meaning it changes how you should read everything else here: it means official fraud statistics likely understate the true scale of the problem, possibly by a lot. *(Sources: [The420.in — ₹805 Crore Lost to UPI Frauds](https://the420.in/india-upi-fraud-data-fy26-parliament-digital-payments/), [Business Standard — how digital scams hit your wallet in 2025](https://www.business-standard.com/finance/personal-finance/how-digital-scams-hit-your-wallet-in-2025-lessons-for-2026-to-avoid-them-125122600429_1.html))* This is a genuine, underserved pain point. See Gap 1 below.
- **Government as gatekeeper, and arguably an indirect competitor.** This has been verified: NPCI itself, which runs UPI, is a not-for-profit entity set up by the RBI (India's central bank) and Indian banks. It is not a private company. It sets the rules, like the 30% volume cap mentioned above, that every private player must live within. This is a case where "government as gatekeeper" is real and well-documented, not assumed.

## Sub-field 2: Digital Lending

### What this actually is
This means lending money digitally, either to individuals (personal loans, "buy now pay later") or to small businesses (working capital, meaning short-term money to cover day-to-day running costs). It is usually originated through an app or website with minimal paperwork. Instead of a bank manager making a judgment call about you in person, the lender uses data-driven underwriting — meaning a computer model decides whether to approve you and at what interest rate, based on your data.

- A key structural fact worth defining clearly: most digital lending apps in India are **not themselves licensed lenders**. Only regulated entities can actually lend money. These are banks and **NBFCs (Non-Banking Financial Companies)**. An NBFC is a company that lends and provides credit-like services, but it cannot take public deposits the way a bank can — in other words, you cannot open a savings account with an NBFC the way you would with a bank. NBFCs are regulated by the RBI, under a lighter but still substantial regime. Most consumer-facing lending apps are technically **"LSPs" (Lending Service Providers)**. They do the marketing, the app experience, and often the underwriting/data work. But they partner with a bank or NBFC that is the actual, legal lender on paper. In other words, the app you see and tap on is often not the entity actually lending you money — it's a middleman working on behalf of a licensed lender behind the scenes. This distinction matters enormously for anyone considering this space. It determines whether you need a banking/NBFC license — expensive, slow, and closely regulated — or whether you can operate as a tech layer on top of one — faster to start, but dependent on a partner, and exposed if that partnership breaks.
- Scale (estimate, from an industry market-sizing report, not government data): digital lending is already one of the largest slices of the fintech market by "service proposition." It is projected to keep growing its share of fintech revenue through 2030. *(Source: [Mordor Intelligence — India Fintech Market](https://www.mordorintelligence.com/industry-reports/india-fintech-market))* Flagged: this is a market-research firm's projection, not a verified outcome. Treat the growth trajectory as a forecast, not a fact.

### How the money moves, and the regulation that shapes it
- On **May 8, 2025**, the RBI introduced the **RBI (Digital Lending) Directions, 2025**. This is a binding regulatory framework — meaning it is already law, not just a proposal — covering all regulated lenders and their LSP partners. Verified key provisions:
  - Lenders must give borrowers a **Key Fact Statement (KFS)** before the loan is accepted. This document discloses all costs, tenure (the length of the loan), APR, and the actual legal lender's name. APR stands for Annual Percentage Rate — the true yearly cost of a loan including fees, not just the headline interest rate. In other words, APR is meant to stop a lender from advertising a low interest rate while hiding extra fees that make the real cost much higher.
  - A mandatory **cooling-off period** applies, lasting a minimum of one day. During this time a borrower can exit the loan penalty-free, minus a one-time processing fee. In other words, if you take a loan and change your mind almost immediately, you get a short window to back out without being punished for it.
  - Borrower data must be stored within India. Explicit, purpose-specific consent is required before an app can access something like a user's contacts.
  - Money must flow directly between lender and borrower. No LSP — the middleman app described above — can sit in the money-flow path. This was previously a major source of fraud and opacity (opacity means it was hard to see clearly where the money actually went).
  - **DLG (Default Loss Guarantee)** arrangements are now capped at 5% of the disbursed loan portfolio, and must be invoked within 120 days of default. A DLG is where an LSP — the middleman app — promises to cover a lender's losses if borrowers default (fail to repay). In other words, this let an unlicensed tech company effectively take on real credit risk, like a lender would, without actually holding a lending license. The new cap limits how much of that risk the middleman can take on.
  - All Digital Lending Arrangements must be reported to RBI's Centralized Information Management System (CIMS) portal. The deadline for this was June 15, 2025.
  *(Sources: [OPL Innovate — RBI Digital Lending Guidelines 2025](https://www.oplinnovate.com/blogs/rbi-digital-lending-guidelines-2025-nbfcs-fintechs-compliance.html), [IndiaCorpLaw — RBI's Revised Digital Lending Guidelines](https://indiacorplaw.in/2025/07/19/rbis-revised-digital-lending-guidelines-strengthening-guardrails-in-an-evolving-ecosystem/))*
- Separately, in August 2024, RBI proposed tightening **P2P (peer-to-peer) lending** rules. P2P lending is when ordinary people lend money directly to other ordinary people through a platform, rather than through a bank. The proposed rules specifically bar P2P NBFCs from assuming credit risk themselves, and from cross-selling credit-enhancing insurance. This is another sign of the regulator actively closing loopholes. Those loopholes had let non-bank platforms behave like de facto lenders, without meeting a lender's capital requirements. *(Source: search result summary, RBI P2P amendments August 2024)*
- The government has also been developing a **"Digital Lending (Prohibition of Unauthorised Platforms) Bill,"** reportedly expected around 2025. This is flagged here as **not yet verified as enacted law**. Treat this as "proposed, watch for confirmation," not as settled fact.

### The BNPL (Buy Now, Pay Later) cautionary tale — a real, verified case study
This sub-field is worth a dedicated look. It is the clearest, best-documented example in Indian fintech of a hot business model getting shut down by regulation. It is directly instructive for anyone evaluating "is this gap real or a trap."

- **ZestMoney**, once a prominent BNPL player with a substantial user base, was built substantially around a **prepaid credit model that RBI later banned**. That ban restricted non-bank prepaid instruments from directly extending unsecured credit lines (loans not backed by any collateral). ZestMoney failed to complete an acquisition by PhonePe, and shut down entirely by December 2023. *(Source: search result summary)*
- **Simpl**, another well-known BNPL app, was ordered by the RBI on **September 25, 2025** to cease all payment, clearing, and settlement activity. The regulator found it was running a payment system without the authorization required under the Payment and Settlement Systems Act, 2007. Simpl was also reportedly under Enforcement Directorate scrutiny. The allegation was that it mischaracterized its actual financial-services business as "IT services," to access easier foreign investment approval routes — IT services get 100% automatic FDI (Foreign Direct Investment) approval, while financial services require prior government approval. *(Source: [X/Twitter post citing RBI order, aggregated in search](https://x.com/rajivmehta19/status/1982498076872015978))* **This claim about the FDI/ED angle should be treated as reported-but-not-independently-verified by this research.** It traces to a single social media source in the search results, not a primary regulatory filing. Flagging it accordingly.
- Survivors that reportedly remain operating within RBI's framework include LazyPay, Amazon Pay Later, Flipkart Pay Later, ICICI PayLater, and Paytm Postpaid. The pattern here: BNPL products backed by, or run in visible partnership with, a bank/NBFC/large regulated entity survived. Standalone, thinly-licensed BNPL apps did not.
- **The lesson for a founder, stated plainly as reasoning, not fact:** a "gap" that looks like "give people small, easy credit without going through a bank" is not simply an execution problem waiting for a better app. India's regulator has shown it will act decisively against exactly this pattern, when it judges consumer protection or licensing to be at risk. Anyone evaluating a lending-adjacent idea needs to budget real time, money, and expertise for regulatory relationships and licensing. This cannot be an afterthought.

### The credit gap that's real and reasonably well documented
- India's MSMEs (Micro, Small and Medium Enterprises — small and mid-sized businesses) face a credit gap. One source pegs this gap in the hundreds of billions of dollars. Only a small minority of MSMEs have access to credit at all. **This figure should be labeled as an industry estimate.** It comes from a fintech company's own research/marketing content, Credable, not an official government statistic. Treat it as a useful directional signal, not a number to quote as settled fact without further verification. *(Source: [Credable — Addressing the $530 Billion Credit Gap](https://credable.in/insights-by-credable/business-insights/credit-for-the-underserved-addressing-the-massive-dollar-five-hundred-thirty-billion-msme-credit-gap/))*
- Older estimates, from the *India FinTech Report 2019*, put the MSME financing gap and the consumer lending gap at meaningfully lower levels than today's figures. The fact that estimates have moved substantially since 2019 is itself a sign these are soft, model-driven figures, not precise measurements.
- The underlying reason: many small businesses and individuals are "**credit thin-file**" or "**credit invisible**." This means they have no meaningful history with traditional credit bureaus, like CIBIL, India's dominant credit bureau. They may never have had a formal loan or credit card, even though they may actually be creditworthy in practice. In other words, it's not that these people are bad at repaying money — it's that there's simply no paper trail proving it either way, so traditional lenders don't know how to judge them. A large share of India's working-age adult population is estimated to fall into this "credit underserved" category. *(Source: [Credable/Accumn — alternative data articles](https://hello.accumn.ai/msme-lending-challenges-alternate-data/), figure flagged as industry estimate)*
- The fintech response has been **alternative-data underwriting**. This means using GST filings (tax records), bank transaction history — accessed consensually via the Account Aggregator framework, covered in Sub-field 7 — and utility bill payments, instead of a traditional credit score. This is a real, active area, not speculative. But it's also crowded, as the regtech section below shows.

## Sub-field 3: Insurtech

### What this actually is
Insurtech companies are technology-first companies that sell insurance — life, health, motor, and increasingly niche products like gadget or travel insurance — or that help traditional insurers sell policies and process claims faster. Recall from Stage 2 that insurance itself is a distinct business, built on **risk-pooling**: many people pay a small premium, and the few who suffer a covered event get a payout. Insurtech doesn't change this core mechanic. It only changes the distribution and operations layer around it — in other words, it changes how insurance is sold and serviced, not the basic idea of insurance itself.

- Market size (labeled estimate): the India insurtech market is already sizable, and it is projected to keep growing at a very high rate through 2030. This growth rate should be read as a market-research projection, not a guarantee. *(Source: search result summary, market research aggregation)*
- One aggregator source counts well over a hundred insurtech players in India. Collectively, they have raised substantial funding and reached a substantial combined valuation. Again, this is flagged as an industry-count estimate, rather than an audited figure.

### The two very different insurtech models, and why the distinction matters
- **Model A: the aggregator/marketplace.** This matches Stage 1's "Marketplace/Platform" business model. The company doesn't take on insurance risk itself. It helps customers compare and buy policies from real, licensed insurers, earning a commission for each sale. **PB Fintech (parent of PolicyBazaar)** is the dominant player here. It is publicly listed, with a large user base, and its revenue scale is well ahead of other pure aggregators. *(Source: search result summary)*
- **Model B: the digital-first insurer itself.** Here, the company holds an actual insurance license from **IRDAI (Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority of India)** — the sector's regulator, equivalent in role to RBI for banking. This kind of company takes on real underwriting risk itself, meaning it directly bears the cost if it has priced its policies wrongly, just distributed and operated digitally rather than through traditional agents. **Acko** and **Digit Insurance (Go Digit)** are the clearest examples:
  - Acko's revenue has been growing, while its net loss has been narrowing. It has backing from SoftBank and Munich Re, a major global reinsurer (a company that insures other insurance companies, spreading the risk further). *(Source: [InsNerds — Acko revenue jumps](https://insnerds.com/news/insurtech-firm-acko-revenue-jumps-rs-2837-crore-fy25-net-loss-falls-rs-424-crore))*
  - Digit Insurance has backing including Sequoia, and it went public in 2024. This is per general market knowledge and was not independently re-verified in this search pass. Flagging accordingly.
- **Plum** is a narrower B2B2C example. It sells group health insurance specifically to startups and tech companies — a genuine niche, not general-population insurance. It covers a large and growing roster of employer clients. It is known for fast onboarding and support delivered over tools like Slack. This is a useful illustration: "insurance for a specific, underserved customer segment" can be a viable, narrower way into a large sector. Here, the specific segment is small tech employers who found traditional group health insurance slow and paperwork-heavy. Worth remembering for the gap analysis below.

### The pain point underneath all of this: India is genuinely, verifiably underinsured
- India's overall insurance penetration sits at roughly half the global average. Insurance penetration means total premiums paid, as a share of the country's GDP (Gross Domestic Product, a measure of the size of the whole economy) — it is a standard way analysts measure how "insured" an economy is. Life insurance penetration specifically has fallen for three consecutive years, according to IRDAI's own annual report. This is a genuinely counterintuitive, well-sourced fact: insurtech funding and marketing has boomed, while the underlying penetration metric has gone the wrong way. *(Sources: [Deccan Chronicle — Life Insurance Penetration Falls to 2.7%](https://www.deccanchronicle.com/business/life-insurance-penetration-further-falls-to-27-in-fy25-irdai-annual-report-1927344), [Whalesbook — India's Insurance Gap Widens](https://www.whalesbook.com/news/English/economy/Indias-Insurance-Gap-Widens-Penetration-Stagnant-at-Half-Global-Average-Density-Trails-Far-Behind/695435014342f77179ec9edd))*
- Swiss Re, a major global reinsurance company whose research is generally treated as a credible industry source, estimated that the overwhelming majority of insurable exposures in India are uninsured. Separately, Swiss Re sized India's "mortality protection gap" in the tens of billions of dollars, in premium-equivalent terms, as of the end of 2021. The mortality protection gap is the difference between the financial protection families would need if a breadwinner died, and what they actually have via insurance. *(Source: search result summary, citing Swiss Re analysis)*
- The stated reason, per the same research, is limited awareness and low perceived need for insurance — not primarily affordability. This distinction is worth sitting with. It changes what a founder would need to fix: education and trust, not just cheaper pricing.

## Sub-field 4: Wealthtech (investing and wealth management)

### What this actually is
Wealthtech platforms are technology platforms that let ordinary people invest, mainly in the stock market and mutual funds, with far lower cost and friction (meaning fewer obstacles and less hassle) than a traditional, human stockbroker or wealth advisor.

### The dominant business model: discount broking
- The Stage 1 concept most relevant here: these companies largely abandoned the traditional broker's commission-heavy model, in favor of very low, flat, or even zero brokerage fees. Instead, they make money on **volume** (a very large number of trades) and **cross-selling adjacent products**. This is much like the payments companies in Sub-field 1, which make money around, not on, the core free or cheap product.
- **Zerodha** is the long-standing profitability leader, and still the most profitable brokerage in India by a wide margin. Its revenue and profit both grew sharply through FY24 (Financial Year 2024). FY25 profit came in lower than FY24's peak. The company itself has flagged a further meaningful year-on-year hit to brokerage revenue, in the quarter ending June 2025. *(Sources: [YourStory — Zerodha crosses $1B in revenue](https://yourstory.com/2024/12/zerodha-crosses-1b-in-revenue-reports-89-profit-growth-fy24), [Startuppedia — Zerodha vs Groww FY25](https://startuppedia.in/trending/trending/zerodha-vs-groww-fy25-zerodha-stays-most-profitable-at-4200-cr-with-81m-users-groww-makes-1824-cr-profit-with-126m-active-clients-10613131))*
- **Groww** is the user-growth leader. Its revenue and profit both grew rapidly through FY24 and FY25. It has reportedly overtaken Zerodha in market share by user count, even though Zerodha remains more profitable in absolute terms. Groww also made the largest fintech M&A move of 2025 (M&A means Mergers and Acquisitions — one company buying or combining with another), acquiring **Fisdom**. *(Sources: [Startuppedia — Zerodha vs Groww FY25](https://startuppedia.in/trending/trending/zerodha-vs-groww-fy25-zerodha-stays-most-profitable-at-4200-cr-with-81m-users-groww-makes-1824-cr-profit-with-126m-active-clients-10613131), [Entrackr — Indian startup funding 2025 report](https://entrackr.com/report/annual-funding-report/indian-startup-funding-drops-10-to-13-bn-in-2025-but-ipo-count-hits-record-18-10958045))*
- **Why this matters as a structural risk, verified and not merely speculative:** both companies are explicitly and publicly flagging a *regulation-driven* revenue decline. India's securities regulator, **SEBI (Securities and Exchange Board of India)**, introduced a new fee circular and tightened rules on **index derivatives**. Index derivatives are a category of high-frequency, high-risk trading product that had become a large, controversial revenue source for discount brokers. This regulatory change is expected to meaningfully cut discount brokers' revenue, with the derivatives-specific hit reportedly the steepest part of it. In other words, a large chunk of what made these companies profitable was trading activity that regulators have now decided is too risky to encourage, and are actively discouraging. This is a live, current example of "government/regulator directly reshaping a fintech sub-sector's unit economics" — worth remembering alongside the BNPL case in Sub-field 2.

### What this leaves open
Wealthtech in India today is heavily concentrated in *trading/brokerage*, rather than *long-horizon financial planning*. This distinction is worth being precise about. "Robo-advisory" (mentioned in Stage 2 — automated, algorithm-based investment advice instead of a human advisor) and comprehensive financial planning for retail India remain comparatively thin, especially outside of large cities. This connects directly to the retirement/pension gap discussed in the gap analysis below.

## Sub-field 5: Neobanking

### What this actually is, and a genuinely important regulatory fact
A **neobank** is a digital-first "bank-like" experience. It offers sleek apps with savings accounts, budgeting tools, and cards, sometimes with a distinct visual brand — Jupiter, Fi Money, and Niyo are common examples. It feels like a bank to the end user.

- **The critical fact a founder must know: India has not issued a "neobank license."** There is no regulatory category called "neobank" or "digital bank" that lets a company independently hold customer deposits the way a licensed bank can. Every Indian neobank operates by holding a *narrower* license instead — typically a **PPI (Prepaid Payment Instrument) license**, which lets it issue a digital wallet. It then *partners* with a real, RBI-licensed bank — examples cited are Federal Bank and CSB Bank — which actually issues the underlying savings account, fixed deposit, or debit/credit card. This is a textbook example of the **B2B2C model** from Stage 1: the neobank is the consumer-facing brand you see and use, but the licensed bank behind it is the actual regulated entity holding your money.
  - **Jupiter** specifically: it holds a PPI license from RBI, confirmed. Its operating entity, **Amica Financial Technologies**, reportedly also holds an NBFC license. But Jupiter does not hold, and does not claim to hold, a banking license. *(Sources: [BW Disrupt — Jupiter Gets Final RBI Nod](https://www.bwdisrupt.com/article/neobank-startup-jupiter-gets-final-rbi-nod-for-mobile-wallet-license-523118), [The Paypers — Jupiter secures RBI licence for digital wallet operations](https://thepaypers.com/payments/news/jupiter-secures-rbi-licence-for-digital-wallet-operations))*
- **Growth is real and fast, per market-research projection.** Neobanking is cited as the fastest-growing fintech sub-segment over the next several years. Again, this is flagged as a market forecast, not a delivered outcome. *(Source: [Mordor Intelligence](https://www.mordorintelligence.com/industry-reports/india-fintech-market))*
- **The implication for a founder:** "build a neobank" in India, correctly understood, actually means something more specific. It means "build a very good consumer app, secure a PPI/NBFC license, then negotiate a dependent partnership with an actual bank." This is a materially different, and in some ways more fragile, proposition than "become a bank." It's worth being explicit about that difference before treating neobanking as a simple category to enter.

## Sub-field 6: Embedded finance and "Credit on UPI"

### What this actually is
**Embedded finance** means financial products — credit, insurance, payments — offered *inside* a non-financial company's app or platform, rather than requiring the user to go to a separate bank or lender. The most important, concretely verified example unfolding in India right now is **Credit Line on UPI (CLOU)**. This is a mechanism, built on UPI's rails, that lets a bank or NBFC extend a **pre-approved line of credit** directly usable through any UPI app, rather than issuing a separate physical credit card. In other words, instead of applying for and carrying a physical credit card, you get a pre-approved borrowing limit that lives inside the same UPI app you already use for regular payments.

- This is a genuinely new development, not a settled or mature market: NPCI opened UPI to bank credit lines around September 2025. *(Source: [Payment Expert — India's NPCI opens UPI to bank credit lines](https://paymentexpert.com/2025/09/01/india-npci-opens-upi-to-bank-credit-lines/))*
- Reported early performance signals for banks scaling CLOU are flagged as vendor/industry-reported figures, not independently audited. NPA (Non-Performing Asset — a loan that's gone bad or unpaid) rates are reportedly under 2% for standard small-ticket credit. Products can reportedly go live in 8–12 weeks. Acquisition cost — the cost of signing up one new customer — is reportedly around one-fifth of a traditional credit card's. *(Source: [IBS Intelligence — FinTech 2026: Credit Line on UPI](https://ibsintelligence.com/blogs/fintech-2026-why-credit-line-on-upi-will-reshape-the-entire-ecosystem/))*
- Separately and relatedly, **RuPay's** share of India's credit card *spending* reportedly surged to about 16% in 2025. RuPay is India's domestic card network, an alternative to Visa/Mastercard. Close to half of those RuPay transactions were routed through UPI. This illustrates that UPI is actively pulling volume away from the traditional Visa/Mastercard credit-card model, not just from cash. **Google** itself was reported to be deepening a consumer credit push in India via a UPI-linked card, as of December 2025. *(Sources: [TechCrunch — Google deepens consumer credit push](https://techcrunch.com/2025/12/17/google-deepens-consumer-credit-push-in-india-with-upi-linked-card/), search summary on RuPay credit card share)*
- **Why this matters for India-vs-global comparison:** this is a place where India is arguably *ahead* of, or structurally different from, the US and China, rather than catching up. The US model still centers on physical or virtual credit cards issued by banks and card networks. China's model, Alipay/WeChat Pay, is a closed-platform, super-app model, meaning one company's app controls the whole experience. India's approach is different from both: an open, publicly-run instant-payment rail (UPI) that any bank or fintech can plug into, now being extended to carry credit. This doesn't have a clean equivalent in either the US or China, as described in the sources reviewed. UPI itself was reported to represent about 49% of all real-time payment transactions globally in 2024, by one estimate. *(Source: [Medium/Citi Ventures aggregated summary](https://www.citi.com/ventures/perspectives/opinion/fintech-in-india.html))* Meanwhile, India's credit card penetration remains in the single digits, around 3%, versus under 40% in China. This is a genuinely different comparison from the payments-rail comparison above, where India arguably leads. **The two facts should not be blended into one "India is ahead/behind" claim,** because they answer different questions: payment rail design, versus credit-card ownership.

## Sub-field 7: The B2B "fintech infrastructure" layer (often invisible to consumers)

### What this actually is
This sub-field is a set of companies that don't sell to end consumers at all. They sell picks-and-shovels software and data infrastructure to banks, NBFCs, and the consumer-facing fintechs described above, so those companies can meet compliance requirements or move faster. This is a purely **B2B** model, in Stage 1's vocabulary. It's worth its own section because it's easy for a founder scanning "fintech" to overlook it entirely, while it's actually one of the more durable, less regulation-battered parts of the sector.

- **Account Aggregators (AA)** are a genuinely distinctive piece of Indian financial infrastructure. They are sometimes called India's version of "open banking" — a concept where, with your consent, your financial data can move between institutions, rather than being locked inside one bank's silo. An AA is a licensed intermediary that lets a person consent to share their financial data — bank statements, tax filings, and so on — directly and securely with a lender, without manually gathering and uploading documents. In other words, instead of you printing bank statements and emailing them to a lender, you tap "allow" once, and your data moves securely and automatically.
  - Verified adoption trajectory: accounts linked through AAs, total consents, and the number of participating regulated entities have all grown sharply year over year, since the framework launched in September 2021. A meaningful volume of loans has now been disbursed through AA-enabled flows. *(Sources: [CGAP — Convenience Drives Rapid Adoption of Account Aggregators](https://www.cgap.org/blog/convenience-drives-rapid-adoption-of-account-aggregators-in-india), [Sahamati — 100 million consents milestone](https://sahamati.org.in/media-article/indias-account-aggregator-framework-crosses-100-million-consents-in-three-years/))*
  - **Sahamati** is the industry alliance that coordinates and certifies participants in this ecosystem. It is not a company, and it is not a regulator.
  - **OCEN (Open Credit Enablement Network)** extends this idea specifically into embedding AA-powered credit directly into the platforms MSMEs and gig workers already use. For example, a business software tool could offer a loan button that is powered by OCEN underneath. This is a promising but still-maturing extension, per the sources reviewed — not yet a mature mass-market channel.
- **Regtech (regulatory technology)** companies, like **Signzy**, **Perfios**, **IDfy**, **Tookitaki**, **Karza Technologies**, and **Crediwatch**, sell identity verification, fraud detection, and underwriting-automation tools to banks and fintechs. The identity verification process is called **KYC (Know Your Customer)** — the legally mandated process of verifying who a customer is, before providing financial services. Market-size estimate, flagged as not government data: India's regtech market is still fairly small in absolute terms today, but it is projected to grow at a very high rate over the rest of the decade. One estimate suggests India could become one of the world's largest regtech hubs within a few years. *(Source: [BlueWeave Consulting — India RegTech Market](https://www.blueweaveconsulting.com/report/india-regtech-market))*
- **Payment aggregators** — Razorpay, Pine Labs, PayU — are already covered in Sub-field 1. They are worth re-flagging here as also fitting this "B2B infrastructure" category, since their real customer is the merchant or business, not the end consumer.
- **Supply chain finance / invoice discounting**: **TReDS (Trade Receivables Discounting System)** is an RBI-regulated electronic marketplace where financiers bid to pay MSMEs early on their outstanding invoices, so a small business doesn't have to wait 60-90 days to get paid by a large buyer. Alongside TReDS, fintech-led platforms such as **KredX**, **Vayana Network**, and **CredAble** operate a similar service. These reportedly can serve businesses that don't qualify under TReDS's stricter eligibility rules, or handle international invoices/purchase orders that TReDS doesn't cover. *(Source: [Decentro — trade finance companies](https://decentro.tech/blog/trade-finance-companies/), [KredX — company site](https://www.kredx.com/))*

### Why this layer is structurally different from the consumer-facing sub-fields above
Unlike UPI apps, BNPL, or neobanks, this B2B infrastructure layer has **not** been the subject of the same kind of dramatic regulatory shutdown, like Simpl's case described in Sub-field 2. This is likely because these companies aren't themselves extending credit or holding customer money. They're selling software and data services to entities that are already regulated. **This is offered as reasoning and pattern-recognition, not a confirmed regulatory guarantee.** A founder should not assume this layer is risk-free — only that its risk profile looks structurally different from consumer lending and payments.

## Gap and opportunity analysis

Of everything surfaced above, four gaps stand out as both genuinely underserved and realistically sized for a new entrant. This is as opposed to gaps that are large but already saturated, like UPI itself, or that exist because they are extremely hard for good structural reasons, like standalone consumer BNPL, per Sub-field 2's cautionary tale.

### Gap 1 — Consumer-facing fraud protection and financial safety layer

**The gap:** UPI fraud losses are large, rising, and — per the survey cited in Sub-field 1 — under-reported (51% of victims in one survey never reported their fraud). Fraud-awareness training has reportedly reached only a small minority of Indians. Meanwhile, the payment apps themselves — PhonePe, Google Pay, Paytm — have limited incentive to build aggressive fraud-prevention features into their apps. This is because fraud liability disputes are often absorbed by banks/NPCI rather than the app itself, and because building strong anti-fraud friction can hurt the exact transaction-volume growth these apps are chasing.

- **Existing competitors/players:** regtech/fraud-detection vendors, such as Tookitaki, Signzy, and IDfy, sell fraud detection *to banks and fintechs*, not to consumers directly. There is no widely known, India-specific consumer-facing "financial safety" brand, of the kind seen as identity-theft-protection products in some other markets. This should be treated as an observation based on the search results reviewed, not proof that no such company exists. A founder should verify this gap more directly — through app store searches, user forums — before betting on it.
- **What a new entrant could do differently:** build a real-time personal fraud-shield layer. This would flag suspicious QR codes, screen-sharing app requests, or "digital arrest" style social-engineering scam patterns, before a transaction completes. It could be sold either direct-to-consumer as a subscription, or B2B2C through banks/NBFCs looking to reduce their own fraud-related costs and reputational risk.
- **Honest risks:** this gap may exist partly *because* the economics are hard. Banks and NPCI already run fraud detection at the rail level, so a third party has to prove it catches something the existing system misses. That requires real technical differentiation, not just a marketing angle. There is also a serious trust/access hurdle: a fraud-protection app plausibly needs deep permissions, like SMS reading and overlay detection, that Play Store/RBI/data-protection rules increasingly restrict. This is the same direction of regulation that sank ZestMoney and Simpl, described in Sub-field 2 — it could just as easily constrain this idea, if implemented carelessly.
- **Economics:** no comparable revenue/cost data was found in this research pass for a consumer fraud-protection fintech specifically in India. **This must be labeled an unresourced estimate area.** A real founder would need primary research, such as interviews and pricing tests, rather than relying on published figures, since none surfaced here.
- **Evidence of customer want:** indirect but real. The volume of news coverage on "digital arrest" scams, the scale of reported 2025 fraud losses, and Supreme Court commentary on the scale of digital-arrest scams specifically all point to a population actively searching for help and protection — even if no dominant branded solution has emerged yet in the sources reviewed.

### Gap 2 — Retirement and old-age income products for the informal-sector majority

**The gap:** India's government-run pension schemes — NPS (National Pension System) and APY (Atal Pension Yojana) — together reportedly reach only about **5.3% of the total population**, and only around **12% of the workforce**. This is despite APY having crossed a very large number of gross enrollments. APY's payout is a small, *fixed* amount starting at age 60. It is likely to lose significant real value to inflation by the time a young subscriber today actually retires. Wealthtech, covered in Sub-field 4, in India is heavily concentrated on trading/brokerage, not long-horizon retirement planning for the mass-market informal workforce.

- **Existing competitors/players:** NPS/APY themselves are government schemes, not companies. Traditional insurers sell pension/annuity products through agents. Wealthtech apps, like Groww and Zerodha, offer NPS access as one feature among many, not a core focus. No player identified in this research is specifically dedicated to informal-sector retirement planning as a core, standalone product. This is flagged as a gap based on absence of evidence in searches conducted, not a confirmed absence.
- **What a new entrant could do differently:** design a product around irregular, cash-heavy informal income — for gig workers, small traders, and daily-wage workers. For example, this could mean voluntary, flexible micro-contributions timed to income spikes, paired with plain-language education on why such a small fixed monthly payout won't be enough decades from now.
- **Honest risks:** this is a genuinely hard customer segment. There is low willingness or ability to pay today for a benefit decades away, low financial literacy — cited as a specific barrier to APY adoption itself — and thin margins per user. This means this likely needs massive scale, or a distribution partnership, such as being embedded inside a gig platform's app, to work economically. The regulatory environment for pension products is also tightly controlled by PFRDA (Pension Fund Regulatory and Development Authority). A new entrant would very likely need to partner with, not compete against, an existing licensed pension fund manager.
- **Economics:** no verified revenue/margin data for a retirement-focused fintech in India surfaced in this pass. **This is flagged as unresourced.** The wealth-management industry standard globally — charging a fee as a percentage of assets under management, noted in Stage 2 — would presumably apply. But real Indian figures for this specific under-40-informal-sector segment were not found.
- **Evidence of customer want:** indirect. APY's substantial enrollment growth shows real demand for *some* pension product exists, even at the current fixed low payout. This suggests a better-designed, better-communicated, digitally distributed product could plausibly find willing customers. However, this is reasoning and inference, not a direct market-research finding.

### Gap 3 — Insurance and financial protection for gig and platform workers specifically

**The gap:** India's gig workforce — delivery and ride-hailing workers — recently won a form of legal recognition. New rules reportedly require aggregator platforms, such as Swiggy, Zomato, Uber, and Ola, to contribute a small, capped share of revenue to a government social security fund. But the actual delivery mechanism and benefit levels remain unclear, as of the sources reviewed. Existing platform-provided insurance has documented gaps: Zomato and Swiggy have spent meaningfully on gig-worker insurance premiums, yet delivery partners report delays and denials of coverage for medical needs. There is a structural problem specific to this segment: workers who earn from *multiple* platforms simultaneously fall outside how the Code on Social Security, 2020 is architected, since it assumes one clear employer-like relationship. *(Sources: [TechCrunch — India's gig workers win legal status](https://techcrunch.com/2025/11/24/indias-gig-workers-win-legal-status-but-access-to-social-security-remains-elusive/), [Whalesbook — Gig Workers Expose Zomato Swiggy Insurance Gaps](https://www.whalesbook.com/news/English/Economy/Gig-Workers-Expose-Zomato-Swiggy-Insurance-Gaps-Amid-Worker-Strife/6962103aef4ed95f980ea21d))*
- **Existing competitors/players:** the platforms themselves provide some insurance directly, imperfectly, per the reporting above. No independent, platform-agnostic insurtech specifically built to aggregate a gig worker's multi-platform income and provide portable insurance/benefits was identified in this research. This is flagged as an absence-of-evidence gap, to be verified further before acting on it.
- **What a new entrant could do differently:** build a portable, worker-owned — not platform-owned — insurance and benefits product. It would aggregate income evidence across multiple gig platforms, conceptually similar to how Account Aggregator, described in Sub-field 7, already lets financial data move with consent, to prove eligibility and calculate fair premiums, independent of any single platform's goodwill.
- **Honest risks:** this is a genuine trap-shaped gap. It may be underserved precisely *because* the economics are difficult: gig workers are price-sensitive, meaning they are highly sensitive to cost. The new social-security-fund rules could make a private solution partly redundant, or crowd it out once implemented. There's also real political and regulatory uncertainty about how the mandatory platform contribution scheme will actually work, which makes it hard to design a private product around a moving target.
- **Economics:** the combined insurance spend reported by Zomato/Swiggy gives a rough sense of scale, but is not a reliable base for calculating a standalone company's revenue potential. **This is flagged as insufficient for real financial modeling.** It would need direct primary research into gig-worker willingness to pay.
- **Evidence of customer want:** reasonably strong and directly documented. Worker complaints, strikes, and union demands specifically about insurance gaps are covered in mainstream reporting. This is a stronger form of evidence than the more inferential case in Gap 2.

### Gap 4 — MSME lending using alternative/GST-linked data, for the specific "thin-file but not actually risky" segment

**The gap:** As detailed in Sub-field 2, the large majority of India's MSMEs lack access to formal credit, largely because they're "credit thin-file," not because they're genuinely poor credit risks. TReDS and fintech invoice-discounting platforms — KredX, Vayana, CredAble — already address part of this, for MSMEs that supply large, established buyers. But by definition this leaves out MSMEs that sell to other small businesses or directly to consumers, who have no big "anchor" buyer to base supply-chain financing on.

- **Existing competitors/players:** this is the most *crowded* of the four gaps. Perfios, Lentra AI, Crediwatch, Finbox, and others already sell alternative-data underwriting tools to banks/NBFCs. KredX/Vayana/CredAble already serve the anchor-buyer-based segment described above. This is explicitly flagged so a founder doesn't mistake "real gap" for "empty field" — the *general idea* is well covered. The specific under-covered slice is MSMEs without a large anchor buyer relationship.
- **What a new entrant could realistically do differently:** most differentiation left here is narrow and vertical-specific. For example, a founder could build underwriting around one specific informal-sector vertical's transaction patterns — such as local kirana/retail stores' UPI transaction history, accessed via Account Aggregator consent — rather than GST/invoice data, which assumes a formal B2B supply relationship. A founder would need to pick one underserved vertical deeply, rather than compete horizontally against well-funded regtech incumbents.
- **Honest risks — the clearest "this might be a trap" case of the four:** this space has already seen the large majority of all historical Indian fintech capital raised, concentrated in payments and lending, per Sub-field 1's opening research. This means the "obvious" version of this idea is very likely already tried. A new entrant needs a genuinely narrow, defensible wedge, not a general "we do alternative data lending" pitch.
- **Economics:** the aggregate credit-gap figure, from Sub-field 2, gives an aggregate market-size sense, but is, again, an industry-marketing-derived estimate, not an official figure. Real unit economics — cost per loan originated, default rates by vertical — were not found in this research pass. These would need direct sourcing from NBFC partners before underwriting a business plan on this gap.
- **Evidence of customer want:** indirect but plausible. The sheer size of the documented, oft-repeated MSME credit gap statistic across multiple industry sources suggests real latent demand. However, this reasoning should be treated as inference from a repeated but unverified number, not a confirmed customer survey.
